# Visual Style Decision Framework

## Visual Toolkit

### B-Roll

#### Archival Footage
> Shotlist type: `archival_video`

**Visual:** Full-frame historical footage -- black-and-white or sepia-toned film of rural landscapes, structures, crowds, and individual figures. Ranges from heavily degraded celluloid with scratches and frame edges to cleaner vintage footage with natural desaturation.

**When to use:**
- Narration references historical events, time periods, or locations from the past
- Establishing the setting or era before a story begins
- Showing group dynamics, communities, or gatherings in a historical context

**When NOT to use:**
- When events are contemporary or post-2000
- When the narration is abstract or psychological -- archival footage grounds things in the literal

**Rules:**
- ALWAYS: Include small disclaimer text at bottom-left ("Not actual footage") in white sans-serif when footage is representative rather than authentic
- ALWAYS: Apply film grain and vignette to maintain consistent aged aesthetic
- NEVER: Use clean, modern-looking footage in archival segments
- CONSIDER: Varying degradation level -- heavier degradation for older or more uncertain events

**Production spec:**
- Background: Full-frame footage, no compositing
- Subject: Landscapes, buildings, silhouetted figures, crowds, rural/remote scenes
- Treatment: High contrast B&W or sepia, film grain, vignette, scratches, aged celluloid artifacts
- Duration: 4s-12s typical (up to 17s for establishing shots)
- Text: Small disclaimer only when needed
- Colors: Black, white, sepia, grey, cream

**Variations:**
- Rural/Landscape: Wide establishing shots of terrain, mountains, settlements -- use when grounding narrative in geography
- Figure Portrait: Single person in profile or posed -- use when introducing a historical individual
- Group/Crowd Scene: Multiple figures gathered -- use when illustrating social dynamics, rituals, or community
- Heavily Degraded: Near-illegible footage with extreme grain and damage -- use when implying lost or suppressed records

**Weight:** Heavy (14%)

#### Landscape Establishing Shot
> Shotlist type: `archival_video`

**Visual:** Wide aerial or ground-level color footage of geographic terrain -- mountains, desert, rural fields, vegetation. Slightly desaturated with atmospheric haze and cinematic grading.

**When to use:**
- Opening a new chapter or geographic section of the narrative
- Conveying remoteness, isolation, or scale of a location

**When NOT to use:**
- Mid-scene during rapid narrative progression
- When the location is urban or well-known (use archival footage or maps instead)

**Rules:**
- ALWAYS: Hold for at least 10 seconds to allow audience to absorb setting
- NEVER: Use fully saturated modern drone footage -- must feel muted and cinematic
- CONSIDER: Aerial for scale, ground-level for intimacy and vegetation detail

**Production spec:**
- Background: Natural landscape filling entire frame
- Subject: Wide shot of terrain, no human subjects
- Treatment: Slight desaturation, atmospheric haze, warm tones, subtle grain
- Duration: 12s-17s
- Text: None
- Colors: Muted earth tones, blue sky, brown terrain, greens

**Variations:**
- Aerial Wide: Overhead/elevated angle showing geographic scale -- use for chapter openings
- Ground Level: Eye-level view of vegetation and terrain -- use for intimate location setting

**Weight:** Light (2.4%)

### Archival Photos

#### Historical Photograph
> Shotlist type: `archival_photo`

**Visual:** Period-appropriate still photographs -- mugshots, press photos, location shots. Displayed full-frame with aged tinting, visible wear, and scan artifacts against a dark vignette background.

**When to use:**
- Introducing a key historical figure by showing their actual likeness
- Establishing a location or scene with photographic evidence
- Grounding narration in documented visual record

**When NOT to use:**
- When the person's identity should remain concealed (use silhouette instead)
- When no authentic photograph exists (use silhouette or illustration instead)

**Rules:**
- ALWAYS: Apply aged treatment -- grain, sepia/muted toning, visible wear
- NEVER: Show clean modern photographs without distressing treatment
- CONSIDER: Dark vignette framing to focus attention on the subject

**Production spec:**
- Background: Dark frame with vignette, or full-frame image
- Subject: Historical photograph of person, location, or scene
- Treatment: Aged color tint, visible wear, film grain, scan artifacts, high contrast B&W
- Duration: 3s-7s
- Text: None
- Colors: Muted sepia, dark brown, faded flesh tones, black, white, grey

**Variations:**
- Portrait: Single person mugshot or press photo -- use when naming or identifying an individual
- Location/Scene: Photograph of a place or event -- use when establishing factual setting

**Weight:** Medium (11%)

### Graphics & Animation

#### Silhouette Figure
> Shotlist type: `animation`

**Visual:** Human silhouettes rendered against deep red, foggy, or black backgrounds. Central figures may glow or be labeled with role text. Compositions range from single figures to crowd arrangements with power-dynamic staging.

**When to use:**
- Representing individuals 
- Showing power dynamics and actions
- Introducing a figure by their role or title when no imagery exists
- Depicting anonymous victims, aggressors, or crowds

**When NOT to use:**
- When an actual photograph of the person is available and appropriate
- For more than 3 consecutive shots -- alternate with other block types

**Rules:**
- ALWAYS: Use deep red or black backgrounds to maintain visual identity
- ALWAYS: Apply atmospheric fog/smoke for depth
- NEVER: Add facial features to silhouettes -- they must remain abstract
- CONSIDER: Label text (role titles like "FAITH HEALER") in white sans-serif beneath figures for introductions

**Production spec:**
- Background: Deep red smoky gradient, solid black, or black with vertical red accent stripe
- Subject: Human silhouettes at varying scales -- single, paired, crowd configurations
- Treatment: Soft glow on key figure, atmospheric fog, film grain, flat black silhouettes
- Duration: 3s-10s typical (up to 34s for complex compositions)
- Text: Optional role label in white sans-serif below figure
- Colors: Deep red, black, yellow-gold accents, white glow

**Variations:**
- Power Dynamic: Central glowing figure flanked by smaller dark figures -- use for leader/follower dynamics
- LED Matrix: Figure rendered in red dot-matrix LED style -- use for dehumanized or abstracted representation
- Crowd Worship: Silhouetted crowd with arms raised, religious symbol above -- use for cult/mass movement scenes
- Labeled Role: Single silhouette with role text identifier -- use for first introduction of a key figure
- Victim/Aggressor: Figures in positions suggesting violence or threat -- use for crime scene reconstruction
- Solo Approach: Single figure emerging from darkness -- use for building suspense around an unknown subject

**Weight:** Heavy (13%)

#### Concept Diagram
> Shotlist type: `animation`

**Visual:** Clean graphic illustrations of abstract concepts -- profile head silhouettes with layered internal shapes, brain scans, group diagrams with dividing lines, or identity transformation compositions. Rendered in flat graphic style with subtle glow effects.

**When to use:**
- Explaining psychological manipulation, indoctrination, or mental processes
- Visualizing group divisions, factions, or power structures
- Illustrating medical, scientific, or clinical concepts
- Showing identity transformation or duality

**When NOT to use:**
- When concrete visual evidence exists -- always prefer real imagery
- For simple narrative beats that do not require conceptual explanation

**Rules:**
- ALWAYS: Use clean line art with minimal detail
- ALWAYS: Include labels or text annotations on diagrams
- NEVER: Make diagrams overly complex -- one concept per frame
- CONSIDER: Using vertical red/magenta accent dividers to separate opposing elements

**Production spec:**
- Background: Dark blue/black, solid black, or black with vertical red accent divider
- Subject: Profile head silhouettes with internal layers, medical imagery, group silhouettes facing off, symbolic icons
- Treatment: Smooth gradients, subtle glow, CRT scanlines, retro illustration style
- Duration: 4s-8s
- Text: Small uppercase labels in white or gold, condensed sans-serif
- Colors: Dark blue, purple, grey, white, gold/amber accents, red/magenta dividers

**Variations:**
- Mind Layers: Profile head with concentric internal shapes -- use for psychological concepts
- Medical Scan: Brain MRI or clinical imagery -- use for physiological effects or forensic detail
- Mind Influence: Head profile with arrow/information entering skull -- use for indoctrination or manipulation
- Faction Split: Two groups of figures separated by divider line -- use for schisms or opposing sides
- Identity Transformation: Cultural symbols flanking human figures -- use for identity change or dual nature

**Weight:** Medium (3%)

#### Ritual Illustration
> Shotlist type: `animation`

**Visual:** Stylized graphic illustrations of religious, ritual, or mythological concepts. Rendered in flat neon line art -- red symbolic scenes, green/cyan indigenous iconography, white glowing divine figures, or warm-toned offering hands. Always abstract and symbolic, never photorealistic.

**When to use:**
- Depicting religious ceremonies, rituals, or spiritual beliefs
- Representing ancient cultural or indigenous iconography
- Visualizing claimed divine authority or spiritual hierarchy
- Showing manipulation through religious symbolism

**When NOT to use:**
- For modern or secular events
- When the concept can be shown through photographs or documents

**Rules:**
- ALWAYS: Keep illustrations stylized and abstract -- never attempt photorealism
- ALWAYS: Use neon glow effects on key elements
- NEVER: Depict recognizable religious symbols of mainstream religions disrespectfully
- CONSIDER: Matching illustration color to the cultural origin (green/cyan for indigenous, red for violence-adjacent, white for claimed purity)

**Production spec:**
- Background: Solid black or dark grey with subtle noise
- Subject: Symbolic scenes -- trees with figures, deity masks, thrones, hands holding symbols
- Treatment: Flat graphic style, neon glow, high contrast, CRT scanlines
- Duration: 5s-12s
- Text: None
- Colors: Black with red, green/cyan, white, or orange/red accents depending on variant

**Variations:**
- Symbolic Scene: Red-toned illustration of ritual activity -- use for violence-adjacent ceremonies
- Indigenous Iconography: Green/cyan line art of pre-Columbian masks or deities -- use for ancient belief systems
- Divine Authority: White glowing figure with throne/wing motifs -- use for claimed spiritual power
- Offering Hands: Warm-toned hands presenting symbolic objects -- use for manipulation or transfer of control

**Weight:** Medium (3%)

#### Symbolic Icon
> Shotlist type: `animation`

**Visual:** Bold, flat graphic icons representing objects, substances, or concepts -- substance silhouettes, ritual objects, skull and crossbones. Rendered against deep crimson or dark backgrounds with scanline texture.

**When to use:**
- Representing a dangerous substance, weapon, or tool central to the narrative
- Marking moments of death or mortality
- When showing the actual object is inappropriate, unavailable, or too graphic

**When NOT to use:**
- As a sustained visual -- icons are punctuation marks, not paragraphs
- When a more descriptive visual (illustration or footage) would better serve comprehension

**Rules:**
- ALWAYS: Keep icons universally recognizable -- no ambiguity
- ALWAYS: Use deep crimson or black backgrounds
- NEVER: Hold for more than 5 seconds -- these are visual stingers
- CONSIDER: Using striped background pattern for emphasis on substance-related icons

**Production spec:**
- Background: Deep crimson solid with scanline texture, or solid black
- Subject: Recognizable silhouette icon -- substance, object, skull, ritual tool
- Treatment: Flat graphic, horizontal scanlines, slight grain, bold contrast
- Duration: 2s-5s
- Text: None
- Colors: Deep red, black, white/grey accents

**Variations:**
- Substance Icon: Recognizable substance silhouette on crimson -- use when narration identifies a specific drug/poison
- Ritual Object: Stylized chalice or ceremonial tool -- use for objects central to rituals
- Death Symbol: Skull and crossbones in distressed style -- use to punctuate death or mortality

**Weight:** Light (1.6%)

#### Location Map
> Shotlist type: `map`

**Visual:** Stylized geographic maps with location labels, glowing points, connection arcs, and iconographic elements. Range from parchment-textured illustrated maps to neon-outlined geographic shapes on black backgrounds.

**When to use:**
- Introducing a new location central to the narrative
- Showing geographic relationships or distances between key places
- Narrowing focus from a broad region to a specific point
- Anchoring events to dates and places simultaneously

**When NOT to use:**
- When geography is irrelevant to the current narrative beat
- For locations already well-established in prior segments

**Rules:**
- ALWAYS: Include bold location name labels in display typography
- ALWAYS: Use a glowing point or marker to indicate the focal location
- NEVER: Show cluttered maps with too many locations -- maximum 3 labeled points per frame
- CONSIDER: Adding date labels when the map introduces a new time period

**Production spec:**
- Background: Textured parchment with topographic patterns, or solid black
- Subject: Geographic region outline, location markers, connection arcs between places
- Treatment: Halftone/risograph on parchment, or neon glow on black
- Duration: 6s-13s
- Text: Bold display font location names (serif or sans-serif), date labels when applicable
- Colors: Cream/red/black on parchment, or black/cyan/green/yellow neon palette

**Variations:**
- Illustrated Map: Parchment-textured map with icons and halftone printing -- use for period-appropriate or local-scale geography
- 3D Geographic: LED dot-matrix rendered terrain with colored labels -- use for dramatic location reveals with dates
- Region Highlight: Country/state outline with single glowing point -- use for zooming into a specific area
- Connection Arc: Two locations linked by a glowing line -- use for showing movement, migration, or connection between places

**Weight:** Medium (4%)

#### Glitch Stinger
> Shotlist type: `animation`

**Visual:** Full-screen digital corruption -- vertical red bars with horizontal scanline distortion, or fragmented geometric shapes with RGB split. Pure visual disruption with no representational content.

**When to use:**
- Punctuating a disturbing revelation or narrative shock
- Signaling a jarring shift between chapters or tonal sections
- Creating a beat of visual chaos after an intense passage

**When NOT to use:**
- More than twice per video -- overuse diminishes impact
- During quiet, reflective passages
- As a substitute for meaningful visual content

**Rules:**
- ALWAYS: Use as a brief punctuation mark, not a sustained visual
- NEVER: Place two glitch stingers back-to-back
- CONSIDER: Using the red bar variant for single-beat shocks, digital corruption for sustained transitions

**Production spec:**
- Background: Solid black with digital noise
- Subject: Vertical red bar with scanline distortion, or abstract fragmented glitch patterns
- Treatment: Heavy VHS glitch, horizontal line artifacts, chromatic aberration, RGB split, horizontal tearing
- Duration: 1s-3s (can extend to 5s for chapter transitions)
- Text: None
- Colors: Black, red, white, faint blue

**Variations:**
- Red Bar: Single vertical red stripe with horizontal distortion -- use for sharp single-beat shocks
- Digital Corruption: Full-frame fragmented geometry -- use for extended disruption or chapter breaks

**Weight:** Medium (5.5%)

#### Static Noise / Corruption
> Shotlist type: `animation`

**Visual:** Heavy TV static filling the frame with barely visible dark shapes -- architectural silhouettes or indeterminate forms -- emerging from the noise field. Near-total visual obscuration.

**When to use:**
- Representing events too disturbing to depict directly
- Evoking corrupted, lost, or suppressed footage
- Creating extreme tension at narrative peaks

**When NOT to use:**
- When the audience needs to see or understand something clearly
- More than twice per video

**Rules:**
- ALWAYS: Allow at least a faint shape to be barely visible through the static
- NEVER: Use as a lazy gap-filler -- must serve a narrative purpose
- CONSIDER: Pairing with audio distortion for maximum impact

**Production spec:**
- Background: Full-frame TV static/noise
- Subject: Barely visible dark silhouette of a structure or shape
- Treatment: Extreme static overlay, near-total obscuration, monochrome
- Duration: 4s-8s
- Text: None
- Colors: Black, grey, white noise

**Variations:**
- Architecture: Building or structure faintly visible through static -- use for locations of atrocity
- Pure Disruption: No discernible shape, just noise -- use for maximum disorientation

**Weight:** Light (1%)

#### Abstract Texture

**Visual:** Non-representational dark streaks, drip-like shapes, or abstract smears against a deep crimson background. Scanlines and noise overlay. Deliberately ambiguous -- suggests violence without depicting it.

**When to use:**
- During narration describing graphic violence
- When explicit imagery would be inappropriate or gratuitous
- Sustaining visual unease during disturbing passages

**When NOT to use:**
- When the narration is factual/expository without violent content
- For more than 6 seconds -- these are bridging visuals, not sustained ones

**Rules:**
- ALWAYS: Keep imagery fully abstract and non-representational
- ALWAYS: Use deep crimson/dark red palette
- NEVER: Include recognizable forms that could be interpreted as depicting actual violence
- CONSIDER: Layering heavy scanlines to further abstract the image

**Production spec:**
- Background: Deep crimson/dark red solid
- Subject: Dark vertical streaks, drip shapes, abstract marks
- Treatment: Heavy scanlines, noise, textured overlay
- Duration: 3s-6s
- Text: None
- Colors: Dark red, black, crimson

**Weight:** Light (0.7%)

**When to use:**

- When actual photos exist but identities need partial redaction

- NEVER: Show unredacted faces unless confirmed public domain and legally cleared

**Production spec:**

- Subject: Portrait photos stacked vertically with redaction bars and name labels

- Duration: 4s-6s
- Text: Glowing teal/cyan sans-serif names beside portraits; small disclaimer
- Colors: Black, teal/cyan, magenta

#### Retro Code Screen
> Shotlist type: `animation`

**Visual:** Black CRT-style screen displaying lines of BASIC or early programming code in white monospaced text. Phosphor glow and scanline effects create authentic vintage computer aesthetic.

**When to use:**
- Evoking a specific technological era (1970s-1990s)
- Suggesting digital investigation or data retrieval processes
- Brief visual texture between longer shots

**When NOT to use:**
- For more than 1 second at a time -- this is a flash-frame accent
- When the narrative has no connection to technology or investigation

**Rules:**
- ALWAYS: Keep as extremely brief flash frames (under 1 second)
- NEVER: Make the code content readable or relevant -- it is purely atmospheric
- CONSIDER: Using in rapid montage sequences with other glitch elements

**Production spec:**
- Background: Black CRT screen
- Subject: Lines of monospaced code text
- Treatment: CRT scanlines, phosphor glow, slight flicker
- Duration: 0.5s-1s (flash frame)
- Text: Monospaced code font, glowing white on black -- part of visual effect
- Colors: Black, white phosphor

**Weight:** Accent (0.3%)

### Text Elements

#### Quote Card
> Shotlist type: `text_overlay`

**Visual:** Direct quotes from sources, documents, or testimony displayed as readable text blocks on dark backgrounds. Serif or sans-serif fonts in cream/white with subtle grain and warm muted tones.

**When to use:**
- Presenting direct quotes from historical sources or documents
- Highlighting confessions, claims, or key statements
- Letting primary source material speak for itself
- When spoken narration would diminish the impact of the written word

**When NOT to use:**
- For paraphrased or editorialized content -- quotes must be verbatim
- More than 3 times in a row without visual variety between them

**Rules:**
- ALWAYS: Use quotation marks or clear attribution to signal these are direct quotes
- ALWAYS: Allow sufficient screen time for reading (minimum 4 seconds per 20 words)
- NEVER: Display quotes too small to read comfortably
- CONSIDER: Using serif font for historical sources, sans-serif for modern testimony

**Production spec:**
- Background: Dark muted color (brown, dark grey, purple, or solid black) with subtle texture
- Subject: Block of quoted text, left-aligned or centered
- Treatment: Slight film grain, warm muted tones, subtle CRT effect
- Duration: 6s-20s depending on text length (up to 47s for extended passages)
- Text: Serif or sans-serif, cream/white, medium size, readable paragraph format
- Colors: Dark brown/purple/black background, cream/white text

**Variations:**
- Source Quote: Period document or historical text with serif font -- use for archival sources
- Testimony Excerpt: Witness or participant statement -- use for first-person accounts
- Narrative Statement: Key phrase emphasized in sans-serif -- use for confessions or shocking claims

**Weight:** Heavy (10%)

#### Testimony Attribution Card
> Shotlist type: `text_overlay`

**Visual:** Bold dark text identifying the source of testimony or an official statement, displayed on a solid deep crimson/red background. Clean graphic design with authoritative typography.

**When to use:**
- Before presenting extended testimony or deposition content
- Identifying the speaker/source before their words appear on screen

**When NOT to use:**
- For brief quotes that include inline attribution
- When the source is already established in prior frames

**Rules:**
- ALWAYS: Use solid crimson/red background to distinguish from quote cards
- ALWAYS: Place before (not after) the testimony content
- NEVER: Include the testimony text itself on this card -- it is a header only
- CONSIDER: Holding for 3-5 seconds as a beat before the testimony begins

**Production spec:**
- Background: Solid deep crimson/red
- Subject: Source identification text -- name, title, context
- Treatment: Clean graphic design, bold typography
- Duration: 3s-8s
- Text: Black serif font, centered, medium-large
- Colors: Deep crimson, black

**Weight:** Medium (4%)

#### Keyword Stinger
> Shotlist type: `text_overlay`

**Visual:** Large bold text phrases or single words filling the frame on black backgrounds, often with glitch distortion, chromatic aberration, or red glow effects. High-impact typographic moments.

**When to use:**
- Delivering a shocking phrase or revelation with maximum impact
- Introducing a key concept or theme for the upcoming section
- Punctuating moments of uncertainty (question marks, unanswered questions)
- Highlighting a single disturbing detail

**When NOT to use:**
- More than 5 times per video -- each use must feel earned
- For routine information delivery
- Back-to-back without intervening visual content

**Rules:**
- ALWAYS: Use large, bold typography that commands the frame
- ALWAYS: Apply some form of distortion (glitch, chromatic aberration, or glow)
- NEVER: Use more than 5-6 words per frame -- brevity is the weapon
- CONSIDER: Red text for disturbing content, white for neutral revelations

**Production spec:**
- Background: Solid black, sometimes with subtle radial red glow or glitch texture
- Subject: Large text phrase, concept word, question mark, or detail label
- Treatment: Glitch distortion, scanlines, chromatic aberration, CRT phosphor glow
- Duration: 2s-6s (up to 19s for concept introductions)
- Text: Large bold sans-serif, centered or left-aligned, red/white with glitch effects
- Colors: Black, red, white, grey

**Variations:**
- Impact Phrase: Full phrase filling frame width -- use for shocking statements
- Concept Intro: Concept word with small graphic element below -- use at section openings
- Symbol: Large question mark or punctuation -- use for moments of mystery or uncertainty
- Glitch Emphasis: Heavily distorted single keyword -- use for emotional climax words

**Weight:** Light (3%)

### Evidence & Documentation

#### Wiki/Encyclopedia Text Block
> Shotlist type: `document_scan`

**Visual:** Formatted text passages in encyclopedia or wiki style with highlighted keywords in red/bold. Structured paragraphs with section headers presenting background information.

**When to use:**
- Rapidly establishing factual context the audience needs
- Presenting definitions, profiles, or background information
- When extensive factual setup is needed before the narrative resumes

**When NOT to use:**
- When the information can be more engagingly delivered through narration alone
- For more than 20 seconds -- audiences disengage from dense text

**Rules:**
- ALWAYS: Highlight key terms in red or bold to guide the eye
- ALWAYS: Use clean sans-serif body text that is easily readable
- NEVER: Display walls of text without visual hierarchy (headers, highlights)
- CONSIDER: Using section headers ("Psychiatric profile", "Background") for longer passages

**Production spec:**
- Background: Dark grey/black
- Subject: Formatted encyclopedia-style text with highlighted keywords
- Treatment: Slight glow on text, clean digital render, structured paragraphs
- Duration: 8s-18s (up to 27s for extended passages)
- Text: Sans-serif body text, left-aligned, with bold red keyword highlights
- Colors: Dark grey, white text, red/yellow highlights

**Variations:**
- Highlighted Keywords: Standard passage with red keyword highlights -- use for quick context
- Profile Section: Structured sections with headers -- use for biographical or psychological profiles
- Long Passage: Extended text with highlights throughout -- use when extensive background is essential

**Weight:** Medium (4%)

#### Newspaper Clipping
> Shotlist type: `document_scan`

**Visual:** Historical newspaper articles or front pages shown as physical documents with aged paper texture, yellowing, fold lines, and period typography. Displayed with spotlight lighting and slight tilt.

**When to use:**
- Validating events through contemporaneous media coverage
- Showing that an event was publicly reported and documented
- Anchoring the story in journalistic record

**When NOT to use:**
- For events that were not covered by media (use other evidence types)
- When the newspaper content is not legible or relevant

**Rules:**
- ALWAYS: Show the headline clearly readable
- ALWAYS: Apply aged paper treatment (yellowing, fold lines, warm lighting)
- NEVER: Use modern newspaper layouts for historical events
- CONSIDER: Slight tilt and spotlight effect to suggest physical document being examined

**Production spec:**
- Background: Dark surrounding with spotlight focus on document
- Subject: Newspaper clipping with visible headline and body text
- Treatment: Aged paper texture, slight tilt, warm lighting, scan artifacts
- Duration: 8s-18s (up to 25s for detailed articles)
- Text: Original newspaper serif typeface -- part of source document, not overlaid
- Colors: Cream, dark brown, faded black

**Weight:** Light (2.7%)

#### Credential / Authority Card
> Shotlist type: `document_scan`

**Visual:** Large stacked text identifying official titles and names with governmental badge/seal graphics. Heavy CRT distortion and halftone rendering create an authoritative but surveillance-like aesthetic.

**When to use:**
- Introducing law enforcement, investigators, or authority figures
- Establishing the credibility of an official source before their testimony

**When NOT to use:**
- For non-official sources or civilian witnesses (use quote card attribution instead)
- When the authority figure's identity is not relevant to the narrative

**Rules:**
- ALWAYS: Include title/rank prominently alongside name
- ALWAYS: Include badge or seal graphic element for visual authority
- NEVER: Use for informal or unverified sources
- CONSIDER: Holding for extended duration (15-25s) when presenting official testimony

**Production spec:**
- Background: Dark grey/black with film grain
- Subject: Stacked text with official title and name, badge/seal graphic
- Treatment: CRT scanlines, heavy distortion, halftone badge rendering
- Duration: 10s-25s
- Text: Bold condensed stencil-style font, white, left-aligned; halftone seal on right
- Colors: Black, dark grey, off-white

**Weight:** Light (2%)

#### Document Scan
> Shotlist type: `document_scan`

**Visual:** Historical documents, official records, book covers, or anatomical illustrations displayed as physical artifacts with aged paper texture and warm tinting. Suggests the examination of primary evidence.

**When to use:**
- Presenting primary source evidence (court records, official documents, registries)
- Referencing published books or studies central to the investigation
- Showing medical or anatomical details in clinical illustration form

**When NOT to use:**
- When the document content is not legible or meaningful
- As generic visual filler without narrative relevance

**Rules:**
- ALWAYS: Apply warm tinting and paper texture to maintain consistent aesthetic
- ALWAYS: Ensure any critical text is legible to the viewer
- NEVER: Show documents that contain personally identifying information of living individuals
- CONSIDER: Slight zoom on the relevant portion of large documents

**Production spec:**
- Background: Dark surround with centered document, or aged paper filling frame
- Subject: Official records, book covers, anatomical illustrations, or other primary sources
- Treatment: Slight zoom, warm tint, paper texture, low contrast, visible wear
- Duration: 3s-8s
- Text: None overlaid -- text is part of the document itself
- Colors: Cream, dark brown, faded black, muted red (for anatomical)

**Variations:**
- Official Record: Columned text entries, census or court style -- use for legal/governmental evidence
- Book Cover: Published source with title visible -- use for referencing key texts
- Anatomical Illustration: Vintage medical engraving with CRT effect -- use for forensic or medical details

**Weight:** Light (1.2%)

#### Digital Screen Capture
> Shotlist type: `document_scan`

**Visual:** Screenshots or screen recordings of digital interfaces -- Wikipedia edit histories, terminal data displays, or database records. Shown with CRT glow or clean digital aesthetic.

**When to use:**
- Demonstrating how information was tracked, edited, or manipulated online
- Showing investigative processes or data retrieval
- Referencing digital provenance of information

**When NOT to use:**
- When the digital interface is not recognizable or relevant
- For generic internet browsing without investigative purpose

**Rules:**
- ALWAYS: Apply CRT glow or terminal aesthetic for period-appropriate digital content
- NEVER: Show URLs or interface elements that could lead to harmful content
- CONSIDER: Zooming in on relevant data entries or edits

**Production spec:**
- Background: White webpage or black terminal screen
- Subject: Wikipedia edit history, terminal data columns, or database interface
- Treatment: Screen recording/screenshot, CRT glow, scanlines, retro terminal aesthetic
- Duration: 3s-7s
- Text: Standard web/terminal UI text -- part of captured screen, not overlaid
- Colors: White/blue/black (web) or black/cyan/white (terminal)

**Weight:** Accent (0.8%)

## Pacing & Sequencing Rules

### Shot Duration by Narrative Mode
- **Exposition/Context Setting:** 8-18s per shot (maps, wiki blocks, establishing footage)
- **Active Narration:** 4-8s per shot (silhouettes, concept diagrams, archival photos)
- **Tension/Horror:** 2-5s per shot (symbolic icons, abstract textures, keyword stingers)
- **Shock/Revelation:** 1-3s per shot (glitch stingers, flash frames, data moshing)
- **Quote/Testimony:** 10-50s per shot (quote cards, testimony cards -- paced by reading time)

### Sequencing Constraints
- **Never** place two glitch/distortion elements back-to-back (stingers, static, data moshing)
- **Never** run more than 3 consecutive text elements (quote cards, date cards, keyword stingers)
- **Always** follow a glitch stinger with a representational visual (silhouette, archival footage, or map)
- **Always** precede testimony cards with a credential card or character profile card
- **Always** alternate between text-heavy and image-heavy blocks in groups of 2-3
- **Silhouette figures** should not exceed 3 consecutive shots without an intervening footage, map, or document visual
- **Date cards** should be immediately followed by the content they timestamp (archival footage, maps, or establishing shots)

### Transition Patterns
- **Cut:** Default transition between all shots within a section (~85% of transitions)
- **Dissolve/Fade:** Used only between major chapter breaks or significant time jumps
- **Glitch transition:** Used sparingly (2-3 times per video) to punctuate tonal shifts
- **Hold to black:** Brief black frames (0.5-1s) used before and after keyword stingers and glitch elements

## Type Selection Decision Tree

IF narration introduces a person by name or role → **Silhouette Figure** (labeled variant) OR **Historical Photograph** (if available)
IF narration introduces a location → **Location Map** (region highlight or illustrated map) → then **Landscape Establishing Shot** or **Archival Footage**
IF narration states a specific date or year → **Date Card** → then the visual content the date contextualizes
IF narration references a document, book, or record → **Document Scan** OR **Newspaper Clipping** OR **Digital Screen Capture**
IF narration quotes a source directly → **Quote Card** (preceded by **Testimony Attribution Card** if first quote from that source)
IF narration introduces an authority figure → **Credential Card** → then **Quote Card** for their statement
IF narration describes psychological manipulation or ideology → **Concept Diagram** (mind influence or mind layers variant)
IF narration describes a ritual or religious practice → **Ritual Illustration** (matched to cultural origin)
IF narration describes violence or death → **Abstract Texture** OR **Symbolic Icon** (death symbol)
IF narration mentions a substance or weapon → **Symbolic Icon** (substance or ritual object variant)
IF narration builds suspense about an unknown figure → **Silhouette Figure** (solo approach variant) with fog
IF narration reveals a shocking fact → **Keyword Stinger** (impact phrase) → optionally followed by **Glitch Stinger**
IF narration poses an unanswered question → **Keyword Stinger** (symbol variant -- question mark)
IF narration provides background/context exposition → **Wiki Text Block** (highlighted keywords) OR **Archival Footage**
IF transitioning between chapters → **Glitch Stinger** or **Date Card** → then establishing visual for new section
IF narration describes group division or conflict → **Concept Diagram** (faction split variant)
IF narration references ancient culture or mythology → **Ritual Illustration** (indigenous iconography) OR **Glitch Icon** (serpentine form)
IF narration describes a cover-up or hidden information → **Static Noise** OR **Data Moshing Montage**
IF narration warns of upcoming disturbing content → **Warning Card**
