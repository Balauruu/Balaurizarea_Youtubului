In the late 1940s, the government of Quebec and the Catholic Church began falsely certifying thousands of orphaned children as mentally deficient, reclassifying them as psychiatric patients to collect higher federal subsidies. The scheme ran for over a decade. It produced approximately 20,000 victims.

This is the true story of the Duplessis Orphans and the institutions that turned children into revenue.

## 1. Children of Sin

In Quebec in the 1940s, the Catholic Church did not advise on social welfare. It administered it. Religious orders ran the hospitals. They ran the orphanages. They ran the schools. When government money flowed to care for dependents, it flowed through the Church first.

The orphanages were full. Thousands of children — orphaned, born outside marriage, or surrendered by families who could not support them — lived inside Catholic institutions across the province. A child born to an unmarried woman carried a specific classification in this system. The word was illegitimate. The social reality was closer to contaminated. These were not children who had been unfortunate. They were evidence of sin. A moral problem to be managed.

As one survivor, Louis-Joseph Hebert — who went by Nestor — described it: "When you are a bastard, it's like being born into a garbage can. You never have a happy life. Nobody will know you. You will be the dirt of the society."

Nestor was born to an unmarried woman and institutionalized as an infant. The nuns gave him and the other babies born that month a shared, generic surname. He did not choose the name Nestor. It was assigned. Inside the institution, he learned one rule above all others: do not show emotion. When he cried, the nuns beat him.

That conditioning outlasted the institution. Decades later, as an adult, he described what it had done to him: "I have trouble to cry when I have a big emotion. I would be so happy if I could cry."

The capacity for grief had been trained out of him. It did not return.

## 2. The Arithmetic

Maurice Duplessis returned to power as Premier of Quebec in 1944. His government, the Union Nationale, had built its authority on a structural alliance with the Church — not a political convenience, but a governing arrangement. The Church ran the institutions. The government funded them. Neither questioned the other.

Federal funding at the time drew a sharp line between two categories of dependent person. A child in an orphanage received approximately $0.70 per day in government subsidy. A psychiatric patient in a hospital received $2.25 per day. The difference was $1.55 per child per day. Across thousands of children held for years, that arithmetic produced a very large number.

Someone did the math.

Beginning in the late 1940s, Duplessis signed an order-in-council redesignating orphanages as psychiatric hospitals. He did not construct new facilities. He did not hire psychiatrists. He did not commission clinical reviews of the children. He signed a paper. The buildings stayed the same. The children stayed the same. The funding category changed.

The per-head subsidy tripled.

Seven religious communities operated the redesignated institutions: the Sisters of Providence, the Sisters of Mercy, the Grey Nuns of Montreal, the Sisters of Charity of Quebec, the Little Franciscans of Mary, the Brothers of Notre-Dame-de-la-Misericorde, and the Brothers of Charity. Each institution needed medical files to justify the reclassification. Psychiatrists — many of them employed by the Church-run facilities — produced diagnoses. The process was not individual. It was formulaic. The diagnoses needed to exist in the files. They did not need to reflect any clinical assessment of the child.

The legal architecture was already in place. Quebec's 1909 Loi sur les Asiles d'alienes had permitted psychiatric commitment on grounds that included the maintenance of "social order" — a phrase left undefined, the discretion delegated to the certifying psychiatrist. It had always been legally possible in Quebec to commit a person for reasons unrelated to mental illness. The reclassification of the 1940s and 1950s did not invent a new mechanism. It scaled one that already existed.

A 1999 financial analysis by Leo-Paul Lauzon and Martin Poirier at the Universite du Quebec a Montreal estimated that religious bodies collected approximately $70 million in subsidies through the scheme. The Quebec government itself saved $37 million through the redesignation of a single institution. The total number of children reclassified was approximately 20,000.

## 3. What the File Did

A psychiatric diagnosis in 1940s Quebec was not a medical record. It was an administrative instrument. Once it entered a child's file, it appeared on civil documents — the records consulted during employment proceedings, marriage applications, any official interaction where a person's history was relevant. The diagnosis did not merely classify a child. It shaped the plausibility of everything the child said afterward.

Children labeled severely mentally deficient had their education curtailed or eliminated entirely. The reasoning was self-fulfilling. If a child was certifiably impaired, education was a waste of resources. Years of no schooling produced adults with limited literacy, limited documentation skills, and limited ability to navigate bureaucratic systems. The diagnosis created the conditions that made it nearly impossible to dispute the diagnosis.

The institutional logic went further. A psychiatric patient in a hospital could be restrained. Sedated. Subjected to procedures. The redesignation of orphanages as hospitals made those interventions administratively coherent. The redesignation of children as patients made them defensible.

Survivors describe corporal punishment, electroshock therapy, administration of psychiatric drugs, forced labor recorded in institutional files as therapeutic activity, and sexual abuse. These were the conditions inside buildings that had been reclassified on paper as hospitals, administered by religious orders whose commitment to child welfare was legally assumed and never examined.

Paul St. Aubain entered St. Michel Archange mental hospital in Joliette at eighteen years old. A lobotomy was performed on him. He was not informed. He did not consent.

As he described it: "I wasn't ill. They did it without my consent, without my permission. They were experimenting with me. I was a prisoner."

St. Aubain spent twenty-five years moving between mental institutions. By the time journalists found him in the 1990s, he was living on welfare. He could not work. The lobotomy had been performed on a healthy teenager. The psychiatric diagnosis that justified it was fraudulent.

The diagnoses followed the children into adulthood. Into employment records. Into civil registration documents. Into the credibility assessments officials made when survivors, years or decades later, tried to report what had happened to them. A person with a documented psychiatric history describing institutional abuse faced a structural problem. The document had already decided their reliability.

## 4. The Dead

In 1942, the Quebec legislature passed a law allowing religious institutions to sell unclaimed bodies of deceased residents to medical schools. The price was $10 per body.

Albert Sylvio survived the St. Jean de Dieu asylum in Montreal. His assigned labor inside the institution involved the dead. As he described it: "I undressed them and washed them and prepared them for burial. We put them in cardboard boxes. Some of them were children."

The hospital registries at Cite de St-Jean-de-Dieu documented who died and how. They recorded causes of death, dates, names. By the time survivors began organizing in the 1990s and requested those records, the registries were not in provincial archives. They were not in Church archives. They were not in any location that any researcher or government office could find.

The official position was that they no longer existed.

Whether they were deliberately destroyed or lost through institutional neglect, the record does not establish. The question is not abstract. Those registries would have documented which children died, under what circumstances, and whether their bodies were transferred to medical schools under the 1942 law. Without them, the scope of what happened to the children who did not survive cannot be determined.

In 2004, a group of survivors petitioned a Quebec court for permission to exhume an abandoned cemetery in east Montreal believed to contain remains of children who died at the asylum. They cited the 1942 body-sale law, the disappeared registries, and survivor testimony. The names of who is buried in that cemetery are not documented in any accessible source. The number is not known.

## 5. The Report

In 1961, Jean-Charles Page published Les fous crient au secours — The Mad Cry for Help — an account of conditions inside Quebec's psychiatric institutions. A second account, Ma chienne de vie, followed in 1964. Duplessis had died in office in 1959. The Union Nationale government that had authorized the reclassification was losing its grip on Quebec's institutional structure.

The publications created noise. They did not trigger reform.

Reform came from inside the government. In 1962, Quebec commissioned a formal investigation into its psychiatric hospital system. The resulting document — the Bedard Report — examined approximately 22,000 patients classified as mentally incompetent across the provincial network. Its central finding was specific: roughly one-third of those patients had been classified for fiscal rather than clinical reasons.

This was not a finding by outside critics. It was an official government commission, reporting to the government that had authorized the reclassification, documenting in its own terms that thousands of people were being held in psychiatric institutions because of a funding formula.

Quebec ceased the practice. The Bedard Report contributed to the broader secularization of the province's institutions during the Quiet Revolution — the period in the 1960s when Quebec removed the Church's administrative control over health, education, and welfare.

What the report did not produce was any accounting for what the reclassification had done to the people it reclassified. It identified the problem. It did not mandate correction of individual diagnoses. It did not require correction of civil records. It did not acknowledge the children. The practice stopped. The records remained. The survivors — now in their twenties and thirties — carried fraudulent psychiatric classifications in their civil documentation and had no mechanism to correct them.

## 6. The Settlement

Public reckoning came three decades later. In 1991, Pauline Gill published Les enfants de Duplessis, reintroducing the survivors' story to a Quebec public that had largely not encountered it. The survivors were in their forties and fifties. The youngest among them had spent their entire working lives with false psychiatric diagnoses in their records.

Herve Bertrand and other survivors began pursuing class action lawsuits in 1992. Seven requests were submitted to the Court of Quebec in 1993. The court rejected all seven in 1995, citing procedural failure to meet class action criteria.

In January 1997, Quebec Ombudsman Daniel Jacoby submitted a report recommending a public apology and compensation involving the Quebec government, the medical profession, and the religious congregations. He named the harm directly. His report was not acted on immediately.

That same year, a comparative study found that Duplessis orphans showed markedly lower educational attainment, higher rates of chronic stress, and more pronounced attachment difficulties than a matched low-income control group. The outcomes were consistent with the deprivation documented in survivor testimony.

In 1999, the Lauzon-Poirier financial analysis put public numbers on what the institutions had extracted. The Quebec government under Premier Lucien Bouchard issued a formal apology in March 1999 and established a $3 million compensation fund. Ombudsman Jacoby publicly criticized the fund as trivializing the abuse.

In 2001, Premier Bernard Landry presented a final offer: $10,000 per person, plus $1,000 per year of wrongful confinement, capped at $25,000. Over 1,000 survivors accepted. They shared approximately $26 million.

Three civil servants administering the program received fees exceeding $1,000 per day. The orphans received $1,000 per year of wrongful confinement.

The settlement came with a condition. Survivors who accepted the money were required to sign a waiver barring any future legal claims against the Catholic Church. The government administered the settlement. The Church issued no apology. The survivors who accepted signed away the right to seek one through any court.

As survivor Martin Lecuyer stated in 2006: "It's important for me, that the Church, the priests, that they recognize they were responsible for the sexual abuse, and the aggression. It's not for the government to set that peace. It's an insult, and it's the biggest proof that the government is an accomplice of the Church."

## 7. Across the Ocean

In 2010, survivors presented their case before the United Nations Human Rights Council. Researchers estimated that approximately 300 to 400 of the original 20,000 remained alive.

In 2017, a video conference connected Duplessis Orphan survivors in Montreal with survivors of Irish Mother and Baby Homes in Dublin. Two groups separated by a language and an ocean described nearly identical experiences. False mental deficiency labeling. Abuse under Catholic institutional care. An identical logic that treated illegitimate birth as a moral condition requiring confinement. The parallel had not been organized by academics. The survivors had found each other.

New class action suits were filed against eight religious communities in April 2018, seeking $875,000 per community. The Superior Court of Quebec rejected them. Additional suits followed in July 2020. The courts did not provide relief.

The Catholic Church never issued a formal apology.

In 2012, the College des medecins — the medical licensing body — sent a letter expressing regrets for the role physicians had played in providing fraudulent diagnoses. It came eleven years after the government settlement had closed the legal window for survivors who had accepted it. It was not an apology from the Church.

The Church's position attributed conditions inside the institutions to insufficient personnel and funding — an operational limitation, not a policy. The seven religious communities maintained that reclassification decisions had been made by government, not by the Church. The 1962 Bedard Report, commissioned by that government, documented institutional misclassification as fact. The Lauzon-Poirier analysis quantified what the religious communities received through it.

The waiver signed in 2001 remains in force. For survivors who accepted the settlement, there is no remaining legal avenue against the Church. For those who declined and pursued further litigation, the courts have not ruled in their favor.

The hospital registries from Cite de St-Jean-de-Dieu remain missing. The 1942 law allowed unclaimed bodies to be sold to medical schools for $10. The records documenting which bodies were transferred, to which institutions, and for what purposes have not been found.

The false psychiatric diagnoses placed in the civil records of approximately 20,000 children were not corrected as a condition of the government's apology or compensation. Survivors who accepted the settlement received money. The documents that had labeled them mentally deficient stayed in the files where the institutions had placed them.

What happened to the children who did not survive — what was done to them, and by whose decision — belongs to registries that no longer exist and to the people who ran those institutions. Most of those people are dead. The rest have not spoken.
