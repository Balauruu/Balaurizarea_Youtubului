"When you are a bastard, it's like being born into a garbage can. You never have a happy life. Nobody will know you. You will be the dirt of the society."

In the late 1940s, the provincial government of Quebec and seven Roman Catholic religious orders began falsely certifying thousands of orphaned children as mentally deficient and reclassifying them as psychiatric patients. The mechanism was a federal funding formula. It ran for over a decade.

This is the true story of the Duplessis Orphans and the bureaucratic machinery that erased them.

## 1. The Arithmetic of Abandonment

In 1944, Maurice Duplessis returned to power in Quebec for a second consecutive run as Premier. His government, the Union Nationale, had built its political foundation on a close alignment with the Roman Catholic Church — not a casual alliance, but a structural one. In Quebec at that time, the Church did not merely advise on social welfare. It administered it. Catholic religious orders ran the hospitals, the orphanages, and the schools. When money flowed from the provincial and federal governments to care for society's dependents, it flowed through the Church first, and the Church decided what happened next.

The orphanages were full. Through the 1940s, Quebec's Catholic institutions housed thousands of children — orphaned, illegitimate, or surrendered by families who could not support them. A child born outside of marriage carried a specific stigma in this culture. The term for it was illegitimate, but the social reality was closer to contaminated. These children were not merely unfortunate. They were considered evidence of sin, a moral problem requiring management. The institutions received them. The institutions kept them.

Federal funding at the time drew a sharp distinction between two categories of dependent person. A child in an orphanage received approximately $0.70 per day in government subsidy. A psychiatric patient in a hospital received $2.25 per day. The difference was $1.55 per child per day. Across thousands of children, held for years, that arithmetic resolved into a very large number.

Someone did the math.

Beginning in the late 1940s, Duplessis signed an order-in-council redesignating orphanages as psychiatric hospitals. He did not build new facilities. He did not hire psychiatrists to replace the nuns who ran the wards. He did not evaluate the children individually or commission any clinical review. He signed a paper. The buildings stayed the same. The children stayed the same. The funding category changed. Overnight, the orphans became psychiatric patients, and the per-head subsidy tripled.

Seven religious communities operated the redesignated institutions: the Sisters of Providence, the Sisters of Mercy, the Grey Nuns of Montreal, the Sisters of Charity of Quebec, the Little Franciscans of Mary, the Brothers of Notre-Dame-de-la-Misericorde, and the Brothers of Charity. To maintain the reclassification and defend it against administrative scrutiny, the children needed medical diagnoses. Psychiatrists — many of them employed by the Church-run institutions — produced them. The process was not individual. Diagnoses were formulaic. They needed to exist in the file, not to reflect any clinical assessment of the child.

The law governing psychiatric admissions in Quebec dated to 1909. The Loi sur les Asiles d'alienes allowed commitment on grounds that included the maintenance of "social order" — a phrase left undefined, the discretion delegated entirely to the certifying psychiatrist. It had always been possible under Quebec law to commit someone for reasons that had nothing to do with mental illness. The reclassification of the 1940s and 1950s used a legal architecture that was already in place. The order-in-council did not invent a new mechanism. It scaled one that already existed.

A 1999 financial analysis by Leo-Paul Lauzon and Martin Poirier, researchers at the Universite du Quebec a Montreal, estimated that the religious bodies collected approximately $70 million in subsidies through the reclassification scheme over its duration. The Quebec government itself saved an estimated $37 million simply by redesignating a single institution. The total number of children caught in this reclassification was approximately 20,000.

## 2. What the Diagnosis Did

A psychiatric diagnosis in 1940s Quebec was not merely a medical record. It was an administrative instrument. Once it entered a child's file, it appeared in civil documents — the records consulted during employment, during marriage proceedings, during any official interaction where a person's history might be relevant. The diagnosis did not merely label a child as mentally deficient. It shaped the plausibility of everything the child said afterward.

Children classified as severely mentally deficient had their educational provision curtailed or eliminated entirely. The reasoning was self-fulfilling: if a child was certifiably impaired, education was a waste of institutional resources. The certification made the impairment real in every subsequent transaction — not because the impairment existed, but because the document said it did. Years of no schooling produced adults with limited literacy, limited documentation skills, and limited ability to navigate bureaucratic systems. The diagnosis created the conditions that made it difficult to dispute the diagnosis.

Louis-Joseph Hebert, who went by the name Nestor, was born to an unmarried woman and institutionalized as an infant. The nuns gave him and the other babies born that month a shared, generic surname. He grew up inside the Catholic institutions, and inside those institutions, emotion was dangerous. When he cried, the nuns beat him.

He described what that conditioning had done to him in adulthood. As he put it: "I have trouble to cry when I have a big emotion. I would be so happy if I could cry."

The conditioned suppression of emotion outlasted the institution. It belonged to him in every context that followed, in every moment that required a grief response he could not produce.

Paul St. Aubain entered St. Michel Archange mental hospital in Joliette as a young man. He was 18 years old. A lobotomy was performed on him. He was not informed in advance. He did not consent.

As he described it: "I wasn't ill. They did it without my consent, without my permission. They were experimenting with me. I was a prisoner."

St. Aubain spent 25 years moving between various mental institutions. By the time journalists located him in the 1990s, he was living on welfare. He could not work.

The range of treatment documented by survivors extends across corporal punishment, electroshock therapy, the administration of psychiatric drugs, forced labor described in institutional records as therapeutic activity, and sexual abuse. These were the conditions inside buildings that had been reclassified on paper as hospitals, administered by religious orders whose commitment to child welfare was legally assumed and institutionally unexamined. The psychiatric diagnosis was the mechanism that made each category of harm administratively coherent. A psychiatric patient in a hospital could be restrained, sedated, subjected to procedures. The framing of the institution as a hospital made those interventions possible. The framing of the child as a patient made them defensible.

The diagnoses followed the children into adulthood. Into employment records. Into civil registration documents consulted during marriage proceedings. Into the credibility assessments made by officials when survivors, years or decades later, tried to report what had happened to them. A person with a documented psychiatric history describing institutional abuse faced a structural disadvantage that the document itself had created.

## 3. The Record That Disappeared

In 1942, the Quebec legislature passed a law allowing religious institutions to sell unclaimed bodies of their residents to medical schools. The price was $10 per body.

Albert Sylvio survived the St. Jean de Dieu asylum in Montreal. Part of his work inside the institution — assigned labor, framed as therapy — involved the dead. As he described it: "I undressed them and washed them and prepared them for burial. We put them in cardboard boxes. Some of them were children."

The hospital registries at Cite de St-Jean-de-Dieu documented who died and how. They recorded causes of death, dates, names. By the time survivors began organizing in the 1990s and making formal requests for documentation of what had happened, those registries were not in provincial archives. They were not in Church archives. They were not anywhere that any researcher or government office could locate them.

The official position was that they no longer existed.

Whether they were deliberately destroyed or lost through institutional neglect, the record does not establish. The question is not academic. The registries would have documented which children died, under what circumstances, and whether their bodies were transferred to medical schools under the 1942 law. Without them, the scope of what happened to the children who did not survive their institutionalization cannot be established.

In 2004, a group of survivors petitioned a Quebec court for permission to exhume an abandoned cemetery in east Montreal believed to contain the remains of children who died at Cite de St-Jean-de-Dieu. The petition cited the 1942 body-sale law, the disappeared registries, and survivor testimony. The names of who is buried in that cemetery are not documented in any source available to the public. The number is not known.

## 4. The Report No One Acted On

By 1961, survivors were beginning to write publicly about what the institutions had been. Jean-Charles Pagé published Les fous crient au secours — The Mad Cry for Help — an account of conditions inside Quebec's psychiatric facilities. A second autobiography, Ma chienne de vie, followed in 1964. Both were published during Duplessis's final years in power; he died in office in 1959, and the Union Nationale government that had authorized the reclassification was already losing its grip on Quebec's institutional life.

The publications created public noise but did not trigger immediate institutional reform. The reform that ended the reclassification came not from public pressure but from inside the government itself.

In 1962, the Quebec government commissioned a formal investigation into its psychiatric hospital system. The resulting document — the Bedard Report, named for its lead author — examined the approximately 22,000 patients classified as mentally incompetent across the provincial psychiatric network. Its central finding was specific and documented: roughly one-third of those patients had been classified as mentally incompetent for fiscal rather than clinical reasons.

This was not a finding by outside critics or advocacy groups. It was an official government commission, reporting to the government that had authorized the reclassification, documenting in its own language that thousands of people were being held in psychiatric institutions because of a funding formula.

Quebec ceased the practice. The Bedard Report contributed to the broader secularization of Quebec's social institutions during the Quiet Revolution, a period in the 1960s when the provincial government progressively removed the Church's administrative control over health, education, and welfare. The shift was overdue and, in institutional terms, comprehensive.

What the Bedard Report did not produce was any formal accounting for what the reclassification had done to the people it had reclassified. The report identified the problem and the practice. It did not mandate any review of individual diagnoses, any correction of civil records, any acknowledgment to the children who had been redesignated. The practice stopped. The records remained. The survivors — now in their twenties and thirties — carried fraudulent psychiatric classifications in their civil documentation and had no mechanism through which those classifications could be formally corrected.

## 5. Thirty Years Later

Public reckoning came in 1991, when Pauline Gill published Les enfants de Duplessis, reintroducing the survivors' story to a Quebec public that had largely not encountered it in formal terms. The survivors were by then in their forties and fifties. The youngest among them had spent their entire working lives with false psychiatric diagnoses in their records.

Beginning in 1992, Herve Bertrand and other survivors worked with lawyers to pursue class action lawsuits. Seven requests were submitted to the Court of Quebec in 1993, documenting the experiences of dozens of institutionalized survivors and seeking legal accountability. The Court rejected all seven in 1995. The basis for rejection was procedural: the plaintiffs had not met Quebec's statutory criteria for class action certification.

In January 1997, Quebec Ombudsman Daniel Jacoby submitted a formal report recommending a public apology and proposing compensation structures that would involve the Quebec government, the medical profession, and the religious congregations. He named the harm directly. He identified the parties responsible. His report was not acted on immediately.

That same year, a comparative study was released examining Duplessis orphans against a matched low-income control group. The orphans showed markedly lower educational attainment, higher rates of chronic stress, and more pronounced attachment difficulties — outcomes consistent with the deprivation and coercive treatment documented in survivor testimony.

In 1999, the Lauzon-Poirier financial analysis placed public numbers on the scale of what the institutions had extracted: $70 million to the religious communities, $37 million saved by the provincial government through a single redesignation. The Quebec government under Premier Lucien Bouchard issued a formal apology in March 1999 and established a $3 million compensation fund. Ombudsman Jacoby, whose 1997 report had called for exactly this kind of action, publicly criticized the fund as trivializing the abuse.

In 2001, under Premier Bernard Landry, the government presented what it framed as a final offer: $10,000 per person, plus $1,000 for each year of wrongful confinement, capped at a maximum of $25,000. Over 1,000 survivors accepted. They shared approximately $26 million in total compensation.

Three civil servants administering the compensation program received fees exceeding $1,000 per day for their work on it. The orphans received $1,000 per year of wrongful confinement.

The settlement came with a legal condition. Survivors who accepted the compensation were required to sign a waiver barring any future claims against the Catholic Church. The Quebec government administered the settlement. The Church issued no apology. The survivors who accepted the money signed away the right to seek one through any court.

Martin Lecuyer, one of the survivors, addressed this directly in 2006. As he stated: "It's important for me, that the Church, the priests, that they recognize they were responsible for the sexual abuse, and the aggression. It's not for the government to set that peace. It's an insult, and it's the biggest proof that the government is an accomplice of the Church."

## 6. The Absence of Accountability

The Catholic Church never issued a formal apology to the Duplessis Orphans.

In 2012, the College des medecins — the medical licensing body — sent a letter expressing regrets for the role physicians had played in providing fraudulent diagnoses. It was not an apology from the Church. It came eleven years after the government settlement had closed the legal window for survivors who had accepted it.

New class action suits were filed against eight religious communities in April 2018, seeking $875,000 per community. The Superior Court of Quebec rejected them. Additional suits were filed in July 2020. The pattern of judicial rejection continued.

In 2010, survivors presented their case before the United Nations Human Rights Council. At that point, researchers estimated that approximately 300 to 400 of the original 20,000 survivors remained alive. The rest had died — some in the institutions, some in the years that followed — without any formal acknowledgment of what had been done to them.

In 2017, a video conference connected Duplessis Orphan survivors in Montreal with survivors of Irish Mother and Baby Homes in Dublin. Two groups separated by a language and an ocean described nearly identical experiences: false mental deficiency labeling, abuse under Catholic institutional care, and an identical institutional logic that treated illegitimate birth as a moral condition requiring confinement. The parallel had not been organized by researchers. The survivors had found each other.

The Church's official position attributed the conditions inside the institutions to insufficient personnel and inadequate funding — an operational limitation, not a deliberate policy. The seven religious communities that had operated the redesignated facilities maintained that the reclassification decisions had been made by government, not by the Church. The 1962 Bedard Report, produced by the government that had authorized the reclassification, documented institutional misclassification of patients as fact. The Lauzon-Poirier analysis quantified what the religious communities had received through it.

The waiver signed in 2001 remains in force. For survivors who accepted the settlement, there is no remaining legal avenue against the Church or the religious communities. For those who declined the settlement and pursued further litigation, the courts have not provided relief.

## 7. What Remains

The Quebec government apologized. The government paid. The apology came 37 years after the Bedard Report documented the reclassification scheme in official terms. The compensation was negotiated and paid. The legal process was concluded.

The hospital registries from Cite de St-Jean-de-Dieu remain missing. No official explanation for their absence has been established. The 1942 law allowed unclaimed bodies to be sold to medical schools for $10 each. The records documenting which bodies were transferred, and to which institutions, and for what purposes, have not been located in any accessible archive.

The petition to exhume the abandoned east-Montreal cemetery was submitted to a Quebec court in 2004. What the court decided is not clearly documented in the public record. Whether any exhumation occurred is not established. The names of the children buried there remain unknown. The number is unknown.

The false psychiatric diagnoses placed in the civil records of approximately 20,000 children were not formally corrected as a condition of the government's apology or compensation offer. Survivors who accepted the settlement received money. The documents that had labeled them mentally deficient stayed in the files where the institutions had placed them decades earlier.

The Catholic Church has not apologized. The religious communities that collected approximately $70 million in subsidies through the reclassification scheme have not been held legally liable in any court proceeding that has concluded in the survivors' favor. No religious order has acknowledged institutional responsibility for what was done to the children in its care.

What happened to the children who did not survive their time inside those institutions — what was done to them, why, and by whose decision — belongs to registries that no longer exist and to the people who ran those institutions. Most of those people are dead. The rest have not spoken.
