From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia
Canadian children, wrongly classified as mentally ill
[![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b9/Maurice_Duplessis.jpg/250px-Maurice_Duplessis.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Maurice_Duplessis.jpg)Maurice Duplessis in 1952
The **Duplessis Orphans** (French: _les Orphelins de Duplessis_) were a population of Canadian children[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Duplessis_Orphans#cite_note-1) [wrongly certified](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Involuntary_commitment "Involuntary commitment") as mentally ill by the [provincial government of](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Government_of_Quebec "Government of Quebec") [Quebec](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quebec "Quebec") and confined to [psychiatric institutions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Psychiatric_hospital "Psychiatric hospital") in the 1940s and 1950s. Many of these children were deliberately miscertified in order to acquire additional subsidies from the federal government. They are named for [Maurice Duplessis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maurice_Duplessis "Maurice Duplessis"), who served as [Premier of Quebec](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Premier_of_Quebec "Premier of Quebec") for five non-consecutive terms between 1936 and 1959. The controversies associated with Duplessis, and particularly the corruption and abuse concerning the Duplessis Orphans, have led to the popular historic conception of his term as Premier as _[La Grande Noirceur](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grande_Noirceur "Grande Noirceur")_ ("The Great Darkness") by its critics. 
The Duplessis Orphans have accused both the government of Quebec and the [Catholic Church](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Catholic_Church "Catholic Church") of wrongdoing. The Church has denied involvement in the scandal, and disputes the claims of those seeking financial compensation for harm done.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Duplessis_Orphans#cite_note-2)
It is believed to be the largest case of child abuse in Canadian history outside of the [Canadian Indian residential school system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Canadian_Indian_residential_school_system "Canadian Indian residential school system").[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Duplessis_Orphans#cite_note-3)[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Duplessis_Orphans#cite_note-4)
## Background
[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Duplessis_Orphans&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Background")]
During the 1940s and 1950s, limited [social services](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Social_services "Social services") were available to residents of [Quebec](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quebec "Quebec"). Before the [Quiet Revolution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quiet_Revolution "Quiet Revolution") of the 1960s,[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Duplessis_Orphans#cite_note-Farnsworth-5) most of the social services available were provided through the [Catholic Church](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Catholic_Church "Catholic Church"). Among their charges were people considered to be socially vulnerable: those living in poverty, alcoholics or other individuals deemed unable to retain work, unwed mothers, and [orphans](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orphan "Orphan").[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Duplessis_Orphans#cite_note-Ref_3-6)
The Catholic Church urged[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Duplessis_Orphans#cite_note-7) many mothers to admit children to orphanages despite not having been formally orphaned due to their "bastard" status (being born to [unwed mothers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Single_parent "Single parent")). Some of these orphanages were operated by Catholic religious institutions, due to a lack of secular investment in social services; they encouraged unwed mothers to leave their children there, so that they might be raised in the Catholic Church. Maternity homes for unwed mothers, too, then prevalent, often encouraged the giving up of these "bastard" children.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Duplessis_Orphans#cite_note-8)[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Duplessis_Orphans#cite_note-Ref_2-9)
The _Loi sur les Asiles d'aliénés_ (Lunatic Asylum Act) of 1909 governed [mental institution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Psychiatric_hospital "Psychiatric hospital") admissions until 1950. The law stated the mentally ill could be committed for three reasons: to care for them, to help them, or as a measure to maintain social order in public and private life. However, the act did not define what a disruption of social order was, leaving the decision to admit patients up to psychiatrists.[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Duplessis_Orphans#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThifaultPerreault2002133-10)
The [provincial government](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Government_of_Quebec "Government of Quebec") of [Union Nationale](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Union_Nationale_\(Quebec\) "Union Nationale \(Quebec\)") [Premier](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Premier_of_Quebec "Premier of Quebec") [Maurice Duplessis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maurice_Duplessis "Maurice Duplessis") received [subsidies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subsidy "Subsidy") from the federal government for building hospitals, but received substantially fewer subsidies to support orphanages. Government contributions were only $1.25 a day for orphans, but $2.75 a day for psychiatric patients. This disparity in funding provided a strong financial incentive for reclassification. Under Duplessis, the provincial government was responsible for a significant number of healthy older children being deliberately classified as mentally ill[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Duplessis_Orphans#cite_note-11)[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Duplessis_Orphans#cite_note-12)[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Duplessis_Orphans#cite_note-13) and sent to psychiatric hospitals, based on diagnoses made for fiscal reasons.[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Duplessis_Orphans#cite_note-tce-14) Duplessis also signed an [order-in-council](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Order_in_Council "Order in Council") which changed the classification of orphanages into hospitals in order to provide them with federal subsidies.[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Duplessis_Orphans#cite_note-Ref_4-15)
A commission in the early 1960s investigating mental institutions after Duplessis' death revealed one-third of the 22,000 patients classified as "mentally ill" were classified as such for the province's financial benefit, and not due to any real psychiatric deficit.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Duplessis_Orphans#cite_note-Farnsworth-5) Following the publication of the Bédard report in 1962, the province ceased retaining the institutional notion of "asylum". When many of the orphans reached adulthood, in light of these institutional changes, they were permitted to leave the facilities.[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Duplessis_Orphans#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThifaultPerreault2002133-10)
## Impacts on orphans
[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Duplessis_Orphans&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Impacts on orphans")]
Years later, long after these institutions were closed, survivors of the asylums began to speak out about [child abuse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Child_abuse "Child abuse") which they endured at the hands of some staff and medical personnel.[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Duplessis_Orphans#cite_note-16)[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Duplessis_Orphans#cite_note-17) Many who have spoken publicly about their experiences say that they had been abused [physically](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physical_abuse "Physical abuse") and [sexually](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Child_sexual_abuse "Child sexual abuse"), and were subjected to [lobotomies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lobotomy "Lobotomy"), [electroshock](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electroconvulsive_therapy "Electroconvulsive therapy") and [straitjackets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Straitjacket "Straitjacket").[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Duplessis_Orphans#cite_note-tce-14)
In a psychiatric study completed by one of the involved hospitals, middle-aged Duplessis Orphans reported more physical and mental impairments than the [control group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Treatment_and_control_groups "Treatment and control groups"). In addition, the orphans were less likely to be married or to have a healthy social life. 80% reported they had suffered a [traumatic](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/trauma "wikt:trauma") experience between the ages of 7 and 18. Over 50% said they had undergone physical, mental, or sexual abuse. About 78% reported difficulty functioning socially or emotionally in their adult life.[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Duplessis_Orphans#cite_note-Ref_14-18)
## Legal recourse in the 1990s
[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Duplessis_Orphans&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Legal recourse in the 1990s")]
By the 1990s, about 3,000 survivors and a large group of supporters formed the Duplessis Orphans Committee, seeking [damages](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Damages "Damages") from the Quebec provincial government. In March 1999, the provincial government made an offer of approximately [CAD$](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Canadian_dollar "Canadian dollar")15,000 as full compensation to each of the victims. The offer was rejected and the provincial government was harshly criticized, with Quebec's [ombudsman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ombudsman#Canada "Ombudsman") at the time, Daniel Jacoby, saying that the government's handling of the affair trivialized the abuse alleged by the victims.[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Duplessis_Orphans#cite_note-CBC_1999-19) In 2001, the claimants received an increased offer from the provincial government for a flat payment of $10,000 per person, plus an additional $1,000 for each year of [wrongful confinement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wrongful_confinement "Wrongful confinement") to a mental institution. The offer amounted to approximately $25,000 per orphan,[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Duplessis_Orphans#cite_note-CBC-20) but did not include any compensation for alleged victims of sexual or other abuse. 
After the offer was accepted by representatives of the Duplessis Orphans Committee, the result was bitterly contested by other members upon learning that under the terms of the [settlement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Legal_settlement "Legal settlement"), the committee's lawyer, president, and former [public relations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Public_relations "Public relations") official would receive six- to seven-figure payments, in comparison with the paltry amount given to the actual victims. The committee subsequently voted to replace both the president and the public relations official.[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Duplessis_Orphans#cite_note-Mirror2002-21) Critics of the judgment pointed out that three of the bureaucrats running the government's compensation program were being paid over $1,000 per day for work,[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Duplessis_Orphans#cite_note-Mirror2003-22) whereas the orphans themselves received the same amount of money for an entire year of their confinement. 
Seven religious communities were involved in operating some of the facilities: the [Sisters of Providence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sisters_of_Providence_\(Montreal\) "Sisters of Providence \(Montreal\)"), the [Sisters of Mercy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sisters_of_Mercy "Sisters of Mercy"), the [Grey Nuns of Montreal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grey_Nuns "Grey Nuns"), the Sisters of Charity of Quebec, the [Little Franciscans of Mary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Little_Franciscans_of_Mary "Little Franciscans of Mary"), the Brothers of Notre-Dame-de-la-Misericorde, and the [Brothers of Charity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brothers_of_Charity "Brothers of Charity").[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Duplessis_Orphans#cite_note-Farnsworth-5) When the settlement with the provincial government was reached, the committee agreed to drop any further legal action against the Catholic Church.[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Duplessis_Orphans#cite_note-tce-14) This offended some survivors; in 2006, one of the orphans, Martin Lécuyer, stated, "It's important for me, that the Church, the priests, that they recognize they were responsible for the sexual abuse, and the aggression. It's not for the government to set that peace... It's an insult, and it's the biggest proof that the government is an accomplice of the Church."[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Duplessis_Orphans#cite_note-ref_3-23): 376 
## Aftermath
[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Duplessis_Orphans&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Aftermath")]
In 1999, researchers [Léo-Paul Lauzon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/L%C3%A9o-Paul_Lauzon "Léo-Paul Lauzon") and Martin Poirier issued a report arguing that both the Quebec provincial government and the Catholic Church made substantial profits by falsely certifying thousands of Quebec orphans as mentally ill during Duplessis' premiership. The authors made a conservative estimate that religious groups received $70 million in subsidies (measured in 1999 dollars) by claiming the children as "mentally deficient", while the government saved $37 million simply by having one of its orphanages redesignated from an educational institution to a psychiatric hospital. A representative of a religious order involved with the orphanages accused the authors of making "false assertions".[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Duplessis_Orphans#cite_note-24)[_[better source needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability#Questionable_sources "Wikipedia:Verifiability")_] In 2010, it was estimated that approximately 300–400 of the original Duplessis Orphans were still alive.[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Duplessis_Orphans#cite_note-25)
On March 4, 1999, after a criminal investigation where 240 Duplessis Orphans alleged 321 criminal accusations against those in charge of the hospitals, former Quebec Premier [Lucien Bouchard](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lucien_Bouchard "Lucien Bouchard") declared a public apology to the orphans, but "without blaming or imputing legal responsibility to anyone."[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Duplessis_Orphans#cite_note-Rowe20210728-26)
## Fate of human remains
[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Duplessis_Orphans&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Fate of human remains")]
In 2004, some Duplessis Orphans asked the Quebec government to unearth an abandoned cemetery in the east end of [Montreal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Montreal "Montreal"), which they believed to have held the remains of orphans who may have been the subject of [human experimentation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Human_experimentation "Human experimentation"). According to testimony by individuals who were at the Cité de St-Jean-de-Dieu insane asylum, the orphans in the asylum's care were routinely used as non-consensual experimental subjects, and many died as a consequence. The group wanted the government to exhume the bodies so that [autopsies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Autopsy "Autopsy") may be performed.[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Duplessis_Orphans#cite_note-CBC_2004-27) In November 2010, the Duplessis Orphans made their case before the [United Nations Human Rights Council](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Nations_Human_Rights_Council "United Nations Human Rights Council").[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Duplessis_Orphans#cite_note-28) In 2021, preliminary ground-penetrating radar analyses on grounds around former [Canadian Indian residential schools](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Canadian_Indian_residential_schools "Canadian Indian residential schools") allegedly indicated the presence of unmarked graves that could [include the remains of Indigenous children](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Canadian_Indian_residential_school_gravesites "Canadian Indian residential school gravesites") that were also mainly administered by Christian churches. This has spurred further calls for the Quebec government and the Catholic Church to excavate former psychiatric hospital sites where the orphans were committed, with a class action lawsuit launched in 2018 denouncing the earlier settlement as "an insult" and not a "true apology" by the government and religious organizations.[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Duplessis_Orphans#cite_note-Rowe20210728-26)

# Duplessis Orphans
Article by  [Serge Dupuis](https://thecanadianencyclopedia.ca/en/author/serge-dupuis)
Published Online  November 21, 2007 
Last Edited  December 22, 2020 
Share Print
The Duplessis orphans were a cohort of children placed, from 1935 to 1964, in nurseries, orphanages and psychiatric hospitals, where many of them were mistreated or abused. A significant number of these children were falsely diagnosed as being mentally defective, to enable the institutions housing them to receive subsidies allocated to psychiatric facilities. This practice primarily occurred under Premier[ Maurice Duplessis](https://www.thecanadianencyclopedia.ca/en/article/maurice-le-noblet-duplessis), whose name has therefore been used to designate these children. Following a number of years of legal battles and political pressure, most of the Duplessis orphans have obtained a measure of compensation from the Quebec state.
_This article contains sensitive material such as physical and sexual abuse that may not be suitable for all audiences._
![Notre-Dame-de-la -Merci Orphanage, Huberdeau, c. 1941](https://d3d0lqu00lnqvz.cloudfront.net/06M_P833S3D0722_0001_1.jpg)

Notre-Dame-de-la-Merci Orphanage in Huberdeau, Quebec was one of several institutions included in the Duplessis Orphans settlement.

### Sociopolitical Background
In 1948, the [federal government ](https://www.thecanadianencyclopedia.ca/en/article/federal-government)introduced a subsidy program with respect to provincial health services. At that time, the Premier of [Quebec](https://www.thecanadianencyclopedia.ca/en/article/quebec), [Maurice Duplessis](https://www.thecanadianencyclopedia.ca/en/article/maurice-le-noblet-duplessis), instead advocated that institutions managed by religious communities be funded by private sources. His primary concern was federal interference in their activities. Nevertheless, the federal funding of hospitals and private care institutions increased substantially in Quebec during this period.
From 1935 to 1964, thousands of orphaned children were abandoned (sometimes because they were born out of wedlock) or entrusted to the State by a parent or other members of their family. These orphans were raised in facilities like nurseries, orphanages or psychiatric hospitals run by Catholic congregations.
In 1999, researcher Martin Poirier and Professor Léo-Paul Lauzon maintained that the institutionalization of some children beginning in the 1950s may have been done for financial reasons. They showed that, in 1956, the [federal government](https://www.thecanadianencyclopedia.ca/en/article/federal-government) allocated a subsidy of $0.70 per day for every child living in an orphanage, while allocating $2.25 for every patient diagnosed with a mental illness and confined in a psychiatric institution. These investigators demonstrated that falsely diagnosing some orphans with mental disorders enabled some Quebec institutions to receive these larger subsidies from the federal government.
### Abuse and Consequences
The children confined in these institutions received basic care; the discipline was harsh and some were neglected, molested or abused. Many were subjected to severe physical violence by the religious staff or by their guardians, who sometimes used leather straps or wooden rods. Those who were falsely diagnosed with an intellectual disability were medicated. Some even underwent electroshock treatment.
The children subjected to abusive treatment in these institutions had no opportunity for healthy development. The level of education they were given was largely inadequate. They did not have the support of a normal family and could not easily take up a career. Many saw their [mental health](https://www.thecanadianencyclopedia.ca/en/article/mental-health) profoundly affected.
The awareness of this phenomenon began with the publication of autobiographies such as the _Les fous crient au secours_ (1961) and _Ma chienne de vie_ (1964). These autobiographies exposed the situation of patients in the asylums. The _Rapport de la Commission d’étude des hôpitaux psychiatriques_ submitted to the Government of Quebec in 1962 helped bring an end to this practice of committing children to psychiatric hospitals.
The children who left the institutions were not equipped to live in the outside world. They disappeared from public awareness for approximately twenty years. Their story resurfaced in 1991, with the publication of _Les enfants de Duplessis_ by Pauline Gill (1991). This led to the publication of many other stories and accounts that laid bare the experiences of these children.

### Legal Proceedings
In 1992, Hervé Bertrand and other individuals institutionalized during their childhood as orphans began the process, with attorney support, of initiating a class action lawsuit. They were able to meet with dozens of people who had been institutionalized during their childhood, to paint a picture of the events and experiences common to these orphans.
In 1993, seven requests for permission to launch a class action lawsuit were submitted to the Court of Quebec. The prospect of class actions was initially a source of hope; however, a judgment rejected the requests in 1995, stating that they did not meet the criteria required for class actions. Moreover, the initiative was not supported by the Quebec Class Action Fund and most of the orphans did not have the means to fund these legal proceedings on their own. The charges for which they were seeking redress included the erroneous placement of orphans in psychiatric hospitals and the lack of adequate education. Although they did not deny placing children in institutions, the religious congregations noted the problems with the lack of personnel and money with which they were dealing at the time. They did, however, dispute the accusations of abuse and attempted to prevent them from devaluing all of the work done by the religious congregations throughout Quebec history.
Meanwhile, the Montreal police suggested to some of the orphans that they file formal complaints regarding acts of a criminal nature. The file was transferred to the [Québec Provincial Police](https://www.thecanadianencyclopedia.ca/en/article/quebec-provincial-police), as the seven institutions involved were located in different regions. At times, some of the complainants found the investigation intimidating, disrespectful or misleading. Following the assessment of the 321 complaints lodged by the orphans, Paul Bégin, the [Attorney General](https://www.thecanadianencyclopedia.ca/en/article/attorney-general) of Quebec, decided not to proceed. This decision was explained by a lack of evidence, statutes of limitation, the withdrawal of some complaints, and the fact that some of the accused had died.
### Formal Apologies and Compensation
The Ombudsman, Daniel Jacoby, did, however, agree to the request by the representatives of the orphans to investigate the case. In January 1997, he filed a report recommending that the orphans receive, at the very least, a public apology from the parties involved and suggested compensation scenarios to be funded by the Quebec state, the medical profession and the religious congregations.
In November 1997, the federal Minister of Justice, [Anne McLellan](https://www.thecanadianencyclopedia.ca/en/article/anne-mclellan), mandated the Law Commission of Canada to assess “the measures to redress physical and sexual abuse of children in institutions… managed, funded or sponsored by the government.” A study conducted in 1997 compared the quality of life and mental health of a group of Duplessis orphans with those of another group of low-income individuals who participated in a study conducted in 1988. The findings showed the potential for long-term negative effects of the institutionalization on the children. The Duplessis orphan group had a significantly lower level of education, more marked attachment difficulties, a lower degree of well-being and a higher level of stress.
In March 1999, the Government of Quebec, under [Lucien Bouchard](https://www.thecanadianencyclopedia.ca/en/article/lucien-bouchard), made a public apology to the Duplessis orphans. A $300,000 grant was paid to the Duplessis Orphans Committee. A $3-million fund to assist approximately 3,000 victims was also created, but no compensation was awarded on an individual basis. Further, the Quebec bishops refused to apologize and dismissed the idea of paying financial compensation.
In the spring of 2000, the _Comité d’appui pour la justice aux orphelins de Duplessis_ obtained the support of public figures, including former PQ Health Minister Denis Lazure and some members of the medical profession, to reopen the debate regarding compensation for the formerly institutionalized children. On 30 June 2001, the Duplessis Orphans Committee accepted the government’s offer of compensation. Under the National Reconciliation Program for Duplessis Orphans, this offer provided for $10,000 per person, in addition to a further $1,000 per year of confinement, up to a maximum of $25,000. Any individuals who accepted such compensation were required to waive any further actions regarding the damages covered under this program.
Subsequent to 2001, more than 1,000 Duplessis orphans have thus shared compensation totalling approximately $26 million. In 2007, a new Quebec government program provided that an additional 1,270 orphans committed to non-psychiatric institutions would receive $15,000 each. In total, the Quebec government received more than 6,500 requests for financial assistance from Duplessis orphans. Although it did not offer a formal apology, the _Collège des médecins_ did express its regrets in a letter sent to the orphans in 2012.
In March 2018, some of the orphans filed a class action suit for $875,000 each against 8 religious communities. However, the suit was rejected by the Superior Court of Quebec. Two other class action suits were filed in April 2018 and July 2020.

In a tucked-away office at Montreal's Concordia University, a video conference connects two groups of survivors separated by an ocean but linked by their so-called "illegitimate" births — Quebec's Duplessis Orphans and the survivors of Ireland's Mother and Baby Homes.
One by one, they introduce themselves, starting with their names and where they were born: Mount Providence orphanage in Montreal, Saint Patrick's Home in Dublin, Baie-Saint-Paul orphanage in Quebec.
Communication is slow and halting; the Quebecers speak French, the Irish, English. Some never learned to read or write.
But when survivors hear the familiar story — even in a foreign language — they nod along.
> - Nestor
On both sides of the ocean, children born to women out of wedlock were abandoned to institutions run by the Catholic Church, in many cases falsely labelled as mentally deficient and abused sexually and physically for years.
"When you [are] a bastard … [it's like] being born into a garbage can," says Quebecer Louis-Joseph Hébert, or Nestor, as he prefers to be called.
"You never have a happy life. Nobody will know you. You will be the dirt of the society."
He says the nuns gave him the surname Hébert, just like every other baby born in the same month as he was.
Nestor wishes he could cry about the abuse that he suffered and witnessed during his childhood, but he can't. 
"I have trouble to cry when I have a big emotion. [I would be] so happy if I could cry."
When he cried as a child, he says, the nuns beat him.
Even as an adult, it's something he can't shake.
![](https://i.cbc.ca/ais/1.4143242,1496419517000/full/max/0/default.jpg?im=Crop%2Crect%3D%280%2C0%2C1334%2C750%29%3B)
A group of Duplessis Orphans exchanged stories by video conference with the survivors of Irish Mother and Baby Homes Thursday. It was the first time the two groups have met. (Jaela Bernstien/CBC)
Nestor and his fellow Quebec survivors were born in or turned over to Catholic-run institutions in the 1940s and 50s. They lived out their childhood — and in many cases, their early adult years as well — in orphanages-turned-psychiatric institutions.
They were labelled as psychiatric patients as part of a plan by then-premier Maurice Duplessis to obtain federal subsidies.
> - Nestor
As the Duplessis Orphans shared those stories during Thursday's video conference, the Dublin survivors chimed in with similar anecdotes.
They pointed to birth records and medical documents that declared them mentally deficient. 
  * [**Mass grave at former Irish Catholic home for mothers and babies confirmed by investigators**](http://www.cbc.ca/news/world/mass-grave-ireland-orphanage-commission-1.4008349)


The parallels are striking — both groups have stories of physical and sexual abuse, being forced to take medication and to perform hard labour as children — all because, as they put it, the Church regarded them as "children of sin."
  * **[CBC Digital Archives: The Duplessis Orphans](http://www.cbc.ca/archives/topic/the-duplessis-orphans)**


They paid the price, says Nestor.
"We never did nothing wrong to God," he protests. "I didn't even know Him. My eyes weren't even open, I was in the stomach of my mother."
![](https://i.cbc.ca/ais/1.4143246,1496437484000/full/max/0/default.jpg?im=Crop%2Crect%3D%2877%2C2%2C1039%2C645%29%3B)
Hervé Bertrand, one of the surviving Duplessis Orphans, holds the microphone during a conversation with Ireland's Mother and Baby Home survivors. (Jaela Bernstien/CBC)
The Catholic Church has never offered a specific apology to the Duplessis Orphans.
In 2001, then-Quebec [premier Bernard Landry offered compensation](http://www.cbc.ca/news/canada/duplessis-orphans-accept-quebec-s-final-offer-1.278029) and apologized for the "sombre episode in our history." 
  * [**Duplessis orphans accept Quebec's final offer**](http://www.cbc.ca/news/canada/duplessis-orphans-accept-quebec-s-final-offer-1.278029)


In 2006, the Quebec government announced it would [pay a further $26 million in compensation](http://www.cbc.ca/news/canada/montreal/duplessis-orphans-get-26m-from-quebec-1.620714). 
Survivors were required to sign a waiver declaring that, in exchange, they would not take legal action against the Catholic Church.

It was Canadian composer Alyssa Ryvers who helped connect the Duplessis Orphans to some of the Irish survivors.
She'd worked with the Quebec survivors for years, [using music to share their stories](http://alyssaryvers.com/odp.html).

The burial site was found at a former Catholic care home.

"I thought to myself, 'Oh my God, this sounds a lot like what happened here,'" Ryvers said. 
She managed to meet a group of adults who'd lived through the Irish care-homes experience, and she was struck by how their stories echoed those of the Duplessis Orphans.
"Sitting around those survivors, it was the weirdest thing.... There was just this sort of sameness," she says.

"It seems clear that there were some parallels, and we could try to get to the bottom of why those parallels may or may not exist."
"It's going to be interesting to see how things unfold in the next while. I don't think it's the end of it," she says.

### Church, State, Bureaucracy, and Delayed Justice in Mid-20th-Century Quebec

## Introduction: When Classification becomes Violence
In mid-twentieth-century [Quebec](https://brewminate.com/governance-and-sites-of-power-in-early-modern-north-american-new-france/), thousands of children classified as orphans were redefined on paper as psychiatric patients. Under the government of Premier [Maurice Duplessis](https://thecanadianencyclopedia.ca/en/article/maurice-le-noblet-duplessis), administrative reclassification transformed institutions originally intended for child care into facilities eligible for higher federal subsidies tied to mental health funding. What appeared to be a matter of bureaucratic adjustment in fact reshaped the lives of vulnerable children, subjecting many to confinement, forced labor, neglect, and abuse. The Duplessis Orphans scandal reveals how classification, when embedded in political and religious authority, can function not as neutral description but as an instrument of systemic harm.
The regime of Duplessis governed Quebec during much of the 1940s and 1950s through a model often described as clerical nationalism, in which the Catholic Church exercised sweeping authority over education, healthcare, and social services. In practical terms, this meant that religious orders administered a vast network of orphanages, hospitals, and schools, operating with significant autonomy but within a political environment that reinforced ecclesiastical legitimacy. Provincial policy did not merely tolerate this arrangement; it depended upon it. The Church provided social infrastructure, and the state provided legal and financial reinforcement. When federal funding formulas allocated higher per capita subsidies to psychiatric institutions than to orphanages, a structural incentive emerged. Rather than expand social welfare expenditures directly, provincial authorities and religious administrators could reclassify facilities and, by extension, the children housed within them. Institutions were accordingly redesignated as psychiatric hospitals, and thousands of children were recorded as mentally ill, often without meaningful medical examination or due process. The bureaucratic act of labeling became a fiscal strategy embedded in governance itself. What appeared administratively rational within a funding framework translated into lifelong consequences for children who were transformed, in official records, from wards of care into patients of confinement.
The violence in this system was not solely physical, though physical abuse and deprivation were widely reported in later testimonies. It was also epistemic and administrative. To be named mentally ill within an institutional framework that denied recourse was to be placed outside the boundaries of credible speech. Classification determined confinement, labor assignments, educational access, and the plausibility of one’s own account of suffering. In this sense, paperwork functioned as both shield and weapon. The state could claim administrative legitimacy, the Church could invoke moral guardianship, and the children themselves were rendered suspect by the very labels imposed upon them.
The [Duplessis Orphans scandal](https://www.ebsco.com/research-starters/health-and-medicine/quebec-offers-support-abused-duplessis-orphans) was not merely an episode of cruelty concealed in an otherwise functioning democracy. It was a failure of governance made possible by the convergence of bureaucratic incentives, clerical authority, political power, and public deference. The case demonstrates how democratic societies can sustain systemic abuse when institutional classifications are treated as self-validating truths. Recognition and compensation, which emerged decades later, reveal not only delayed justice but the difficulty of confronting harms that were embedded in the ordinary language of governance. When classification becomes violence, it does so quietly, through forms, funding formulas, and institutional silence.
## Quebec under Duplessis: Clerical Nationalism and Political Authority
![The Duplessis Orphans: Mental Illness and Child Abuse in Mid-20th Century Quebec](https://brewminate.com/wp-content/plugins/a3-lazy-load/assets/images/lazy_placeholder.gif)**Cathedral-Basilica of Notre-Dame de Québec / Courtesy[Wikimedia Commons](https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Basilique-Cath%C3%A9drale_Notre-Dame_Qu%C3%A9bec.JPG)**
Duplessis served as Premier of Quebec from 1936 to 1939 and again from 1944 until his death in 1959, presiding over a political order that fused provincial nationalism with Catholic institutional authority. His party, the Union Nationale, cultivated a rhetoric of autonomy, tradition, and moral stability in opposition to federal centralization and perceived secular modernism. This political vision rested on the premise that Quebec’s distinct identity was inseparable from its Catholic heritage. The state, in this framework, did not displace ecclesiastical authority but reinforced it. Governance and guardianship were conceived as mutually supportive enterprises.
The structure of social services during this period reflected that ideological alignment. Education, hospitals, orphanages, reform schools, and charitable institutions were largely administered by religious orders rather than directly by secular bureaucracies. Clergy and members of religious congregations exercised managerial authority over institutions that in other provinces might have fallen more squarely within the purview of civil servants. This ecclesiastical administration extended beyond spiritual oversight into budgeting, staffing, discipline, and record-keeping. Religious superiors determined daily routines, educational curricula, and standards of moral evaluation. The provincial government, for its part, allocated funding, granted institutional charters, and conferred legal recognition while refraining from robust regulatory intrusion. Inspection regimes existed but were often deferential and episodic, reflecting an assumption that religious governance guaranteed ethical care. This arrangement was neither informal nor accidental. It was embedded in policy, statute, and public culture, reinforcing a model in which the Church functioned simultaneously as moral arbiter, social service provider, and quasi-public authority. In effect, a significant portion of Quebec’s welfare infrastructure operated through institutions that were publicly funded yet ecclesiastically controlled.
Duplessis’s political strategy depended in part on this partnership. By positioning himself as defender of provincial rights and traditional values, he secured support among rural constituencies and ecclesiastical leaders alike. His government emphasized agricultural development, anti-communism, and resistance to federal intervention, particularly in welfare and education. The result was a political culture that equated criticism of institutional authority with disloyalty to Quebec’s identity. Clerical endorsement strengthened the legitimacy of provincial leadership, while provincial leadership preserved the institutional dominance of the Church.
This fusion of power had concrete administrative consequences. When social services were delivered primarily through religious institutions, oversight mechanisms became diffuse and layered in ways that obscured accountability. Formal responsibility for vulnerable populations rested with the state, yet operational control lay with religious superiors whose authority derived from ecclesiastical hierarchy rather than civil service norms. Inspectors, where they existed, frequently shared the same cultural assumptions as the administrators they evaluated. Reports circulated within networks predisposed to trust clerical judgment. Accountability was mediated through shared convictions about moral competence rather than through systematic, transparent audit. In such an environment, institutional practices were shielded not only by secrecy but by presumption. Decisions made within convent walls or hospital corridors were presumed to align with communal virtue and divine mandate. The boundary between public policy and ecclesiastical discipline blurred, rendering it difficult to distinguish where state responsibility ended and religious autonomy began. This ambiguity functioned as protection, insulating administrative conduct from rigorous external scrutiny.
The cultural atmosphere of the period further reinforced this structure. [Catholic doctrine](https://brewminate.com/the-difference-between-catholic-and-non-denominational/) shaped public education and family life, and clerical authority was rarely contested in mainstream discourse. Media institutions operated within a framework that discouraged direct confrontation with religious leadership. While dissent existed, it was fragmented and often marginalized. For many citizens, institutional stability appeared preferable to social upheaval. The cost of that stability, however, was a reduced capacity for critical scrutiny of the systems entrusted with the care of vulnerable populations.
The later transformation known as the Quiet Revolution would expose the fragility of this arrangement. Beginning in the 1960s, Quebec underwent rapid secularization and administrative reform, transferring authority over education and healthcare from religious orders to the provincial state. Retrospective assessments of the Duplessis era frequently characterize it as “la Grande Noirceur,” or the Great Darkness, though historians debate the adequacy of that label. What remains less contested is that the clerical-nationalist model created structural conditions in which institutional decisions could proceed with minimal external oversight. The Duplessis Orphans scandal must be situated within this broader architecture of governance, where political authority and religious administration were so deeply interwoven that systemic harm could unfold under the banner of moral order.
## Bureaucratic Reclassification: From Orphan to “Mental Patient”
![The Duplessis Orphans: Mental Illness and Child Abuse in Mid-20th Century Quebec](https://brewminate.com/wp-content/plugins/a3-lazy-load/assets/images/lazy_placeholder.gif)**Maurice Duplessis in 1952 / Courtesy Library and Archives Canada,[Wikimedia Commons](https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Maurice_Duplessis.jpg)**
The transformation of orphanages into [psychiatric institutions](https://brewminate.com/institutional-memory-the-mentally-ill-and-st-elizabeths-hospital-in-the-late-19th-century/) did not begin with overt declarations of cruelty but with fiscal policy. During the 1940s and 1950s, federal cost-sharing arrangements provided higher subsidies for psychiatric hospitals than for orphan care facilities. Within Quebec’s clerical-nationalist framework, where religious orders administered most child welfare institutions, this disparity created a powerful incentive structure. Reclassification offered a means to secure additional funding without constructing entirely new facilities. The shift was administrative in form but transformative in effect. Buildings changed designation, institutional ledgers were rewritten, and children who had entered as wards of charity were entered anew as psychiatric cases.
The process of relabeling did not necessarily involve systematic medical evaluation consistent with contemporary psychiatric standards. Later inquiries and survivor testimonies indicate that many children were assigned diagnoses in the absence of thorough examination, longitudinal observation, or independent review. In numerous cases, institutional records contained formulaic language, minimal clinical description, and generalized characterizations that blurred the distinction between behavioral nonconformity, poverty, illegitimacy, and pathology. The diagnostic threshold appeared elastic, expanding to accommodate fiscal categories rather than medical necessity. What counted as mental illness could be shaped by institutional need. Once recorded, the designation carried legal implications that were difficult to contest, particularly for children without family advocates. The psychiatric label functioned as both administrative key and social barrier, unlocking federal subsidies while simultaneously foreclosing avenues of appeal. It transformed children into institutional subjects whose status was defined by documentation rather than demonstrable condition.
This shift altered the daily reality of institutional life in tangible and enduring ways. Psychiatric designation justified stricter confinement protocols, more rigid surveillance, and diminished expectations regarding education or social integration. Within facilities newly categorized as hospitals, children were frequently assigned labor framed as therapeutic engagement or institutional contribution. Tasks that maintained the operation of the facility could be interpreted as part of treatment rather than as coerced work. The language of therapy provided moral cover for practices that limited autonomy and reinforced hierarchy. Moreover, the stigma associated with psychiatric classification extended beyond childhood. Official files and civil records preserved diagnoses that followed many individuals into adulthood, affecting employment prospects, social standing, and self-understanding. Even when later investigations questioned the legitimacy of these diagnoses, the documentary trace remained powerful. Classification did not simply regulate institutional space; it shaped life trajectories long after release.
Administrative rationality provided a vocabulary that muted the moral stakes. No dramatic decree announced the conversion of thousands of children into psychiatric patients. Instead, forms were revised, categories adjusted, and funding streams redirected. Bureaucratic rationality provided a vocabulary that obscured moral stakes. Officials could point to budgetary necessity and institutional sustainability, while religious administrators could invoke care and discipline. The harm was embedded in procedure. In this sense, the Duplessis Orphans scandal demonstrates how modern administrative systems can transform vulnerable populations through paperwork alone, converting fiscal advantage into durable human cost.
## Institutionalized Harm: Conditions, Abuse, and Structural Incentives
![The Duplessis Orphans: Mental Illness and Child Abuse in Mid-20th Century Quebec](https://brewminate.com/wp-content/plugins/a3-lazy-load/assets/images/lazy_placeholder.gif)**Notre-Dame-de-la-Merci Orphanage in Huberdeau, Quebec was one of several institutions included in the Duplessis Orphans settlement. / Courtesy Bibliothèques et archives nationales du Québec, Public Domain**
Reclassification altered more than funding categories. It reshaped the institutional environment in which children lived, learned, and labored. Facilities that had once been designated as orphanages increasingly operated under regimes associated with psychiatric confinement. Architectural features such as locked wards, segregated dormitories, and restricted movement became normalized. The designation of [mental illness](https://brewminate.com/a-history-of-mental-illness-since-the-ancient-world-2/) justified heightened control, including physical restraint and isolation. Institutional order was framed as therapeutic necessity rather than coercive discipline, and the vocabulary of care masked the consolidation of authority over daily life.
Survivor testimonies and later investigations describe conditions marked by overcrowding, inadequate nutrition, and insufficient medical oversight. Dormitories often housed large numbers of children with minimal privacy, sometimes arranged in regimented rows that emphasized surveillance over comfort. Sanitation standards varied widely, and in some facilities resources were stretched thin by expanding patient rolls made possible through reclassification. Staffing ratios were low, with limited numbers of trained psychiatric professionals overseeing populations officially designated as mentally ill. Religious personnel, many of whom lacked specialized clinical training, assumed responsibilities that blurred the line between spiritual guidance and psychiatric management. In some institutions, children reported corporal punishment, humiliation, and prolonged isolation as responses to perceived disobedience. Forced labor, including agricultural work, kitchen duties, and maintenance tasks, was frequently framed as character-building or therapeutic activity. Educational opportunities were curtailed, particularly for those labeled as severely impaired, reinforcing a cycle in which diminished expectations justified reduced instruction. The cumulative effect was not merely episodic mistreatment but a sustained environment in which deprivation, discipline, and diminished opportunity were woven into daily institutional practice.
Abuse in such settings did not necessarily require constant overt brutality to produce harm. The institutional framework itself generated vulnerability. Children labeled as mentally ill possessed diminished credibility in the eyes of external authorities. Complaints could be interpreted as symptoms of pathology rather than as evidence of mistreatment. The classificatory system reinforced a cycle of disbelief. When suffering was voiced, it was filtered through diagnostic assumptions that rendered it suspect. In this way, administrative categories insulated institutions from scrutiny, allowing harmful practices to persist without sustained external challenge.
Financial and administrative incentives further entrenched these dynamics. Financial models rewarded institutional capacity and occupancy, creating pressure to maintain or expand patient populations rather than to question their composition. Federal subsidies tied to psychiatric designation meant that each reclassified child represented not only an administrative entry but a revenue stream. Religious orders operating the facilities faced dual imperatives: spiritual guardianship and institutional solvency. Budgets had to be balanced, buildings maintained, and staff supported. Within this fiscal architecture, reducing the number of psychiatric patients could threaten financial stability. The continuation of funding streams depended upon the maintenance of psychiatric status, and reclassification back to orphan care offered no comparable financial incentive. Consequently, the system was oriented toward preservation rather than reassessment. Institutional survival and fiscal stability could outweigh individualized evaluation or reform, embedding economic logic within the moral language of care.
The broader political environment reinforced this insulation. Provincial authorities, aligned with clerical administration, exercised limited adversarial oversight. Public trust in religious governance discouraged investigative zeal. Media attention to internal institutional conditions remained sporadic and often muted. The absence of sustained scrutiny did not imply universal ignorance; rather, it reflected a culture in which challenging institutional authority carried social and political costs. Harm could persist not because it was entirely hidden, but because it was insufficiently confronted.
Harm in the Duplessis era illustrates how abuse can become normalized within administrative systems. When classification, funding incentives, moral authority, and political partnership converge, harmful practices may be reproduced without requiring explicit directives of cruelty. The suffering of the Duplessis Orphans was not solely the product of individual malice, though individual acts of abuse occurred. It emerged from a structure that aligned economic incentives with classificatory expansion and shielded institutional conduct through deference. This convergence created a self-reinforcing environment in which questioning diagnoses threatened financial flows, challenging authority risked social ostracism, and institutional discipline was recast as benevolent supervision. In such a configuration, reform required more than exposing isolated incidents; it demanded dismantling the administrative and cultural architecture that rendered those incidents intelligible and sustainable. Harm was not an anomaly within the system, but a predictable outcome of incentives the system was poorly equipped, and perhaps insufficiently motivated, to resist.
## Public Knowledge, Silence, and Moral Evasion
![The Duplessis Orphans: Mental Illness and Child Abuse in Mid-20th Century Quebec](https://brewminate.com/wp-content/plugins/a3-lazy-load/assets/images/lazy_placeholder.gif)**During the 1940s and ’50s, Quebec children born to unmarried women were often given up to Catholic-run orphanages and psychiatric institutions. / Courtesy Congrégation des Soeurs de la Charité de Québec/Patrimoine immatériel religieux du Québec, Public Domain**
The endurance of the Duplessis Orphans system cannot be explained solely by administrative concealment. It also depended upon patterns of public perception shaped by deference, stigma, and selective attention. In mid-twentieth-century Quebec, Catholic institutions occupied a position of profound moral authority. Religious orders were not merely service providers; they were embodiments of spiritual discipline and communal continuity. Convents, parishes, and church-run schools structured daily life, and clerical leadership carried both spiritual and social prestige. For many citizens, religious caregivers symbolized sacrifice, humility, and charitable devotion. This moral capital generated a presumption of benevolence that insulated institutional practices from suspicion. Administrative changes within church-operated facilities were rarely interpreted through a lens of fiscal opportunism. When orphanages were redesignated as psychiatric institutions, the shift could be understood as an adaptation to evolving standards of care rather than as a financial strategy tied to subsidy formulas. Institutional authority framed the narrative, and the public largely received it within a framework already predisposed toward trust.
Stigma played a central role in sustaining silence. Children born outside marriage, those from impoverished families, and those deemed socially disruptive already occupied marginal positions within the social order. When such children were classified as mentally ill, the designation reinforced preexisting assumptions about deviance and deficiency. The psychiatric label did not disrupt dominant narratives; it confirmed them. Families, where they existed, often lacked the social capital to challenge institutional authority. In some cases, parents had relinquished children under economic or social pressure and were ill-equipped to contest subsequent decisions. The marginal status of the children themselves reduced the likelihood of widespread public mobilization on their behalf.
Information about institutional conditions did not circulate widely, yet neither was it entirely absent. Occasional reports, local rumors, and private doubts emerged, but they rarely coalesced into sustained public critique. Media institutions operated within cultural constraints that discouraged direct confrontation with clerical authority. Journalists and editors who relied on community relationships and ecclesiastical goodwill faced incentives to avoid antagonism. The absence of investigative reporting should not be mistaken for universal ignorance; rather, it reflected a media environment in which certain subjects were treated as sensitive or untouchable. Silence, in this sense, was not purely imposed from above but reproduced through professional caution and social convention.
Moral evasion also operated at the level of individual conscience. For citizens invested in a vision of Quebec as a devout, cohesive, and morally guided society, acknowledging systemic abuse within church-administered institutions posed a destabilizing threat. To question the integrity of religious caregivers risked undermining faith in institutions that structured education, healthcare, and family life. The prospect of institutional wrongdoing introduced cognitive dissonance into a culture that linked Catholic authority with communal well-being. It was easier to interpret institutional suffering as unfortunate exception than as structural design. Isolated incidents could be rationalized as the failings of particular individuals rather than as products of a system aligned with financial and political incentives. Deference to authority intersected with psychological incentives to preserve collective self-understanding. Silence became a form of self-protection, shielding not only institutions but the public’s image of itself and its moral order.
The Duplessis Orphans scandal therefore demonstrates how systemic wrongdoing can persist within democratic societies not solely through coercion but through habituated trust and selective disbelief. Public reluctance to confront institutional authority allowed administrative classifications and funding incentives to operate with minimal challenge. Recognition and redress would come decades later, when secularization and archival access made retrospective critique possible. In the interim, moral evasion functioned as a stabilizing force. The system endured because too many actors, for too many reasons, found it easier to accept institutional narratives than to interrogate them.
## The Quiet Revolution and Retrospective Reckoning
![The Duplessis Orphans: Mental Illness and Child Abuse in Mid-20th Century Quebec](https://brewminate.com/wp-content/plugins/a3-lazy-load/assets/images/lazy_placeholder.gif)**“ _Maîtres chez nous_ ” (‘Masters in Our Own Home’) was the electoral slogan of the Liberal Party during the 1962 election during the Quiet Revolution. / Courtesy Bibliothèque et Archives nationales du Québec, [Wikimedia Commons](https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Maitres_chez_nous_1962.jpg)**
The political and cultural transformation known as the [Quiet Revolution](https://thecanadianencyclopedia.ca/en/article/quiet-revolution) altered the structural conditions that had enabled the Duplessis Orphans system to endure. Beginning in the early 1960s, Quebec underwent rapid secularization, administrative centralization, and educational reform. Authority over schools, hospitals, and social services gradually shifted from religious orders to provincial ministries staffed by civil servants. This transfer did not immediately expose past abuses, but it disrupted the institutional fusion that had shielded earlier practices from scrutiny. Secular governance introduced new oversight mechanisms, professional standards, and a vocabulary of rights that differed markedly from the clerical-nationalist framework of the preceding decades.
The Quiet Revolution also reshaped public discourse in ways that would eventually make retrospective accountability possible. Intellectuals, journalists, and policymakers increasingly questioned the cultural dominance of the Catholic Church and reevaluated the social order that had characterized the Duplessis era. Universities expanded, media environments diversified, and a new generation of civil servants approached governance with technocratic and secular assumptions. Retrospective critiques of “la Grande Noirceur” framed the period as one of authoritarian stagnation, though historians continue to debate the precision and fairness of that label. What matters for the Duplessis Orphans case is that the moral insulation surrounding clerical authority weakened. As educational and healthcare systems became integrated into provincial administration, bureaucratic transparency improved incrementally, and regulatory oversight acquired greater institutional independence. Archival materials, once dispersed across religious and provincial jurisdictions, became more accessible to researchers and journalists. The shift from ecclesiastical to bureaucratic governance did not guarantee transparency, but it altered the balance of power in ways that made systematic reexamination of past practices both politically conceivable and administratively feasible.
For decades, however, the experiences of the Duplessis Orphans remained fragmented and largely unacknowledged in official narratives. Many former institutionalized children carried the stigma of psychiatric diagnosis into adulthood, complicating efforts to organize collectively or to gain public sympathy. It was only in the late twentieth century, as survivor networks formed and investigative journalism revisited the period, that broader recognition began to emerge. Testimonies accumulated, challenging earlier administrative records and reframing institutional histories. What had once been isolated memories coalesced into a public claim.
Governmental response unfolded gradually and unevenly. In the 1990s, public pressure intensified, leading to inquiries and negotiations overcompensation. Survivors organized associations, retained legal counsel, and brought their experiences into courtrooms and legislative hearings. The Quebec government acknowledged that wrongful reclassification had occurred, though debates persisted regarding the scope of responsibility and the adequacy of redress. Officials distinguished between unlawful psychiatric designation and broader institutional mistreatment, a distinction that shaped eligibility criteria for compensation. Financial settlements were eventually established for recognized victims, but these programs were structured within legal frameworks that limited admission of direct liability. The language of regret and restitution operated alongside careful juridical phrasing designed to balance moral acknowledgment with institutional self-protection. Compensation represented a tangible response, yet it also illustrated the constraints of retrospective justice within a system seeking to reconcile past wrongdoing with contemporary political stability.
Retrospective reckoning raised deeper questions about historical responsibility. Apology and compensation addressed tangible harms, yet they could not fully reconstruct educational opportunities lost, reputations damaged, or psychological trauma endured. Nor could they entirely disentangle the roles of church and state in a system where authority had been so thoroughly interwoven. Public acknowledgment required confronting not only administrative error but the cultural and political conditions that had normalized classificatory expansion and institutional silence. The process of reckoning became both juridical and interpretive.
The legacy of the Duplessis Orphans within Quebec’s collective memory illustrates the limits and possibilities of democratic self-correction. Secularization and institutional reform created the conditions under which past practices could be reevaluated, yet recognition arrived only after many survivors had already lived the majority of their lives under the weight of imposed diagnoses. Delayed justice underscores the temporal fragility of accountability in democratic societies, where structural harm may persist beneath administrative routine long before it becomes publicly legible. Even when cultural transformation exposes prior wrongdoing, the passage of time complicates evidentiary reconstruction, legal remedy, and emotional repair. The Quiet Revolution did not undo the past, nor did compensation fully restore what had been taken. It did, however, alter the horizon against which the past could be judged, embedding the scandal within a broader narrative about institutional reform, secularization, and the ongoing negotiation between memory and governance.
## Structural Abuse in Democratic Contexts: Comparative Reflections
The Duplessis Orphans scandal challenges the assumption that systemic abuse belongs primarily to authoritarian regimes. Quebec in the mid-twentieth century operated within a constitutional democracy, with electoral competition, legislative procedure, and formal civil liberties. Yet the presence of democratic institutions did not prevent the convergence of bureaucratic classification, fiscal incentives, and moral authority from producing durable harm. The case underscores a central tension within democratic governance: legality and legitimacy can coexist with administrative practices that inflict durable injury on marginalized populations. Abuse need not arise from overt dictatorship. It can emerge from administrative rationality operating within culturally sanctioned hierarchies.
Comparative scholarship on institutional abuse reinforces this insight. Investigations into church-run residential schools, psychiatric hospitals, reformatories, and state-funded care facilities in other Western democracies reveal recurring structural patterns. In Canada, Ireland, Australia, and parts of the United States, inquiries have documented how vulnerable populations were confined within institutions that operated at the intersection of public funding and moral authority. In many of these cases, diagnostic categories, moral judgments, or developmental labels were expanded to justify prolonged institutionalization. Economic incentives often reinforced these classificatory decisions, as per capita funding models rewarded occupancy and institutional growth. Social stigma surrounding poverty, illegitimacy, disability, or ethnic difference further insulated institutions from external challenge. The Duplessis Orphans case shares these features: classificatory elasticity, fiscal motivation, and the marginal status of those affected. While Quebec’s clerical-nationalist framework provided its own historical specificity, the broader structural dynamics were not unique. They reflect a pattern in which administrative systems in democratic societies can convert categories of care into mechanisms of control.
What distinguishes structural abuse in democratic contexts is not the absence of formal rights but the mediation of those rights through institutional credibility. When institutions are widely trusted, their classifications acquire presumption of accuracy. Legal recourse may exist in principle, yet it remains inaccessible in practice to those lacking resources, credibility, or social standing. Administrative categories shape who is heard and who is dismissed. In the Duplessis Orphans system, psychiatric designation curtailed not only freedom of movement but also epistemic standing. The democratic framework remained intact, yet those most affected by institutional decisions were positioned outside its effective protections.
The broader lesson is not that democracy is illusory, but that it is structurally vulnerable. Institutional design shapes how power is exercised long before individual decisions are scrutinized. Funding formulas that reward confinement, oversight regimes that rely on internal reporting, and cultural norms that equate moral authority with institutional virtue can combine to create durable blind spots. The Duplessis Orphans scandal reveals how classificatory regimes and fiscal incentives may interact within democratic governance to produce outcomes inconsistent with proclaimed values of dignity and equality. Comparative reflection reveals that such outcomes are not aberrations limited to a single province or historical moment. They emerge when administrative logic proceeds without sustained external critique and when marginalized populations lack the leverage to challenge imposed identities. Democratic accountability demands more than electoral turnover and formal rights declarations. It requires vigilance toward the categories through which institutions define deviance, dependency, and normalcy. When those categories harden into unquestioned truths, structural harm can become routine even within societies that regard themselves as free.
## Conclusion: Delayed Justice and the Limits of Institutional Memory
The history of the Duplessis Orphans exposes a disquieting truth about modern governance: systemic harm can endure not because it is invisible, but because it is normalized through administrative language and authority. Classification transformed children into patients, fiscal policy reinforced confinement, and moral deference insulated administrators from scrutiny. The resulting system operated within a democratic framework yet produced outcomes inconsistent with democratic ideals. The scandal compels a reassessment of how administrative categories, when aligned with political and religious legitimacy, can convert vulnerability into institutional routine.
Delayed justice, when it arrived, carried both moral weight and practical limitation. Official acknowledgment and compensation recognized that wrongful reclassification had occurred, yet such measures unfolded decades after the original harm. By the time inquiries were conducted and settlements negotiated, many survivors had already borne the consequences of imposed diagnoses for the majority of their lives. Childhoods shaped by confinement had long since given way to adulthoods constrained by stigma, limited education, and damaged institutional records. Financial restitution, though symbolically significant and materially meaningful to some, could not restore lost schooling, interrupted development, or the formative years spent under coercive authority. Nor could formal apology fully address the erosion of trust in institutions that had claimed to provide care. The temporal gap between injury and recognition underscores a central tension in democratic accountability: systems capable of reform are not necessarily swift in confronting their own failures. Recognition often depends upon generational change, cultural transformation, and sustained advocacy by those who must relive trauma in order to secure acknowledgment.
Institutional memory presents a further constraint. Governments and religious bodies evolve, personnel change, and administrative frameworks are restructured. The diffusion of responsibility across time complicates efforts to assign culpability. Official reports and archival documents can reconstruct patterns, yet they cannot fully capture the lived experience of confinement or the psychological burden of stigmatization. As collective memory shifts, there is also a risk that structural lessons recede into symbolic acknowledgment rather than sustained vigilance. Commemoration without reform may satisfy moral expectation while leaving underlying vulnerabilities intact.
The Duplessis Orphans scandal ultimately invites reflection on the fragility of democratic safeguards. Elections, formal rights, and constitutional structures do not automatically prevent systemic abuse when administrative classifications harden into unquestioned truths. Democratic societies must continually interrogate the categories through which institutions define illness, deviance, and dependency. When bureaucratic incentives align with moral authority and public deference, harm can become embedded in routine procedure. Delayed justice may acknowledge wrongdoing, but it cannot substitute for proactive scrutiny. The limits of collective memory remind us that accountability requires not only retrospective recognition, but sustained attention to the frameworks that shape human classification in the present.

## Historical Context
### Orphan Care in Quebec Prior to the 1930s
In early 20th-century [Quebec](https://grokipedia.com/page/Quebec), rapid [urbanization](https://grokipedia.com/page/Urbanization) and economic hardships, including the transition from rural agrarian life to industrial centers like [Montreal](https://grokipedia.com/page/Montreal), contributed to elevated rates of family instability, [child abandonment](https://grokipedia.com/page/Child_abandonment), and [orphan](https://grokipedia.com/page/Orphan)hood. Strict Catholic moral norms stigmatized illegitimacy and premarital births, prompting many single mothers to anonymously abandon infants at church-run nurseries or foundling wheels, severing maternal ties under institutional policies emphasizing [penance](https://grokipedia.com/page/Penance) over [family reunification](https://grokipedia.com/page/Family_reunification). While precise illegitimacy rates for 1900–1930 remain underdocumented, historical records indicate that most children in institutions were not true [orphan](https://grokipedia.com/page/Orphan)s (losing both parents) but rather victims of [poverty](https://grokipedia.com/page/Poverty), parental death from disease or [World War I casualties](https://grokipedia.com/page/World_War_I_casualties), or temporary placements from destitute families unable to provide care. A [1933](https://grokipedia.com/page/1933) provincial report, reflecting conditions from prior decades, found that only 12 percent of institutionalized children were genuine [orphan](https://grokipedia.com/page/Orphan)s, with the majority stemming from [broken homes](https://grokipedia.com/page/Broken_Homes) or illegitimate births amid widespread working-class struggles.[](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-4)[[4]](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-4)[](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-5)[[5]](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-5) The [Catholic Church](https://grokipedia.com/page/Catholic_Church) assumed primary responsibility for orphan care, operating the vast majority of orphanages and crèches through religious orders such as the [Grey Nuns](https://grokipedia.com/page/Grey_Nuns) and Sisters of Providence, which established facilities like the Misericordia Hospital nursery in 1890 to handle abandoned infants. These institutions, supplemented by Protestant ones for minority communities (e.g., the Protestant Infants’ Home founded in 1870), relied heavily on private donations, charitable contributions, and volunteer labor rather than robust state support, reflecting Quebec's confessional welfare system where the Church filled gaps left by limited government intervention. Provincial funding was minimal and sporadic before the 1921 Public Assistance Act formalized subsidies, often amounting to per-child grants insufficient to cover basic needs, forcing reliance on ecclesiastical resources amid growing demand from urban poverty.[](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-5)[[5]](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-5)[](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-6)[[6]](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-6)[](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-4)[[4]](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-4) This systemic underfunding and institutional overload led to chronic overcrowding and substandard conditions in pre-1930s orphanages, with nurseries reporting alarmingly high [infant mortality](https://grokipedia.com/page/Infant_mortality)—such as 80 percent at Misericordia from 1890 to 1921—due to artificial feeding practices, poor [sanitation](https://grokipedia.com/page/Sanitation), and infectious outbreaks in under-resourced environments. Examples include the Youville nursery, operational since 1754, where 88 percent mortality occurred in 1875 from similar causal factors like [malnutrition](https://grokipedia.com/page/Malnutrition) and inadequate medical care, underscoring welfare gaps driven by economic pressures rather than deliberate [neglect](https://grokipedia.com/page/Neglect). These pressures created a fragile child welfare framework heavily dependent on religious operators, setting precedents for future reliance on institutional solutions amid fiscal constraints, in contrast to emerging federal subsidies for psychiatric facilities in subsequent decades that incentivized alternative classifications.[](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-5)[[5]](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-5)[](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-5)[[5]](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-5)
### Economic and Political Pressures Under Duplessis
The Union Nationale governments under [Maurice Duplessis](https://grokipedia.com/page/Maurice_Duplessis), governing [Quebec](https://grokipedia.com/page/Quebec) from 1936 to 1939 and 1944 to 1959, adhered to principles of [fiscal conservatism](https://grokipedia.com/page/Fiscal_conservatism), maintaining balanced budgets and reducing provincial debt during a period of post-Great Depression recovery. This approach emphasized limited state intervention in the economy, low taxation, and reliance on private and ecclesiastical institutions for [social services](https://grokipedia.com/page/Social_services), contrasting with expanding welfare states in other Canadian provinces following [World War II](https://grokipedia.com/page/World_War_II). Duplessis's administration forged a close alliance with the [Catholic Church](https://grokipedia.com/page/Catholic_Church), which operated most orphanages and welfare facilities, reflecting Quebec's conservative social policies that prioritized traditional institutional care over comprehensive public assistance programs.[](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-7)[[7]](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-7) Quebec's economy, heavily agricultural and rural, suffered prolonged effects from the Depression, with high [unemployment](https://grokipedia.com/page/Unemployment) and [poverty](https://grokipedia.com/page/Poverty) exacerbating family breakdowns and leading to surges in [child abandonment](https://grokipedia.com/page/Child_abandonment) during the [1940s](https://grokipedia.com/page/1940s). Wartime mobilization provided some industrial growth but did not alleviate underlying fiscal strains on provincial welfare budgets, as demands for orphan care intensified amid broader social hardships like illness and parental incapacity. The government's commitment to fiscal restraint limited expansions in social spending, directing resources toward [infrastructure](https://grokipedia.com/page/Infrastructure) and resource extraction rather than augmenting support for vulnerable populations.[](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-8)[[8]](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-8) A key structural pressure arose from federal-provincial funding disparities: while general orphanages received solely provincial per-diem rates, typically around $0.75 per [child](https://grokipedia.com/page/Child) daily, mental health institutions qualified for additional federal subsidies introduced in [1948](https://grokipedia.com/page/1948) under national health programs, often doubling effective funding to $1.50 or more per [patient](https://grokipedia.com/page/Patient). These incentives stemmed from Ottawa's shared-cost initiatives for psychiatric care, absent for [child](https://grokipedia.com/page/Child) welfare facilities, amid Quebec's resistance to federal encroachment on provincial domains. Such fiscal mechanics underscored pragmatic administrative adaptations to resource constraints without expanding the welfare apparatus.
## The Reclassification Mechanism
### Federal-Provincial Funding Disparities
In 1948, the Canadian federal government established the National Health Grants program, which provided subsidies to provinces for health infrastructure and services, including significantly higher per-patient reimbursements for psychiatric hospitals compared to welfare institutions like orphanages.[](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-9)[[9]](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-9) Under this framework, psychiatric facilities received federal funding at rates up to $2.25 per day per patient, while orphanages were reimbursed at only $0.75 per day per child, creating a stark fiscal disparity that incentivized reallocation of resources toward [mental health](https://grokipedia.com/page/Mental_health) classifications.[](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-10)[[10]](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-10) This structure stemmed from federal priorities to expand [mental health](https://grokipedia.com/page/Mental_health) capacity post-World War II, with provinces bearing primary responsibility for child welfare costs but sharing psychiatric expenses through Ottawa's contributions. Quebec's provincial administration, facing budget constraints during Maurice Duplessis's tenure from 1944 to 1959, adapted to these incentives by facilitating the certification of [children](https://grokipedia.com/page/Child) as mentally deficient or ill, thereby qualifying institutions for the elevated federal rates.[](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-1)[[1]](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-1) Orphanage operators, often religious orders managing facilities under provincial contracts, stood to gain substantially; reclassifying a [child](https://grokipedia.com/page/Child) shifted daily [funding](https://grokipedia.com/page/Funding) from the lower welfare tier to the subsidized psychiatric category, effectively tripling reimbursements without equivalent increases in operational demands.[](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-10)[[10]](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-10) Administrative records indicate that this mechanism generated an estimated $70–100 million in additional funds for Quebec institutions between the late 1940s and 1960, as documented in economic analyses of the era's welfare expenditures.[](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-10)[[10]](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-10) The funding gap directly correlated with observable shifts in institutional populations: Quebec's psychiatric admissions rose sharply after 1948, with facilities like those operated by Catholic orders reporting occupancy increases that aligned with subsidy receipts, independent of documented rises in actual mental health cases among children.[](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-11)[[11]](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-11) Provincial [health department](https://grokipedia.com/page/Health_department) reports from the period confirm that federal grants covered a larger share of costs for "mental deficiency" cases, prompting bureaucratic prioritization of such diagnoses to optimize fiscal transfers amid Duplessis-era austerity measures.[](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-1)[[1]](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-1) This adaptation, while exploiting a policy mismatch between federal [health](https://grokipedia.com/page/Health) priorities and provincial [child](https://grokipedia.com/page/Child) welfare obligations, underscored the rational economic calculus underlying the reclassifications.
### Administrative Processes for Certification
The certification of healthy orphans as mentally ill relied on streamlined medical protocols overseen by provincial physicians in collaboration with [Catholic Church](https://grokipedia.com/page/Catholic_Church) administrators, who managed the majority of Quebec's orphanages and asylums. These officials typically conducted brief, superficial evaluations—often limited to observations of behavioral delays attributed to institutional neglect rather than innate disorders—before issuing certificates of mental incapacity required for institutionalization under Quebec's [mental health](https://grokipedia.com/page/Mental_health) laws. Such documents enabled the legal transfer of children from underfunded orphanages to psychiatric facilities, with no mandatory [parental consent](https://grokipedia.com/page/Parental_consent) for those deemed abandoned or illegitimate, as state and ecclesiastical authorities held plenary guardianship.[](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-1)[[1]](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-1) Transfers peaked between 1949 and 1956, coinciding with federal funding reforms that provided $2.25 daily per patient in psychiatric hospitals versus $0.70 for orphan care, prompting orphanage directors to petition for rapid reclassifications; for instance, the Mont-Providence orphanage in [Montreal](https://grokipedia.com/page/Montreal) was redesignated as a psychiatric institution almost overnight in 1957 following church-led submissions to provincial authorities. Similarly, children were routed to asylums like Saint-Jean-de-Dieu through administrative orders backed by these certificates, bypassing rigorous diagnostic standards such as extended [psychological testing](https://grokipedia.com/page/Psychological_testing) or family histories.[](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-12)[[12]](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-12)[](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-13)[[13]](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-13) Archival documents from provincial health departments and church records reveal implicit pressures on institutions to achieve minimum occupancy thresholds for funding eligibility, resembling quotas in practice as underfilled psychiatric wards risked subsidy reductions; Quebec's Ombudsman report of 1997 highlighted how these mechanics prioritized fiscal compliance over clinical validity, with many certificates retroactively altered or fabricated to align with reimbursement claims.[](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-14)[[14]](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-14)[](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-1)[[1]](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-1)
## Institutional Conditions and Abuses
### Daily Life and Labor in Asylums
In psychiatric asylums repurposed from orphanages, such as St.-Julien Hospital and Mont Providence, daily routines for the Duplessis Orphans shifted abruptly after reclassification to emphasize manual labor over education, with work framed by staff as therapeutic occupation. At Mont Providence, children as young as 8 or 9, including Herve Bertrand, had lessons halted on March 18, 1954, and were assigned tasks like domestic chores and farm labor to sustain the facility, with earnings often remitted back to the institution rather than benefiting the children.[](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-13)[[13]](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-13)[](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-15)[[15]](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-15) Such labor was enforced rigorously, as Alice Quinton recalled at St.-Julien in the 1940s–1950s, where slow performance led to punitive measures, reflecting a broader church-run model where self-sufficiency justified child exploitation under the guise of moral and physical discipline.[](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-13)[[13]](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-13) Overcrowding defined institutional operations, straining resources and fostering chaotic environments in some facilities; St.-Julien accommodated approximately 500 orphans alongside 900 adult psychiatric patients, while Mont Providence held around 500 children aged 4–12 as noted in a 1960 inspection.[](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-13)[[13]](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-13) Nutrition was inadequate amid neglect, contributing to developmental delays where many children lacked basic skills like using utensils or telling time even into adolescence, a consequence of overburdened staff ratios such as one nun overseeing at least 10 infants under age 2.[](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-15)[[15]](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-15) Isolation from broader society was absolute, with orphans like Clarina Duguay barred from family contact or external education, confined to asylum grounds that prioritized containment over socialization, though routines varied by site—more regimented under direct religious supervision at some versus disorganized in larger, mixed-population wards.[](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-13)[[13]](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-13)[](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-16)[[16]](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-16)
### Medical Interventions and Physical Mistreatment
In psychiatric institutions where Duplessis orphans were confined during the 1940s and 1950s, children underwent [electroconvulsive therapy](https://grokipedia.com/page/Electroconvulsive_therapy) (ECT) as a treatment for diagnosed mental illnesses, with applications peaking in that era amid broader psychiatric practices.[](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-17)[[17]](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-17) Experimental pharmaceuticals were administered to control behavior and address purported conditions, often without [informed consent](https://grokipedia.com/page/Informed_consent) or standardized protocols, as corroborated by survivor accounts analyzed in clinical studies.[](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-18)[[18]](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-18) Lobotomies, a prefrontal leukotomy procedure, were performed on a limited number of institutionalized children, including documented cases like that of survivor Rod St-Aubin, who received the intervention as a young adult in [Quebec](https://grokipedia.com/page/Quebec) facilities, resulting in lasting cognitive and physical impairments.[](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-11)[[11]](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-11) These medical interventions were applied in understaffed asylums influenced by prevailing doctrines emphasizing discipline over therapeutic care, contributing to adverse outcomes such as heightened vulnerability to long-term [mental health](https://grokipedia.com/page/Mental_health) disorders. A peer-reviewed analysis of early experiences among Duplessis orphans identified [physical abuse](https://grokipedia.com/page/Physical_abuse), including beatings, as a primary aversive event inflicted by institutional staff, with 81 out of 81 participants reporting multiple forms of maltreatment before age 12.[](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-17)[[17]](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-17) Restraints, including straitjackets, were used routinely for behavioral control, linked to chronic understaffing ratios that exacerbated reliance on coercive measures rather than rehabilitative approaches.[](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-18)[[18]](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-18) Mortality rates in Quebec psychiatric asylums during the Duplessis era exceeded provincial norms for children, attributable primarily to neglect, infectious diseases, and malnutrition rather than verified intentional harm, though precise figures for reclassified orphans remain undocumented in official records. Empirical studies of survivors indicate that institutional conditions fostered environments where physical mistreatment served as a proxy for discipline, with doctrinal views from religious operators prioritizing corporal correction over evidence-based child welfare.[](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-18)[[18]](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-18) Such practices, while not unique to Quebec, amplified risks in overcrowded facilities handling non-psychiatric youth mislabeled as mentally deficient.[](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-17)[[17]](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-17)
## Extent of the Phenomenon
### Estimated Numbers and Demographics
Official inquiries and survivor advocacy groups have identified approximately 3,000 individuals as the core cohort of Duplessis Orphans—children [reclassified](https://grokipedia.com/page/Reclassified) as mentally ill for institutionalization between [1935](https://grokipedia.com/page/1935) and [1964](https://grokipedia.com/page/1964)—based on verified claims submitted for compensation programs established by the [Quebec](https://grokipedia.com/page/Quebec) government in the late [1990s](https://grokipedia.com/page/1990s) and early [2000s](https://grokipedia.com/page/2000s).[](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-9)[[9]](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-9) [](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-19)[[19]](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-19) Broader estimates, incorporating pre- and post-Duplessis era cases and varying criteria for reclassification, range from 1,500 to 20,000 affected children, though precise counts remain elusive due to incomplete archival records, destroyed documentation, and reliance on self-reported testimonies rather than comprehensive institutional logs.[](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-20)[[20]](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-20) Higher figures often encompass all children in Quebec's orphanage and asylum systems during the period, without distinguishing those explicitly reclassified to exploit federal funding disparities, underscoring the need to prioritize documented administrative certifications over anecdotal expansions.[](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-9)[[9]](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-9) Demographically, the group consisted predominantly of French-Canadian children from low-income, unstable, or single-parent households, including many illegitimate births placed in Catholic-run orphanages due to [social stigma](https://grokipedia.com/page/Social_stigma) and economic hardship rather than parental death.[](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-21)[[21]](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-21) Ages at placement typically spanned from [early childhood](https://grokipedia.com/page/Early_childhood) (around 2 years) to mid-adolescence (up to 16 years), reflecting the operational norms of Quebec's child welfare system, which funneled vulnerable minors into institutions under the guise of care.[](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-9)[[9]](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-9) The overwhelming majority were of Catholic heritage, aligned with Quebec's francophone cultural dominance, with minimal representation from immigrant or anglophone communities.[](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-1)[[1]](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-1) These profiles highlight systemic pressures on marginalized families, though exact breakdowns by gender or precise socioeconomic strata are limited by the same record-keeping deficiencies that complicate overall tallies.[](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-20)[[20]](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-20)
### Involved Institutions and Timeline
The reclassification scheme primarily targeted orphanages and psychiatric asylums operated by Catholic religious orders throughout [Quebec](https://grokipedia.com/page/Quebec), with over a dozen facilities converted or utilized for housing the affected children. Prominent institutions included the Mont-Providence orphanage in Longue-Pointe, managed by the Sisters of Providence, which was wholly redesignated as a [mental health](https://grokipedia.com/page/Mental_health) facility to access federal subsidies, and the Hôpital Saint-Jean-de-Dieu in [Montreal](https://grokipedia.com/page/Montreal), a major [psychiatric hospital](https://grokipedia.com/page/Psychiatric_hospital) under the Brothers of Charity. Additional sites encompassed at least 10 other orphanages transformed similarly, alongside established asylums such as Notre-Dame-de-la-Merci in Huberdeau, reflecting the widespread involvement of church-run establishments in the province's child welfare and [mental health](https://grokipedia.com/page/Mental_health) systems.[](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-1)[[1]](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-1)[](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-22)[[22]](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-22)[](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-9)[[9]](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-9) The phenomenon originated in the mid-1930s, with initial reclassifications piloted during Maurice Duplessis's first term as premier from 1936 to 1939, amid efforts to leverage federal funding disparities. It expanded during the 1940s wartime period, driven by economic imperatives, and peaked with intensified transfers in the 1940s and 1950s, particularly following subsidy policy adjustments in 1956.[](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-9)[[9]](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-9) Following Duplessis's death on September 7, 1959, the practice did not cease immediately but persisted under subsequent administrations, including Jean Lesage's Liberal government, with records indicating ongoing certifications and placements into the early 1960s, until the scheme's documented wind-down by 1964. This extension highlights the embedded nature of the administrative mechanisms within Quebec's provincial bureaucracy, independent of Duplessis's personal oversight.[](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-9)[[9]](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-9)
## Emergence of the Scandal
### Initial Testimonies in the 1980s and 1990s
The first major public testimonies from Duplessis Orphans emerged in 1989 during an episode of the Radio-Québec talk show _Parler pour parler_ , hosted by Jeannette Bertrand, where several survivors recounted their experiences of wrongful institutionalization and abuse in psychiatric facilities.[](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-1)[[1]](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-1) These accounts described children declared mentally ill without medical basis, subjected to harsh labor, isolation, and inadequate care, highlighting a pattern of exploitation tied to provincial funding incentives for asylums over orphanages.[](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-1)[[1]](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-1) The episode spurred increased visibility, prompting more orphans to come forward with similar narratives of systemic mistreatment during the 1940s and 1950s.[](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-23)[[23]](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-23) In the early 1990s, survivors formalized their efforts through advocacy groups, including the founding of the Comité des orphelins de Duplessis in 1992, which collected and publicized personal testimonies alleging widespread physical and emotional abuses, such as electroshock therapy without consent and forced menial work under religious orders.[](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-1)[[1]](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-1) Key figures like Rod Vienneau, a survivor who endured institutionalization as a [child](https://grokipedia.com/page/Child), played a central role in organizing these groups and documenting cases, emphasizing the role of church-run facilities in perpetuating the reclassifications for financial gain.[](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-11)[[11]](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-11) Vienneau's accounts, echoed by others, detailed daily regimens of labor disguised as therapy, with children labeled "retarded" to qualify for higher federal subsidies despite lacking diagnosable conditions.[](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-24)[[24]](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-24) While these testimonies provided compelling firsthand [evidence](https://grokipedia.com/page/Evidence) of institutional failures, their evidential value is tempered by the challenges of long-term [recall](https://grokipedia.com/page/The_Recall); events recalled after 30 to 50 years can involve [conflation](https://grokipedia.com/page/Conflation) or [embellishment](https://grokipedia.com/page/Embellishment) due to trauma-induced memory reconstruction, necessitating corroboration with contemporaneous records like admission certificates showing abrupt [mental health](https://grokipedia.com/page/Mental_health) diagnoses.[](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-25)[[25]](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-25) Nonetheless, patterns across multiple independent accounts—such as the sudden influx of "patients" coinciding with funding policy changes—lend [internal consistency](https://grokipedia.com/page/Internal_consistency), though media amplification in Quebec's French-language outlets may reflect selective emphasis on church-state complicity amid broader secular critiques.[](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-1)[[1]](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-1) Cross-verification with archival documents has substantiated core elements, like falsified certifications, in select cases, underscoring the testimonies' role in initiating scrutiny despite inherent limitations in subjective retrospection.[](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-15)[[15]](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-15)
### Media Exposure and Public Inquiries
In the early 1990s, international media coverage brought initial attention to the Duplessis Orphans' allegations of wrongful institutionalization. On April 2, 1993, _[The Washington Post](https://grokipedia.com/page/The_Washington_Post)_ reported claims that as many as 8,000 children in [Quebec](https://grokipedia.com/page/Quebec) had been falsely declared mentally retarded during the [1940s](https://grokipedia.com/page/1940s) and [1950s](https://grokipedia.com/page/1950s), primarily to qualify orphanages for enhanced federal subsidies available for psychiatric patients under Canada's National Health Grants program, which provided roughly double the funding compared to standard child welfare allocations.[](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-26)[[26]](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-26) Similar accounts appeared in _[The New York Times](https://grokipedia.com/page/The_New_York_Times)_ on May 21, 1993, detailing survivor recollections of abrupt reclassifications and transfers to asylums without medical justification.[](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-16)[[16]](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-16) Domestic outlets amplified these stories throughout the decade, with CBC broadcasts in the late [1990s](https://grokipedia.com/page/1990s) featuring orphan testimonies of harsh conditions and coerced diagnoses, contributing to heightened public discourse.[](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-27)[[27]](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-27) This journalistic scrutiny pressured [Quebec](https://grokipedia.com/page/Quebec) authorities, culminating in legislative reviews such as the National Assembly's Commission des institutions hearings starting March 12, 1997, which examined institutional records and witness statements on the orphans' placements.[](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-28)[[28]](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-28) The ensuing probes, including the Quebec Ombudsman's 1999 assessment, substantiated that financial incentives drove many reclassifications, as provincial [records](https://grokipedia.com/page/The_Records) showed orphanages receiving $0.75 per day per [child](https://grokipedia.com/page/Child) versus $1.50 for mental patients, enabling institutions to offset operational deficits amid [postwar](https://grokipedia.com/page/Post-war) [budget](https://grokipedia.com/page/Budget) constraints.[](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-29)[[29]](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-29) However, findings also qualified the scope of extreme abuses, noting evidentiary gaps in claims of systemic [torture](https://grokipedia.com/page/Torture) or experimentation across all 20,000 estimated cases, with documentation indicating some children exhibited behavioral issues warranting care, though diagnostic standards were rudimentary and funding motives pervasive.[](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-30)[[30]](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-30) These inquiries shaped perceptions by prioritizing verifiable archival evidence over anecdotal extremes, influencing subsequent policy without endorsing universal victimhood narratives.
## Governmental and Legal Responses
### Provincial Investigations and Admissions
In March 1999, Quebec Premier [Lucien Bouchard](https://grokipedia.com/page/Lucien_Bouchard) issued a public apology to the Duplessis Orphans, acknowledging that provincial officials had improperly reclassified children as mentally ill to secure enhanced federal funding for institutions, resulting in their wrongful confinement in psychiatric facilities.[](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-21)[[21]](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-21)[](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-29)[[29]](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-29) The statement emphasized systemic errors driven by financial incentives under federal-provincial reimbursement schemes, which provided higher [per capita](https://grokipedia.com/page/Per_capita) subsidies for mental patients than for orphans, without imputing direct orchestration to [Maurice Duplessis](https://grokipedia.com/page/Maurice_Duplessis) himself.[](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-21)[[21]](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-21) Quebec's Protecteur du citoyen ([Ombudsman](https://grokipedia.com/page/Ombudsman)) conducted an official review in early 1999, corroborating survivor testimonies through examination of historical records and confirming that bureaucratic decisions led to the misdiagnosis and institutionalization of an estimated 5,000 to 6,000 children between 1935 and 1964, spanning the Duplessis administration and subsequent governments.[](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-29)[[29]](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-29) The Ombudsman's report highlighted complicity in administrative practices that prioritized fiscal gains over child welfare assessments, noting that reclassifications continued post-1959 despite the shift to the Liberal government under [Jean Lesage](https://grokipedia.com/page/Jean_Lesage).[](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-29)[[29]](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-29) These admissions prompted policy reevaluations, accelerating the secularization of [child protection](https://grokipedia.com/page/Child_protection) services that had originated in the 1960s [Quiet Revolution](https://grokipedia.com/page/Quiet_Revolution) but faced renewed scrutiny in the scandal's aftermath. By the early 2000s, the province established dedicated administrative frameworks for orphan aid, detaching welfare delivery from religious institutions and emphasizing state oversight to prevent funding-motivated placements.[](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-31)[[31]](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-31)
### Compensation Agreements and Ongoing Aid
In July 2001, following negotiations, the Quebec government finalized a compensation agreement with representatives of the Duplessis Orphans, offering each eligible survivor a base payment of $10,000 Canadian dollars plus an additional $1,000 for every year spent in psychiatric institutions.[](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-1)[[1]](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-1) [](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-11)[[11]](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-11) This settlement came after an initial rejection of a lower provincial offer in June 2001, with the majority of survivors ultimately accepting the revised terms, as evidenced by the agreement's endorsement by key advocacy groups.[](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-19)[[19]](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-19) [](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-32)[[32]](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-32) A minority rejected it, demanding higher sums to better reflect the extent of institutionalization and mistreatment endured.[](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-33)[[33]](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-33) The 2001 package included a dedicated $25 million fund for distribution, supplemented by individual claims processing, though total disbursements varied based on verified institutional stays.[](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-34)[[34]](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-34) In [December](https://grokipedia.com/page/December) 2006, Quebec allocated an additional $26 million to cover further claims from survivors who had been excluded or required extended support under the agreement's framework.[](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-35)[[35]](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-35) Acceptance rates, inferred from the settlement's implementation and lack of widespread further litigation at the time, suggest broad uptake among the estimated 3,000 to 5,000 eligible claimants, indicating the amounts met threshold needs for restitution without necessitating prolonged disputes for most.[](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-19)[[19]](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-19) As of April 2024, [Quebec](https://grokipedia.com/page/Quebec) maintains the National Reconciliation Program for Duplessis Orphans, providing ongoing financial assistance to survivors who were orphans or abandoned children admitted to designated institutions between January 1, 1935, and December 31, 1964.[](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-31)[[31]](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-31) Eligibility requires documentary proof of residency in specified facilities, such as psychiatric asylums, with payments structured to offset late-life hardships tied to those experiences, though exact per-person amounts remain case-specific and capped to align with provincial budgetary limits.[](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-31)[[31]](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-31) [](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-36)[[36]](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-36) While some advocates have critiqued these programs as undercompensating relative to documented abuses—citing fixed sums against decades of institutional harm—their continuation reflects fiscal prudence, distributing public resources without unbounded liability to taxpayers.[](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-34)[[34]](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-34) Empirical outcomes, including sustained program applications into the [2020s](https://grokipedia.com/page/2020s), underscore partial adequacy in addressing survivor claims without fiscal overextension.[](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-31)[[31]](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-31)
## Institutional Accountability
### Catholic Church's Role and Refusals
Religious orders affiliated with the [Catholic Church](https://grokipedia.com/page/Catholic_Church), such as the Sisters of Charity and the [Grey Nuns](https://grokipedia.com/page/Grey_Nuns), operated many of the orphanages and psychiatric institutions in [Quebec](https://grokipedia.com/page/Quebec) where Duplessis Orphans were housed during the 1940s and 1950s.[](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-16)[[16]](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-16)[](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-37)[[37]](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-37) These orders received provincial funding to care for the children, which increased substantially after reclassifications from orphans to mentally ill patients enabled access to higher federal subsidies under the National Health Grants program starting in 1945.[](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-1)[[1]](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-1) While providing basic shelter and religious education in line with mid-20th-century institutional norms, the regimes enforced strict discipline, including [corporal punishment](https://grokipedia.com/page/Corporal_punishment) and isolation, which survivors later described as abusive, though contemporaneous Church records emphasized charitable intent amid resource constraints.[](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-21)[[21]](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-21)[](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-22)[[22]](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-22) In September 1999, Bishop Pierre Morisette of the [Trois-Rivières](https://grokipedia.com/page/Trois-Rivi%C3%A8res) diocese, speaking on behalf of Quebec's Catholic bishops after reviewing survivor testimonies and archival materials, rejected calls for a [collective](https://grokipedia.com/page/Collective) apology or compensation.[](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-38)[[38]](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-38) He argued that the Church lacked centralized [authority](https://grokipedia.com/page/Authority) over individual religious communities' actions, attributing any mistreatment to isolated failures rather than institutional [policy](https://grokipedia.com/page/Policy), and contextualized the era's practices as reflective of prevailing societal standards rather than deliberate wrongdoing.[](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-38)[[38]](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-38) This stance echoed internal [ecclesiastical](https://grokipedia.com/page/Ecclesiastical) assessments that acknowledged potential oversight lapses in monitoring personnel but denied evidence of a coordinated scheme to exploit children for financial gain, positioning the orphans as "victims of life" amid broader systemic pressures.[](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-22)[[22]](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-22) The Church maintained this position into the early 2000s, with Quebec's Assembly of Bishops reiterating in November 2000 that no blanket apology was warranted without proven direct culpability by the [hierarchy](https://grokipedia.com/page/Hierarchy), emphasizing case-by-case evaluations over generalized remorse.[](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-39)[[39]](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-39) Such refusals highlighted tensions between the decentralized structure of Catholic orders—each autonomous in daily operations—and demands for unified [accountability](https://grokipedia.com/page/Accountability), while internal reviews focused on verifying claims against historical [documentation](https://grokipedia.com/page/Documentation) rather than presuming institutional guilt.[](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-40)[[40]](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-40)
### Failed Lawsuits Against Religious Orders
In the early 1990s, survivors of the Duplessis Orphans scandal launched [class action](https://grokipedia.com/page/Class_action) lawsuits targeting religious orders that managed orphanages and psychiatric facilities, alleging mistreatment and wrongful institutionalization. A prominent 1992 [class action](https://grokipedia.com/page/Class_action) suit against these orders ultimately failed due to procedural and evidentiary shortcomings, including difficulties in establishing individualized proof of harm decades after the events.[](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-20)[[20]](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-20) Courts emphasized the challenges of retrospective liability, where faded records and deceased witnesses undermined claims of direct institutional responsibility beyond government directives.[](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-1)[[1]](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-1) Subsequent litigation efforts encountered similar dismissals. For instance, in the 2010s case of _Boudreau et al. v. Attorney General of[Quebec](https://grokipedia.com/page/Quebec) et al._, plaintiffs sought authorization for a [class action](https://grokipedia.com/page/Class_action) against the [Quebec](https://grokipedia.com/page/Quebec) government and specific Catholic entities, such as Les Sœurs de la Providence and Les Petites Franciscaines de Marie, for complicity in abuses. The [Superior Court](https://grokipedia.com/page/Superior_court) dismissed the application, citing statutes of limitations that barred claims originating from mid-20th-century events, as well as insufficient evidence linking orders to deliberate falsification of diagnoses independent of state policy.[](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-41)[[41]](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-41) The [Quebec](https://grokipedia.com/page/Quebec) Court of Appeal upheld this rejection in 2022, reinforcing that moral culpability alone could not override legal thresholds for proof, such as contemporaneous documentation of intent or causation.[](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-42)[[42]](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-42) These failures highlighted broader evidentiary hurdles in such cases, including the [erosion](https://grokipedia.com/page/Erosion) of physical and [testimonial](https://grokipedia.com/page/Testimonial) [evidence](https://grokipedia.com/page/Evidence) over time, which courts deemed insufficient to meet civil liability standards without direct attribution of fault to the orders rather than systemic provincial practices. While isolated partial settlements emerged in related [abuse](https://grokipedia.com/page/Abuse) claims against certain orders—often confidential and limited in scope—most suits faltered on the absence of verifiable records proving [negligence](https://grokipedia.com/page/Negligence) or [conspiracy](https://grokipedia.com/page/Conspiracy) beyond subsidized operations.[](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-43)[[43]](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-43) Retroactive liability claims against non-sovereign religious entities thus underscored the primacy of [legal realism](https://grokipedia.com/page/Legal_realism), prioritizing demonstrable causation over aggregate survivor narratives in judicial assessments.[](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-41)[[41]](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-41)
## Enduring Effects
### Survivor Health Outcomes and Social Challenges
Adult survivors of the Duplessis Orphans scandal have exhibited elevated rates of psychological difficulties, including low [self-esteem](https://grokipedia.com/page/Self-esteem), persistent feelings of abandonment, and challenges in forming secure affective bonds, consistent with attachment theory's emphasis on the role of early [caregiver](https://grokipedia.com/page/Caregiver) relationships in emotional development.[](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-18)[[18]](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-18) A qualitative analysis of testimonies from 40 individuals institutionalized between 1935 and 1964 revealed these issues as common long-term sequelae of prolonged institutional care lacking consistent nurturing attachments. [Mental health](https://grokipedia.com/page/Mental_health) problems, such as depression and anxiety, were frequently reported, though quantitative prevalence data specific to this cohort remains limited, with outcomes potentially confounded by pre-institutional factors like parental abandonment or socioeconomic disadvantage.[](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-44)[[44]](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-44) Educationally, many survivors attained low levels of formal schooling, often due to institutional policies prioritizing labor over learning, resulting in [functional illiteracy](https://grokipedia.com/page/Functional_illiteracy) or basic skills insufficient for advanced [employment](https://grokipedia.com/page/Employment).[](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-45)[[45]](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-45) Socially, challenges included difficulties maintaining stable jobs, leading to chronic [poverty](https://grokipedia.com/page/Poverty), and elevated encounters with legal systems, including petty criminality, though these patterns align with broader outcomes for institutionally raised children rather than being uniquely tied to reported abuses.[](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-18)[[18]](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-18) Homelessness appeared sporadically in survivor accounts but lacked cohort-wide statistical elevation attributable solely to the [scandal](https://grokipedia.com/page/Scandal), with [poverty](https://grokipedia.com/page/Poverty) more directly linked to skill deficits from disrupted development.[](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-46)[[46]](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-46) Despite these patterns, some survivors demonstrated adaptive resilience, forming families or achieving modest stability in adulthood, highlighting individual variability and countering narratives of universal victimhood.[](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-47)[[47]](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-47) A study of seven late-adult survivors noted personal strengths like perseverance emerging from traumatic experiences, suggesting that while institutionalization causally impaired attachment and opportunity, inherent traits and post-release supports mitigated total dysfunction for subsets.[](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-48)[[48]](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-48) These mixed outcomes underscore the interplay of causal factors, including baseline vulnerabilities from non-orphaned origins, in shaping life trajectories.[](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-49)[[49]](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-49)
### Disposal of Remains and Archival Issues
The disposal of deceased Duplessis Orphans frequently occurred in undocumented or unmarked graves adjacent to psychiatric institutions and orphanages, often under conditions indicative of institutional expediency rather than formal cemetery protocols. At the Saint-Jean-de-Dieu Hospital (formerly an asylum) in east-end Montreal, an estimated 2,000 bodies were interred in a nearby cemetery from the 1940s through the early 1960s, including at least 42 children, with graves described as shallow and located near a hog farm, earning the site the moniker "pigsty cemetery."[](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-50)[[50]](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-50) Survivor testimonies, such as those from former patients who assisted in body transport, link these burials to orphanage transfers and high mortality from neglect or experimental procedures, though forensic confirmation remains pending due to lack of systematic exhumations.[](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-50)[[50]](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-50) Recent discoveries of potential burial sites have spurred legal demands for exhumations and identifications, grounded in geophysical surveys and historical mapping rather than speculation. In February 2024, concerns over children's remains at a former Sisters of Providence cemetery—repurposed for an SAQ warehouse expansion in [Montreal](https://grokipedia.com/page/Montreal)—prompted Duplessis Orphans committees and allied groups to secure a temporary halt to excavation via court intervention, citing the site's history as an informal dumping ground for asylum dead during the [Duplessis era](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_era).[](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-51)[[51]](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-51)[](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-52)[[52]](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-52) Subsequent assessments in May 2024 found no human remains, illustrating the challenges in confirming undocumented disposals without invasive digs.[](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-53)[[53]](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-53) Efforts to pursue broader exhumations have faced institutional resistance and evidentiary hurdles, resulting in piecemeal outcomes. A 2021 initiative by orphans sought class-action probes and digs at multiple former psychiatric sites, paralleling survivor accounts of mass interments, but progressed only to advocacy rather than mandated forensic work.[](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-54)[[54]](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-54) Similarly, a May 2024 lawsuit against the SAQ emphasized the need for [ground-penetrating radar](https://grokipedia.com/page/Ground-penetrating_radar) and DNA analysis to identify remains potentially tied to orphanage mortality spikes, yet yielded no confirmed recoveries by late 2024.[](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-55)[[55]](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-55) Archival deficiencies compound these issues, with death and burial records systematically incomplete or absent across Quebec's church-run and state-affiliated institutions. At facilities like Saint-Jean-de-Dieu, historical analysis reveals "archival dissolution"—the routine loss or purging of patient files, transfer logs, and mortality data—leaving gaps that obscure individual fates and prevent cross-referencing with survivor claims.[](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-56)[[56]](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-56) Quebec's provincial archives hold fragmented institutional ledgers from the period, but access is impeded by privacy protocols and the absence of centralized indexing, forcing reliance on anecdotal reconstructions over comprehensive documentation.[](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-1)[[1]](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-1) These voids, attributed to wartime resource strains and post-scandal cleanups, have stymied legal identifications despite orphan-led requests for record releases since the [1990s](https://grokipedia.com/page/1990s) inquiries.[](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-22)[[22]](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-22)
## Debates and Critical Analysis
### Questions of Exaggeration and Evidentiary Gaps
While the reclassification of certain orphanages as psychiatric institutions to secure higher federal funding under the National Health Grants program in the 1950s is documented, the broader allegations of systemic abuses—such as widespread sterilizations, lobotomies, and experimental procedures—rely heavily on survivor testimonies rather than contemporaneous [records](https://grokipedia.com/page/The_Records). [Official](https://grokipedia.com/page/Official) reviews, including a 1997 report by Quebec's [ombudsman](https://grokipedia.com/page/Ombudsman), identified approximately 3,000 children affected by the reclassification scheme but found insufficient [documentary evidence](https://grokipedia.com/page/Documentary_evidence) to substantiate many specific claims of medical mistreatment, noting that institutional logs often contradicted or lacked detail on alleged procedures.[](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-57)[[57]](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-57) Claims of mass sterilizations, for instance, appear in anecdotal accounts from survivors but lack verified medical or administrative corroboration beyond isolated cases, with no comprehensive provincial [records](https://grokipedia.com/page/The_Records) confirming large-scale implementation.[](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-13)[[13]](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-13) Discrepancies arise from the tension between aging personal recollections—often from individuals now in their 70s or older, recounting events from 50 to 80 years prior—and surviving institutional documentation, which primarily evidences administrative motives for reclassification rather than deliberate fabrication of mental illness diagnoses for all children involved. Not all children labeled as mentally ill were healthy orphans falsely certified; some exhibited genuine developmental or behavioral issues warranting institutional care, as indicated by pre-existing [orphanage](https://grokipedia.com/page/Orphanage) assessments and partial psychiatric evaluations preserved in select archives. Quebec government admissions in the [1990s](https://grokipedia.com/page/1990s) acknowledged funding incentives but cautioned against generalizing all institutionalizations as fraudulent, emphasizing that diagnostic practices of the era were rudimentary and influenced by limited psychiatric standards.[](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-9)[[9]](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-9)[](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-58)[[58]](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-58) Debates over scale further highlight evidentiary limitations, with activist groups estimating up to [20,000](https://grokipedia.com/page/20,000) victims while official tallies hover around 3,000 to 5,000 directly tied to reclassified facilities, potentially inflated by inclusive definitions encompassing all orphanage residents rather than those transferred under the scheme. Media portrayals in the [1990s](https://grokipedia.com/page/1990s) amplified sympathetic narratives, drawing parallels to broader [institutional abuse](https://grokipedia.com/page/Institutional_abuse) scandals, but inquiries like the ombudsman's noted risks of conflating economic opportunism with intentional cruelty absent forensic or archival proof. This reliance on unverified testimonies, amid destroyed or incomplete records from religious orders, underscores gaps in causal attribution, where administrative policy shifts do not necessarily equate to universal medical fraud or abuse.[](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-57)[[57]](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-57)[](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-1)[[1]](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-1)
### Contextual Comparisons to Global Orphan Systems
The institutionalization of vulnerable children in mid-20th-century [Quebec](https://grokipedia.com/page/Quebec) occurred amid widespread global practices where state funding mechanisms incentivized the expansion of asylums and orphanages, often managed by religious orders, leading to overcrowding, neglect, and misclassification for financial gain. In the United States, [Willowbrook State School](https://grokipedia.com/page/Willowbrook_State_School) in New York, established in the 1930s for children with intellectual disabilities, ballooned to over 6,000 residents by [1965](https://grokipedia.com/page/1965)—far exceeding its designed capacity of 4,000—resulting in severe abuses including medical experiments, hepatitis outbreaks, and inadequate care, all sustained by state funding that prioritized institutional placement over alternatives.[](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-59)[[59]](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-59) Similarly, in Ireland, Catholic Church-run Magdalene Laundries and Mother and Baby Homes from the 1920s to the 1960s received government subsidies tied to labor output and child placements, mirroring Quebec's federal funding incentives for mental health institutions, with documented parallels in the confinement of orphans from unwed mothers and reports of physical and emotional abuses in both systems.[](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-60)[[60]](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-60)[](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-61)[[61]](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-61) Quebec's conservative welfare framework during this era, characterized by limited provincial spending and reliance on Church-operated facilities, proved cost-effective by leveraging federal reimbursements for psychiatric care, which covered up to 80% of costs for designated institutions, in contrast to the higher direct fiscal burdens of emerging secular welfare states in post-war Europe and the U.S. that expanded public asylums without equivalent private partnerships.[](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-62)[[62]](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-62) This approach aligned with global norms where underdeveloped social safety nets deferred to religious institutions for child welfare, avoiding the inflationary expenditures seen in modern expansive models, such as Canada's post-1960s universal programs, which have incurred ongoing scandals like foster care breakdowns despite higher per-capita investments.[](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-63)[[63]](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-63) Empirically, Quebec's per-capita institutionalization rates for psychiatric and [orphan](https://grokipedia.com/page/Orphan) care in the 1940s-1960s remained lower than in comparable U.S. states, where over 550,000 individuals occupied [public](https://grokipedia.com/page/Public) mental hospitals by [1955](https://grokipedia.com/page/1955) amid similar funding-driven expansions, reflecting Quebec's emphasis on familial and [ecclesiastical](https://grokipedia.com/page/Ecclesiastical) support structures that reduced outright reliance on state facilities until welfare maturation in the [1960s](https://grokipedia.com/page/1960s).[](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-64)[[64]](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-64) These international precedents underscore how fiscal incentives universally distorted care priorities, prioritizing institutional bed-filling over individualized needs, without [Quebec](https://grokipedia.com/page/Quebec) deviating as an outlier in an age of systemic under-resourcing for child welfare.[](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-65)[[65]](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-65)
### Political Narratives and Ideological Critiques
The Duplessis Orphans scandal has been prominently invoked in Quebec political discourse to epitomize the perceived authoritarianism and clericalism of Maurice Duplessis's Union Nationale governments, often subsumed under the historiographical label of the _Grande Noirceur_ (Great Darkness), denoting the period from 1944 to 1959 as one of stagnation and abuse.[](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-66)[[66]](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-66) This framing, advanced by opponents including post-Quiet Revolution intellectuals and left-leaning historians, attributes the reclassification of orphans as mentally ill primarily to Duplessis-era collusion between the state and [Catholic Church](https://grokipedia.com/page/Catholic_Church) to secure federal funding, portraying it as emblematic of regressive governance.[](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-1)[[1]](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-1) However, archival and institutional records demonstrate that such practices commenced in 1935 under the preceding Liberal administration of Louis-Alexandre Taschereau and persisted until at least 1964, well into the reformist Liberal government of [Jean Lesage](https://grokipedia.com/page/Jean_Lesage), indicating systemic continuity rather than partisan singularity.[](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-9)[[9]](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-9) Progressive critiques, prevalent in academic and media accounts, tend to elide this bipartisan dimension while emphasizing ethical lapses, yet the mechanism originated from pragmatic fiscal calculations: orphanages received lower per-child reimbursements from [Ottawa](https://grokipedia.com/page/Ottawa) than psychiatric facilities, prompting reclassifications to access enhanced federal per diems under programs like the National Health Grants established in 1948.[](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-13)[[13]](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-13) This approach, while expedient for cash-strapped provincial budgets amid post-Depression recovery, reflected broader mid-20th-century welfare constraints where [Quebec](https://grokipedia.com/page/Quebec) relied on church-managed institutions for scalability, a reality overlooked in narratives that retroactively impose modern standards of state oversight. In contrast, conservative interpretations underscore the Catholic Church's functional necessity in delivering care during an epoch devoid of comprehensive public alternatives, arguing that religious orders absorbed thousands of dependents—estimated at over 20,000 children in [Quebec](https://grokipedia.com/page/Quebec) institutions by the [1950s](https://grokipedia.com/page/1950s)—averting destitution in the absence of viable secular [infrastructure](https://grokipedia.com/page/Infrastructure).[](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-1)[[1]](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-1) Revivals of the [scandal](https://grokipedia.com/page/Scandal) in contemporary media, such as the June 2024 APTN Investigates documentary _Orphans of Church and State_ , which posits connections to Indigenous children and ranks the episode second only to residential schools in scale of institutional harm, may reflect amplified anti-clerical sentiments amid Quebec's accelerating [secularization](https://grokipedia.com/page/Secularization) and national [reconciliation](https://grokipedia.com/page/Reconciliation) efforts.[](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-11)[[11]](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-11) Such coverage, emanating from outlets attuned to Indigenous advocacy, risks conflating distinct historical contexts to bolster broader indictments of religious [authority](https://grokipedia.com/page/Authority), potentially underplaying evidentiary limits in linking specific cases to native origins while aligning with systemic biases in progressive institutions that privilege institutional critiques over chronological nuance.[](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-11)[[11]](https://grokipedia.com/page/Duplessis_Orphans#ref-11)

The Duplessis Orphans were a group of Canadian children in Quebec who, during the 1940s and 1950s, were wrongly certified as mentally ill by the provincial government and confined to psychiatric institutions. Named after Maurice Duplessis, Quebec’s Premier for much of that period, these children were often deliberately misclassified to secure additional federal subsidies, as psychiatric patients brought higher funding than orphans. This scandal has become one of the darkest and largest cases of child abuse in Canadian history, second only to the Indigenous residential school system.
At the time, Quebec’s social services were limited and largely controlled by the Roman Catholic Church, which ran orphanages and maternity homes. Many children born to unwed mothers — labeled as “bastards” — were pressured or forced into institutional care, often in Catholic-run facilities. The Lunatic Asylum Act of 1909 allowed mental institutions to admit patients for vague reasons including maintaining social order, a loophole exploited under Duplessis. The provincial government reclassified orphanages as psychiatric hospitals to receive higher federal funding, leading to healthy children being wrongly institutionalized with fabricated psychiatric diagnoses.

# Duplessis orphans
In Quebec between the 1930s and 1960s, thousands of children in state care were considered "mentally deficient" and placed in institutions led by religious communities for the most part. This practice, encouraged by the administration of Maurice [Duplessis](https://www.biographi.ca/en/bio/duplessis_maurice_le_noblet_18E.html) mainly because it allowed for obtaining federal subsidies, had adverse consequences for children, who were often victims of abuse and stigmatized when they became adults. The Rapport de la commission d’étude des hôpitaux psychiatriques, published in 1962, shed light on the problem and helped put an end to it. The "Duplessis Orphans" started legal proceedings in the 1990s and accepted the provincial government’s offer of compensation in 2001.

## Duplessis Orphans
The Duplessis Orphans (French: les Orphelins de Duplessis) were 20,000 children who were wrongly certified as mentally ill by the provincial government of Quebec and confined to psychiatric institutions in the 1940s and 1950s. The children were deliberately miscertified in order to misappropriate additional subsidies from the federal government. They are named for Maurice Duplessis, who served as Premier of Quebec for five terms between 1936 and 1959. The controversies associated with Duplessis, and particularly the corruption and abuse concerning the Duplessis orphans, have led to the popular historic conception of his term as Premier as La Grande Noirceur ("The Great Darkness") by its critics.  The Duplessis Orphans have accused both the government of Quebec and the Roman Catholic Church of wrongdoing. The Catholic Church has denied involvement in the allegations, and disputes the claims of those seeking financial compensation for harm done.  Background  During the 1940s and 1950s, limited social services were available to residents of Quebec. Prior to the "Quiet Revolution" of the 1960s, most of the social services available were provided, not by the state, but by the Roman Catholic Church. Among their charges were people considered to be socially vulnerable: those living in poverty, unwed mothers, orphans, alcoholics or other individuals deemed unable to work.  Consistent with the values of the Church and the period, many children were admitted to orphanages even though they were not in fact orphans. Many were children born to unmarried mothers). Many of these orphanages were operated by religious institutions. These religious organizations encouraged unwed mothers to leave their children there, so that they might be cared for by the Church. In reality, thee often provided poor care, and children frequently suffered abuse in these institutions.  The Loi sur les Asiles d'ali�n�s (Lunatic Asylum Act) of 1909 governed mental institution admissions until 1950. The law stated the mentally ill could be committed for three reasons: to care for them, to help them, or as a measure to maintain social order in public and private life. However, the act did not define what a disruption of social order was, leaving the decision to admit patients up to psychiatrists.  The Union Nationale provincial government of Premier Maurice Duplessis received subsidies from the federal government for building hospitals, but received substantially fewer subsidies to support orphanages. Government contributions were only $1.25 a day for orphans, but $2.75 a day for psychiatric patients. This disparity in funding provided a strong financial incentive for reclassification. Under Duplessis, the provincial government was responsible for a significant number of healthy older children being deliberately misdiagnosed as mentally incompetent and sent to psychiatric hospitals, based on superficial diagnoses made for fiscal reasons. Duplessis also signed an order-in-council which changed the classification of orphanages into hospitals in order to provide them with federal subsidies.  A commission in the early 1960s investigating mental institutions after Duplessis' death revealed one-third of the 22,000 patients classified as "mentally incompetent" were classified as such for the province's financial benefit, and not due to any real psychiatric deficit. Following the publication of the B�dard report in 1962, the province ceased retaining the institutional notion of "asylum". In light of these institutional changes, when many of the orphans were permitted to leave the facilities when they reached adulthood.  Impacts on orphans  Years later, long after these institutions were closed, survivors of the asylums began to speak out about child abuse which they endured at the hands of some staff and medical personnel. Many who have spoken publicly about their experiences claim that they had been abused physically and sexually, and were subjected to lobotomies, electroshock and straitjackets.  In a psychiatric study completed by one of the involved hospitals, middle-aged Duplessis Orphans reported more physical and mental impairments than the control group. In addition, the orphans were less likely to be married or to have a healthy social life. 80% reported they had suffered a traumatic experience between the ages of 7 to 18. Over 50% said they had undergone physical, mental, or sexual abuse. About 78% reported difficulty functioning socially or emotionally in their adult life.  Legal recourse in the 1990s  By the 1990s, about 3,000 survivors and a large group of supporters formed the Duplessis Orphans Committee, seeking damages from the Quebec provincial government. In March 1999, the provincial government made an offer of approximately CAD$15,000 as full compensation to each of the victims. The offer was rejected and the provincial government was harshly criticized, with Quebec's Ombudsman at the time, Daniel Jacoby, saying that the government's handling of the affair trivialized the abuse alleged by the victims. In 2001, the claimants received an increased offer from the provincial government for a flat payment of $10,000 per person, plus an additional $1,000 for each year of wrongful confinement to a mental institution. The offer amounted to approximately $25,000 per orphan, but did not include any compensation for alleged victims of sexual or other abuse.  After the offer was accepted by representatives of the Duplessis Orphans Committee, the result was bitterly contested by other members upon learning that under the terms of the settlement, the Committee's lawyer, president, and former public relations official would receive six- to seven-figure payments, in comparison with the paltry amount given to the actual victims. The Committee subsequently voted to replace both the president and the public relations official. Critics of the judgment pointed out that three of the bureaucrats running the government's compensation program were being paid over $1,000 per day for work, whereas the orphans themselves received the same amount of money for an entire year of their confinement.  Seven religious communities were involved in operating some of the facilities: the Sisters of Providence, the Sisters of Mercy, the Grey Nuns of Montreal, the Sisters of Charity of Quebec, the Little Franciscans of Mary, the Brothers of Notre-Dame-de-la-Misericorde, and the Brothers of Charity. When the settlement with the provincial government was reached, the orphans agreed to drop any further legal action against the Catholic Church and its affiliated bodies. This offended some survivors; in 2006, one of the Orphans, Martin L�cuyer, stated, "It's important for me, that the Church, the priests, that they recognize they were responsible for the sexual abuse, and the aggression. It's not for the government to set that peace ... It's an insult, and it's the biggest proof that the government is an accomplice of the Church."  Aftermath  In 1999, researchers L�o-Paul Lauzon and Martin Poirier issued a report arguing that both the Quebec provincial government and the Catholic Church made substantial profits by falsely certifying thousands of Quebec orphans as mentally ill during Duplessis' premiership. The authors made a conservative estimate that religious groups received $70 million in subsidies (measured in 1999 dollars) by claiming the children as "mentally deficient", while the government saved $37 million simply by having one of its orphanages redesignated from an educational institution to a psychiatric hospital. A representative of a religious order involved with the orphanages accused the authors of making "false assertions". In 2010, it was estimated that approximately 300-400 of the original Duplessis orphans were still alive.  Fate of human remains In 2004, some Duplessis orphans asked the Quebec government to unearth an abandoned cemetery in the east end of Montreal, which they believed held the remains of orphans who may have been the subject of human experimentation. According to testimony by individuals who were at the Cit� de St-Jean-de-Dieu insane asylum, the orphans in the asylum's care were routinely used as non-consensual experimental subjects, and many died as a consequence. The group wanted the government to exhume the bodies so that autopsies may be performed. In November 2010, the Duplessis orphans made their case before the United Nations Human Rights Council. ## Duplessis orphans call for exhumations: Aim to show children were experimented upon
  
National Post  
Saturday, June 19, 2004  
  
Byline: William Marsden A group of orphans are fighting for the bodies of children to be exhumed from an abandoned Montreal cemetery in a bid to discover whether they were the subject of medical experiments. The group has hired a lawyer to ask a court for permission to dig up the bodies of the children, who were also orphans, and perform forensic examinations. Rod Vienneau, a spokesman for the orphans, said, "We want to show that these people were the victims of medical experiments such as lobotomies." There were about 20,000 orphans during the 1940s, '50s and early '60s -- generally called the Duplessis era, after Quebec premier Maurice Duplessis -- who were handed over to various religious institutions. Thousands were labelled mentally deficient and sent to asylums and other church-run institutions because the nuns running the establishments received larger subsidies for the mentally ill. The orphans' group claims children buried in an abandoned cemetery in east-end Montreal may have been victims of medical experiments performed at the old Cite de St. Jean de Dieu insane asylum, now Louis-Hyppolite Lafontaine Hospital. The group's lawyer, Daniel Lighter, said, "If there is evidence of this kind of activity, then it would certainly be important evidence in a suit against the government, church and doctors." Mr. Lighter said it will take several months to analyze whether there is enough proof to persuade a judge to order the exhumations. Many orphans were sexually abused and forced to work in slave-labour conditions. Some claim as well that many were subjected to medical experiments inside the asylums. Albert Sylvio, 62, was a Duplessis orphan who lived at St. Jean de Dieu in the 1950s. He said that during that time, he transported about 60 bodies of fellow orphans from the operating rooms to the basement morgue. "I undressed them and washed them and prepared them for burial," he said. "We put them in cardboard boxes. Some of them were children." He said the bodies were then taken to the cemetery and buried in unmarked graves. "There was never any ceremony. Some of these people died on the operating table. Some had been sick and some had committed suicide." The medical experiments are alleged to have been performed on living orphans. Paul St. Aubain, another Duplessis orphan, said he was lobotomized at age 18 at a mental hospital in Joliette called St. Michel Archange. "I wasn't ill," he said. "They did it without my consent, without my permission. They were experimenting with me. I was a prisoner." Mr. St. Aubain, 52, said he spent 25 years in various mental institutions run by nuns in Quebec and during that time saw "other orphans who were lobotomized." He said doctors also gave him electroshock therapy and numerous psychiatric drugs. Mr. St. Aubain now lives on welfare in Joliette. He cannot work. A Quebec law passed in 1942 allowed the nuns to sell unclaimed bodies to medical schools for $10. Many dead orphans, whose names and identities had been changed or erased, were dissected. What was left of their bodies was buried in cemeteries such as St. Jean de Dieu. One government registry indicates there were about 2,000 bodies buried at the St. Jean de Dieu cemetery, which locals called the "pigsty" because it was next to a hog farm owned by the nuns. At least 42 were children. How many orphans ended up as cadavers for anatomy students is not known because hospital registries have disappeared from government archives or were never properly filled out. The practice of selling unclaimed cadavers to medical schools continued into the 1960s. In 2002, a group of 1,100 orphans settled with the government for about $25-million for wrongfully placing them in mental institutions. Other Duplessis orphans, who had been placed in religious institutions and claim they were sexually abused, are still fighting for compensation. ----

### Background and Rise to Power
Maurice Duplessis's father, Nérée Le Noblet Duplessis, was a fervently [Catholic](https://thecanadianencyclopedia.ca/en/article/catholicism) and Conservative MLA for [Trois-Rivières](https://www.thecanadianencyclopedia.ca/en/article/trois-rivieres) 1886–1900. He had been an unsuccessful federal [Conservative Party](https://www.thecanadianencyclopedia.ca/en/article/conservative-party) candidate before being named a superior court judge by [Sir Robert Borden](https://www.thecanadianencyclopedia.ca/en/article/sir-robert-borden/) in 1915. Duplessis' mother was of part [Scottish](https://www.thecanadianencyclopedia.ca/en/article/scots) and [Irish](https://www.thecanadianencyclopedia.ca/en/article/irish) descent.
After studying at Collège Notre-Dame in Montreal (where he became something of a protégé of [Brother André](https://www.thecanadianencyclopedia.ca/en/article/saint-andre/)) and the Séminaire de Trois-Rivières, he graduated from [Université Laval](https://www.thecanadianencyclopedia.ca/en/article/universite-laval)'s Montreal law faculty in 1913, spending the [First World War](https://www.thecanadianencyclopedia.ca/en/article/first-world-war-wwi) in the local militia. He developed a successful popular law practice in Trois-Rivières, was narrowly defeated there in the 1923 provincial election, but won the first of nine consecutive elections there in 1927.
He helped mayor [Camillien Houde](https://www.thecanadianencyclopedia.ca/en/article/camillien-houde/) of Montreal ease out Arthur Sauvé as leader of the provincial Conservatives in 1929. Duplessis subsequently deposed Houde after the electoral debacle of 1931, in which the Conservative Party, led by Houde, was routed. Confirmed as leader of the Quebec Conservative Party in 1933, Duplessis wooed disgruntled reform Liberals and Nationalists who had become disillusioned with the arch-conservative Liberal government of [Louis-Alexandre Taschereau](https://www.thecanadianencyclopedia.ca/en/article/louis-alexandre-taschereau/). Duplessis formed a movement styled [Action libérale nationale](https://www.thecanadianencyclopedia.ca/en/article/action-liberale-nationale/), and two weeks before the 1935 election he united with them to form the [Union Nationale](https://www.thecanadianencyclopedia.ca/en/article/union-nationale/), with the rather unworldly [Paul Gouin](https://www.thecanadianencyclopedia.ca/en/article/paul-gouin/) as ostensible coleader.
Taschereau was returned in 1935, but Duplessis forced him out in June 1936 with a sensational performance before the Public Accounts Committee exposing corruption and profligacy in the regime, accompanied by a filibuster in the Assembly. He also dispensed with Gouin and outmaneuvered his other Action libérale nationale allies, defeated the hapless [Joseph-Adélard Godbout](https://www.thecanadianencyclopedia.ca/en/article/joseph-adelard-godbout/), and won a landslide victory in August 1936, ending 39 years of Liberal rule.
### First Term as Premier
Maurice Duplessis's first term was a disappointment. Except for his successful farm credit scheme, his Fair Wages Commission, and provisions for destitute mothers and the blind, little was achieved. The administration was prodigal. Duplessis himself lived riotously (he was a lusty and somewhat alcoholic bachelor in these times, and never did marry), and he blundered disastrously in September 1939 by calling a snap election on the issue of participation in the war effort.
Quebec’s federal ministers, including [Ernest Lapointe](https://www.thecanadianencyclopedia.ca/en/article/ernest-lapointe/), Arthur Cardin and [C.G. Power](https://www.thecanadianencyclopedia.ca/en/article/charles-gavan-power/), threatened to resign, leaving Quebec defenceless against a pro-[conscription](https://www.thecanadianencyclopedia.ca/en/article/conscription/) English Canada if Duplessis was re-elected. They also pledged that they would prevent conscription if Duplessis was defeated.
Though he was personally re-elected, his government lost badly to Godbout. In opposition, Duplessis's health collapsed. After months in hospital in 1941–42 fighting pneumonia and diabetes, he never drank again.
### Second Term as Premier
Maurice Duplessis campaigned strenuously for two years, and was narrowly re-elected in 1944 over Godbout and the nationalist [Bloc populaire canadien](https://www.thecanadianencyclopedia.ca/en/article/bloc-populaire-canadien/). The latter included notable figures like [André Laurendeau](https://www.thecanadianencyclopedia.ca/en/article/joseph-edmond-andre-laurendeau/) and [Jean Drapeau](https://www.thecanadianencyclopedia.ca/en/article/jean-drapeau/) as well as being supported by [Henri Bourassa](https://www.thecanadianencyclopedia.ca/en/article/henri-bourassa/). The Union Nationale was re-elected in 1948, 1952 and 1956. The party also successfully intervened in other elections, especially in defeating Montreal Mayor Jean Drapeau in 1957 and in helping elect 50 of [John Diefenbaker](https://www.thecanadianencyclopedia.ca/en/article/john-diefenbaker/)’s Conservatives in Quebec in 1958. Duplessis’ 15-year second term saw him assert the authority of the Quebec state over that of the Church. He wrestled part of the concurrent jurisdiction over direct taxes back from the federal government after WWII. Under him, the Quebec government introduced social legislations such as Canada's most generous minimum wage and home ownership assistance Acts. His government produced enormous public works, highway, hospital, school and university construction projects. Ambitious hydroelectric power schemes were set up while electrification was extended throughout rural Quebec.

Duplessis became equally known for dealing harshly with striking unions, especially at Noranda, [Asbestos](https://www.thecanadianencyclopedia.ca/en/article/asbestos-que/), Louiseville and Murdochville. (_See also_[Asbestos Strike of 1949](https://www.thecanadianencyclopedia.ca/en/article/asbestos-strike); [Murdochville Strike](https://www.thecanadianencyclopedia.ca/en/article/murdochville-strike).) He also had disdain for most contemporary concepts of civil liberties. This was particularly apparent in litigation over the anticommunist [Padlock Act](https://www.thecanadianencyclopedia.ca/en/article/padlock-act/), overruled by the Supreme Court of Canada in 1957, and the [Roncarelli case](https://www.thecanadianencyclopedia.ca/en/article/roncarelli-v-duplessis/), in which Duplessis was personally ordered to pay damages of $46,132 by the Supreme Court in 1959. (_See also_[Grande Noirceur](https://www.thecanadianencyclopedia.ca/en/article/grande-noirceur).)
Apart from his jurisdictional gains, Duplessis presented a number of symbolic nationalist measures, such as the adoption of the Quebec flag. Duplessis developed a very powerful political machine. Corruption and patronage reached legendary proportions, yet Duplessis presided over a period of unprecedented prosperity, economic growth and investment in which Quebec was for the first time by almost any social or economic yardstick gaining on Ontario.
A modernizer except in political methodology, Duplessis pushed the Quebec state to an unprecedented position of strength in relation to the church, the federal government, and the Anglo-Saxon Montreal business establishment. His system depended upon employing the clergy at bargain wages to do what was really secular work in schools and hospitals, while reducing the episcopate to financial dependence. He reduced taxes, balanced budgets and persuaded conservatives and nationalists to vote together (for "autonomy"as he called it).
His system crumbled after his death in 1959 with the demise of his successors [Paul Sauvé](https://www.thecanadianencyclopedia.ca/en/article/joseph-mignault-paul-sauve/) and [Daniel Johnson](https://www.thecanadianencyclopedia.ca/en/article/daniel-johnson/), and the triumph of the [Quiet Revolution](https://www.thecanadianencyclopedia.ca/en/article/quiet-revolution/). Maurice Duplessis was an enigmatic and picturesque character, a demagogue in public while urbane, elegant and witty in private. For much of his career he was almost universally known as "le Chef" in recognition of his strong, though controversial leadership of Quebec.
