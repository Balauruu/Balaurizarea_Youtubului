# The Duplessis Orphans
*Research dossier — 2026-03-14*

---

### Section 1 — Subject Overview

Between approximately 1935 and 1964, an estimated 20,000 children in the Canadian province of Quebec were placed in nurseries, orphanages, and psychiatric institutions administered primarily by Roman Catholic religious orders. A significant portion of these children were orphaned, born outside of marriage, or surrendered by impoverished families unable to support them. Under the provincial government of Premier Maurice Duplessis, thousands of these children were falsely certified as mentally deficient and administratively reclassified as psychiatric patients. The mechanism was fiscal: the federal government allocated approximately $0.70 per day per child in an orphanage, but $2.25 per day per patient in a psychiatric institution. By redesignating facilities and reclassifying the children housed within them, Quebec institutions could claim substantially higher federal subsidies without constructing new buildings or improving services. Duplessis signed an order-in-council formalizing the reclassification of orphanages as hospitals.

Children assigned psychiatric diagnoses were subjected to conditions consistent with institutional confinement rather than child care. Survivor testimonies describe corporal punishment, forced labor framed as therapeutic activity, electroshock treatment, administration of psychiatric drugs, sexual abuse, and in some cases lobotomies performed without consent. Educational provision was curtailed or eliminated entirely for those classified as severely impaired, foreclosing normal development and future employment. Diagnoses were recorded on official civil documents and followed individuals into adulthood, affecting marriage prospects, employment, and the plausibility of their accounts of mistreatment. Seven religious communities operated the institutions: the Sisters of Providence, the Sisters of Mercy, the Grey Nuns of Montreal, the Sisters of Charity of Quebec, the Little Franciscans of Mary, the Brothers of Notre-Dame-de-la-Misericorde, and the Brothers of Charity.

The Bedard Report, submitted to the Quebec government in 1962, investigated the province's psychiatric hospitals and found that roughly one-third of the approximately 22,000 patients classified as mentally incompetent were so classified for fiscal rather than clinical reasons. This report triggered the end of the practice and the beginning of secularization under the Quiet Revolution. Public reckoning came decades later. In 1991, Pauline Gill's book *Les enfants de Duplessis* reintroduced the orphans' story to Quebec audiences. A research study by Léo-Paul Lauzon and Martin Poirier in 1999 estimated that religious organizations collected approximately $70 million in subsidies (in 1999 dollars) through the reclassification scheme, while the Quebec government saved $37 million simply by redesignating one institution. The Quebec government apologized in March 1999 under Premier Lucien Bouchard and established a $3 million fund. In 2001, under Premier Bernard Landry, survivors accepted a compensation offer of $10,000 per person plus $1,000 per year of wrongful confinement, capped at $25,000. Over 1,000 orphans shared approximately $26 million in total compensation. In accepting the settlement, survivors were required to waive further legal action against the Catholic Church. The Church itself never issued a formal apology; the *Collège des médecins* expressed regrets in a 2012 letter.

### Section 2 — Timeline

- **1909** -- The *Loi sur les Asiles d'aliénés* (Lunatic Asylum Act) governed mental institution admissions, allowing commitment for reasons including maintenance of "social order" — undefined and left to psychiatrists' discretion. *Source: connexions.org*

- **1935–1936** -- Children begin entering Catholic-run orphanages in large numbers. Maurice Duplessis serves his first term as Premier of Quebec (1936–1939), establishing the Union Nationale government's alignment with clerical authority. *Source: thecanadianencyclopedia.ca*

- **1944–1959** -- Duplessis serves a second consecutive period as Premier. Federal subsidies during this period allocate $0.70/day per orphan versus $2.25/day per psychiatric patient. *Source: thecanadianencyclopedia.ca*

- **Late 1940s–early 1950s** -- Orphanages are administratively redesignated as psychiatric hospitals. Children are reclassified as mentally deficient through superficial or formulaic diagnoses. Duplessis signs an order-in-council formalizing the reclassification. *Source: connexions.org*

- **1942** -- Quebec law passed allowing religious institutions to sell unclaimed bodies of deceased residents to medical schools for $10 each. *Source: canadiangenocide.nativeweb.org*

- **1961–1964** -- Autobiographies including *Les fous crient au secours* (1961) and *Ma chienne de vie* (1964) expose conditions in Quebec asylums. *Source: thecanadianencyclopedia.ca*

- **1962** -- The Bedard Report (*Rapport de la Commission d'étude des hôpitaux psychiatriques*) is submitted to the Quebec government. It finds that approximately one-third of the 22,000 patients labeled mentally incompetent were classified for fiscal rather than clinical reasons. Quebec ceases the practice. *Source: connexions.org, grokipedia.com*

- **1991** -- Pauline Gill publishes *Les enfants de Duplessis*, reigniting public awareness of the orphans' experiences after roughly two decades of public silence. *Source: thecanadianencyclopedia.ca*

- **1992–1995** -- Hervé Bertrand and other survivors pursue class action lawsuits; seven requests submitted to the Court of Quebec in 1993. Court rejects them in 1995, citing failure to meet class action criteria. *Source: thecanadianencyclopedia.ca*

- **1997** -- Ombudsman Daniel Jacoby recommends a public apology and compensation. A study compares Duplessis orphans to a low-income control group; orphans show markedly lower education levels, higher stress, more attachment difficulties. *Source: thecanadianencyclopedia.ca*

- **1999** -- Léo-Paul Lauzon and Martin Poirier publish a report estimating $70 million in religious subsidies and $37 million in government savings from reclassification. Quebec government under Bouchard apologizes and creates a $3 million fund. *Source: connexions.org*

- **2001** -- Survivors accept final compensation offer: $10,000 per person plus $1,000 per year of confinement (cap $25,000); waiver of claims against the Church required. *Source: thecanadianencyclopedia.ca, cbc.ca*

- **2004** -- A group of survivors petitions a Quebec court for permission to exhume bodies from an abandoned cemetery in east Montreal believed to contain victims of medical experimentation. *Source: canadiangenocide.nativeweb.org*

- **2010** -- Duplessis orphans present their case before the United Nations Human Rights Council. Approximately 300–400 survivors estimated still alive. *Source: connexions.org*

- **2018–2020** -- New class action suits filed against eight religious communities ($875,000 each); rejected by Superior Court of Quebec in 2018. Additional suits filed in April 2018 and July 2020. *Source: thecanadianencyclopedia.ca*

### Section 3 — Key Figures

**Maurice Duplessis** -- Premier of Quebec, Union Nationale
Duplessis governed Quebec from 1936–1939 and 1944–1959. His government signed the order-in-council redesignating orphanages as psychiatric hospitals and maintained the fiscal arrangement that incentivized mass reclassification. The period of his premiership is retrospectively labeled by critics as *La Grande Noirceur* ("The Great Darkness").

**Daniel Jacoby** -- Quebec Ombudsman
In January 1997, Jacoby filed a report recommending at minimum a public apology to the orphans and proposed compensation scenarios involving the Quebec state, the medical profession, and the religious congregations. He publicly criticized the 1999 government offer as trivializing the abuse.

**Hervé Bertrand** -- Survivor advocate
Initiated the class action process in 1992, working with lawyers to document the experiences of dozens of institutionalized survivors and attempt legal recourse. Appeared as a spokesperson for the orphan group in media coverage.

**Louis-Joseph Hébert ("Nestor")** -- Survivor
Born to an unmarried woman and institutionalized as an infant, Nestor was given a generic surname by nuns along with other babies born the same month. He reports being beaten by nuns whenever he showed emotion as a child, a conditioned response that persisted into adulthood. See QUOTE: The Child of Sin

**Paul St. Aubain** -- Survivor
Lobotomized at age 18 at St. Michel Archange mental hospital in Joliette without his consent. Spent 25 years in various mental institutions. At time of reporting lived on welfare and was unable to work.

**Léo-Paul Lauzon and Martin Poirier** -- Researchers, Université du Québec à Montréal
In 1999 produced a financial analysis estimating the scale of economic benefit extracted from the reclassification scheme: approximately $70 million to religious bodies and $37 million to the provincial government.

### Section 4 — Narrative Hooks

**HOOK: The Subsidy Loophole**
A federal funding formula that allocated $2.25 per day per psychiatric patient versus $0.70 per day per orphan provided the structural incentive for the entire scandal. By signing a single order-in-council redesignating buildings, Duplessis's government transformed thousands of children into revenue-generating psychiatric cases without constructing a single new facility.

**HOOK: The Bodies in the Cemetery**
A group of survivors petitioned a Quebec court in 2004 for permission to exhume an abandoned east-Montreal cemetery believed to hold the remains of children who died during medical experiments at the Cité de St-Jean-de-Dieu asylum. A 1942 Quebec law had allowed institutions to sell unclaimed bodies to medical schools for $10 each. Hospital registries documenting who died and how have since disappeared from government archives.

**HOOK: The Waiver**
When survivors accepted the 2001 compensation settlement — $10,000 per person plus $1,000 per year of confinement — they were required to sign a legal waiver barring any future claims against the Catholic Church. The Church itself never apologized. Survivor Martin Lécuyer stated in 2006: "It's an insult, and it's the biggest proof that the government is an accomplice of the Church."

**HOOK: The Unremovable Diagnosis**
Once a child's psychiatric classification was entered into official civil records, it followed them into adulthood — affecting employment, social standing, and their ability to be believed when reporting abuse. Even decades later, after investigators had confirmed the diagnoses were fraudulent, the documentary trace remained embedded in official archives. Classification did not just imprison children; it shaped life trajectories.

**HOOK: The Parallel Across the Ocean**
A 2017 video conference connected Duplessis orphan survivors in Montreal with survivors of Irish Mother and Baby Homes in Dublin — two groups separated by a language and an ocean, describing nearly identical experiences of abuse under Catholic institutional care, identical processes of false mental deficiency labeling, and an identical institutional logic of treating illegitimate birth as moral contamination requiring confinement.

### Section 6 — Contradictions

**Subsidy Rates**
connexions.org states the government contribution was $1.25/day per orphan versus $2.75/day per psychiatric patient. thecanadianencyclopedia.ca (citing Lauzon and Poirier, 1999) states $0.70/day per orphan versus $2.25/day per psychiatric patient. The Canadian Encyclopedia source cites specific researchers and dates; the connexions.org figures appear in an adapted Wikipedia entry without citation. The thecanadianencyclopedia.ca version has stronger sourcing and is corroborated by other sources including the main Wikipedia article.

**Total Number of Orphans Affected**
connexions.org and canadiangenocide.nativeweb.org both cite approximately 20,000 orphans during the Duplessis era. thecanadianencyclopedia.ca (citing the 1962 Bedard Report) references approximately 22,000 patients total labeled mentally incompetent, of whom one-third were wrongfully classified. These figures are not necessarily in conflict — 22,000 is the total psychiatric patient population at the time, while 20,000 is an estimate of the orphan subgroup — but the distinction is not consistently made across sources.

**Nature of Church Involvement**
The Catholic Church denied institutional involvement in reclassification, attributing conditions to lack of personnel and funding rather than deliberate abuse. Lauzon/Poirier's 1999 financial analysis and survivor testimony directly contradict the Church's framing, estimating $70 million in subsidies received through the scheme. The Superior Court of Quebec rejected the 2018 class action against eight religious communities, though it did not rule on whether abuses occurred.

### Section 7 — Unanswered Questions

1. What happened to the hospital registries documenting deaths at the Cité de St-Jean-de-Dieu asylum — did they disappear accidentally or were they deliberately destroyed, and by whom?
2. How many of the approximately 20,000 reclassified children were subjected to surgical procedures such as lobotomies, and on what evidentiary basis were these decisions made?
3. Why did the Superior Court reject the 2018 class action suits against the eight religious communities, and what specific evidentiary threshold was not met?
4. How many bodies are buried in the abandoned east-Montreal cemetery, and were the exhumation requests granted or permanently denied?
5. What became of the 1942 records documenting which unclaimed bodies were sold to medical schools and for what purposes they were used?
6. Did federal government officials have knowledge of the reclassification scheme at the time it was implemented, and if so, why were subsidies not withheld?

### Section 8 — Correcting the Record

**Mainstream account:** The Duplessis Orphans scandal is commonly described as children being falsely labeled mentally ill to qualify institutions for higher subsidies — an administrative irregularity caused primarily by Duplessis's political alignment with the Church.
**Primary source evidence:** The 1999 Lauzon/Poirier report, citing institutional financial records, estimated that religious organizations collected $70 million in subsidies and the Quebec government itself saved $37 million through redesignation of a single institution. The scale and the government's own financial stake suggest deliberate policy rather than passive acquiescence.
**Source:** connexions.org (citing Lauzon/Poirier, 1999)

**Mainstream account:** Compensation was provided to Duplessis orphans through the 2001 settlement, representing resolution of the government's obligations.
**Primary source evidence:** The 2001 settlement required survivors to waive any further legal claims against the Catholic Church as a condition of receiving funds. Over 1,000 survivors received an average of approximately $25,000 each — for institutionalization that could span decades. Three government bureaucrats administering the compensation program were paid over $1,000 per day, while orphans received the equivalent for an entire year of wrongful confinement.
**Source:** connexions.org, cbc.ca

**Mainstream account:** The Catholic Church has no formal record of involvement in reclassification decisions.
**Primary source evidence:** The seven named religious communities directly operated the facilities where reclassification occurred and received the subsidy payments that resulted. The 1962 Bedard Report, commissioned by the Quebec government itself, confirmed institutional misclassification. The Church's own denial is contradicted by the operational reality documented in the report.
**Source:** connexions.org, thecanadianencyclopedia.ca
