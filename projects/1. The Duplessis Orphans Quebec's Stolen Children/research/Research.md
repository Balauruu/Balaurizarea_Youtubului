# The Duplessis Orphans
*Research dossier — 2026-03-20*

### Section 1 — Subject Overview

The Duplessis Orphans (French: *les Orphelins de Duplessis*) were a population of Canadian children wrongly certified as mentally ill by the provincial government of Quebec and confined to psychiatric institutions during the 1940s and 1950s. Named after Maurice Duplessis, who served as Premier of Quebec for five non-consecutive terms between 1936 and 1959, the scandal represents what is believed to be the largest case of child abuse in Canadian history outside of the Canadian Indian residential school system.

From 1935 to 1964, thousands of children — many born to unwed mothers and classified as "illegitimate" — were placed in nurseries, orphanages, and psychiatric hospitals operated by Catholic religious congregations. Before the Quiet Revolution of the 1960s, the Catholic Church controlled most social services in Quebec. The Church encouraged unwed mothers to surrender their children to religious institutions, and maternity homes for unmarried women routinely facilitated these separations.

The financial incentive for reclassification was direct. The federal government allocated $1.25 per day for orphans but $2.75 per day for psychiatric patients. Under Duplessis, the provincial government deliberately reclassified healthy children as mentally ill to capture higher federal subsidies. Duplessis also signed an order-in-council changing the classification of orphanages into hospitals to qualify for additional federal funding. In 1999, researchers Léo-Paul Lauzon and Martin Poirier estimated that religious groups received approximately $70 million (in 1999 dollars) through false certifications, while the government saved $37 million by redesignating a single orphanage from an educational institution to a psychiatric hospital.

A commission in the early 1960s investigating mental institutions after Duplessis's death found that one-third of the 22,000 patients classified as "mentally ill" had been classified for the province's financial benefit rather than any psychiatric condition. The *Rapport de la Commission d'étude des hôpitaux psychiatriques* (Bédard Report) submitted in 1962 helped end the practice. Seven religious communities operated the implicated facilities: the Sisters of Providence, the Sisters of Mercy, the Grey Nuns of Montreal, the Sisters of Charity of Quebec, the Little Franciscans of Mary, the Brothers of Notre-Dame-de-la-Misericorde, and the Brothers of Charity.

### Section 2 — Timeline

- **1909** — The *Loi sur les Asiles d'aliénés* (Lunatic Asylum Act) established the legal framework governing mental institution admissions in Quebec, leaving the definition of "social disorder" to psychiatrists' discretion. *Source: en.wikipedia.org*
- **1935–1964** — Thousands of orphaned and abandoned children were placed in nurseries, orphanages, and psychiatric hospitals run by Catholic congregations across Quebec. *Source: thecanadianencyclopedia.ca*
- **1940s–1950s** — Under Premier Maurice Duplessis, healthy orphans were deliberately reclassified as mentally ill to capture higher federal subsidies. Orphanages were redesignated as hospitals via order-in-council. *Source: en.wikipedia.org*
- **1948** — The federal government introduced a subsidy program for provincial health services, increasing the financial incentive for reclassification. *Source: thecanadianencyclopedia.ca*
- **1961** — Publication of *Les fous crient au secours* by Jean-Charles Pagé exposed conditions inside psychiatric asylums. *Source: thecanadianencyclopedia.ca*
- **1962** — The Bédard Report (*Rapport de la Commission d'étude des hôpitaux psychiatriques*) revealed that one-third of 22,000 patients were misclassified. Quebec ceased the institutional practice of committing children to asylums. *Source: en.wikipedia.org*
- **1991** — Publication of *Les enfants de Duplessis* by Pauline Gill brought the orphans' stories back to public awareness after two decades of silence. *Source: thecanadianencyclopedia.ca*
- **1992** — Hervé Bertrand and other survivors began organizing a class action lawsuit with attorney support. *Source: thecanadianencyclopedia.ca*
- **1995** — Quebec courts rejected class action requests, ruling they did not meet the required criteria. *Source: thecanadianencyclopedia.ca*
- **March 4, 1999** — Premier Lucien Bouchard issued a public apology "without blaming or imputing legal responsibility to anyone." The initial compensation offer of approximately $15,000 per victim was rejected. *Source: en.wikipedia.org*
- **July 1, 2001** — The Duplessis Orphans Committee accepted the government's revised offer: $10,000 per person plus $1,000 per year of wrongful confinement, up to $25,000 maximum. Recipients waived further legal action against the Catholic Church. *Source: en.wikipedia.org*
- **2004** — Some orphans requested the Quebec government exhume an abandoned cemetery in east Montreal believed to contain remains of children used as non-consensual experimental subjects. *Source: en.wikipedia.org*
- **November 2010** — The Duplessis Orphans presented their case before the United Nations Human Rights Council. *Source: en.wikipedia.org*
- **2018–2020** — Multiple class action suits filed against religious communities; the first ($875,000 per plaintiff against 8 religious communities) was rejected by the Superior Court of Quebec. Additional suits were filed in April 2018 and July 2020. *Source: thecanadianencyclopedia.ca*

### Section 3 — Key Figures

**Maurice Duplessis** — Premier of Quebec (1936–1939, 1944–1959)
Signed the order-in-council that reclassified orphanages as hospitals, enabling the systematic misdiagnosis of healthy children. His administration received the higher federal subsidies that incentivized the scheme. The period of his rule is referred to by critics as *La Grande Noirceur* ("The Great Darkness").

**Daniel Jacoby** — Quebec Ombudsman
Investigated the orphans' case and filed a January 1997 report recommending public apologies and compensation. Criticized the government's initial 1999 offer as trivializing the victims' abuse.

**Lucien Bouchard** — Premier of Quebec (1996–2001)
Delivered the formal public apology on March 4, 1999, though "without blaming or imputing legal responsibility to anyone." His government made the initial rejected compensation offer and the later accepted settlement.

**Hervé Bertrand** — Duplessis Orphan and activist
Initiated the class action organizing effort in 1992, becoming one of the most visible survivors advocating for recognition and compensation. See QUOTE: A Survivor's Struggle.

**Léo-Paul Lauzon** and **Martin Poirier** — Researchers
Published the 1999 report demonstrating the financial incentives behind the false certifications, estimating $70 million in subsidies to religious groups and $37 million in government savings.

**Louis-Joseph Hébert ("Nestor")** — Duplessis Orphan and survivor
One of the most vocal surviving orphans, who has spoken publicly about the physical and emotional abuse endured in institutional care. See QUOTE: Born Into a Garbage Can.

### Section 4 — Narrative Hooks

**HOOK: The $1.50 That Bought a Childhood**
The entire scheme turned on a funding gap: $1.25 per day for an orphan versus $2.75 for a psychiatric patient. That $1.50 difference was sufficient incentive for the provincial government to reclassify thousands of healthy children as mentally ill, condemning them to years inside psychiatric institutions.

**HOOK: The Cemetery That Won't Stay Buried**
In 2004, orphan survivors asked Quebec to exhume an abandoned cemetery in east Montreal attached to the Cité de St-Jean-de-Dieu asylum. Testimony from former inmates described children routinely used as non-consensual experimental subjects, with many dying as a consequence. The government has not excavated the site. The 2021 discoveries of unmarked graves at Canadian residential schools have renewed pressure for excavation.

**HOOK: The Letter of Regret That Took Sixty Years**
The Catholic Church has never offered a formal apology to the Duplessis Orphans. The closest was a 2012 letter from the Collège des médecins expressing "regrets" — not an apology. The seven religious orders that operated the facilities have disputed the accusations and contested legal proceedings for decades. When the 2001 settlement was reached, survivors were required to waive any further legal action against the Church.

**HOOK: The Man Who Cannot Cry**
Louis-Joseph Hébert, known as Nestor, says the nuns beat him whenever he showed emotion as a child. As an adult, he physically cannot cry, even when he wants to. His testimony connects the institutional violence to permanent emotional damage that persists seven decades later.

### Section 6 — Contradictions

**Financial Motivation vs. Genuine Care**
Religious congregations stated they were dealing with a "lack of personnel and money" when responding to abuse allegations (thecanadianencyclopedia.ca). However, the Lauzon-Poirier report calculated that the same congregations received approximately $70 million in subsidies through the false certifications (en.wikipedia.org). The defense of resource scarcity conflicts with evidence of substantial financial gain from the reclassification scheme.

**Number of Victims**
The 2001 government settlement was negotiated by representatives of approximately 3,000 survivors who formed the Duplessis Orphans Committee (en.wikipedia.org). However, the Quebec government ultimately received more than 6,500 requests for financial assistance (thecanadianencyclopedia.ca). The true scope of victimization appears larger than the organized advocacy group represented.

**Apology Without Responsibility**
Premier Lucien Bouchard declared a public apology in March 1999 but explicitly "without blaming or imputing legal responsibility to anyone" (en.wikipedia.org). The Quebec Ombudsman, Daniel Jacoby, characterized the government's handling of the affair as trivializing the abuse alleged by the victims (en.wikipedia.org). The formal apology functioned as a rhetorical gesture that the government's own ombudsman deemed inadequate.

### Section 7 — Unanswered Questions

1. What happened to the children whose remains may lie in the abandoned cemetery at the former Cité de St-Jean-de-Dieu asylum in east Montreal, and why has the Quebec government not authorized an excavation?
2. How many children died while institutionalized under the false psychiatric classifications, and were any subjected to fatal medical experiments?
3. Why did the Quebec Class Action Fund decline to support the orphans' 1993 class action requests, effectively blocking their primary legal avenue?
4. What specific role did individual psychiatrists play in issuing false diagnoses, and were any ever held professionally accountable?
5. Who authorized the requirement that orphans waive legal action against the Catholic Church as a condition of accepting the 2001 government settlement?
6. What happened to the institutional records from the seven religious communities, and do complete patient files still exist?

### Section 8 — Correcting the Record

**Mainstream account:** The Duplessis Orphans were "orphans" in the traditional sense — children whose parents had died.
**Primary source evidence:** The majority were not orphaned by parental death. Most were children born out of wedlock, classified as "illegitimate" or "bastards," and surrendered to Church institutions by mothers pressured by social stigma and Catholic doctrine. Some were entrusted to the State by living family members.
**Source:** thecanadianencyclopedia.ca, cbc.ca

**Mainstream account:** The 2001 settlement resolved the Duplessis Orphans' grievances.
**Primary source evidence:** The settlement was bitterly contested by many orphans. The committee's lawyer, president, and former public relations official received six- to seven-figure payments, while individual orphans received approximately $25,000. Three bureaucrats running the compensation program were paid over $1,000 per day. A 2018 class action lawsuit described the earlier settlement as "an insult" and "not a true apology."
**Source:** en.wikipedia.org (citing Montreal Mirror)

**Mainstream account:** The abuse was primarily a government failing during the Duplessis era.
**Primary source evidence:** The practice of institutionalizing children spanned from 1935 to 1964, extending well beyond Duplessis's death in 1959. Multiple premiers and governments were in power during this period. The Catholic Church operated the facilities and its personnel carried out the physical and sexual abuse. The Collège des médecins did not express even regrets until 2012.
**Source:** thecanadianencyclopedia.ca
