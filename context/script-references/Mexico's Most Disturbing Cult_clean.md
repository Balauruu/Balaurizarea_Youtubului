## 1. Strangers in the Jungle

The small indigenous farming community of Yaba Bua was truly remote. It had no roads, no electricity, no running water, and was home to only about 50 native Mexican farmers. The only way in or out of Yaba Bua was through a rough, beaten cart track which led to a distant highway.

It was upon this track in 1961 that newcomers appeared in Yaba Bua. The two would surely be the first outsiders to set foot in the village unannounced in living memory. Situated at the base of Cerro el [unclear], as it was, you couldn't simply stumble across the village. These people had come here with a purpose.

Magdalena and Eleazar Solis were fraternal twins born in 1945. They were only 16 years old when they ventured out of the tree line into Yaba Bua. They had grown up in Monterrey, near [unclear], where they had learned about spiritism — the religious study of the supernatural.

Spiritism is deeply rooted in the indigenous people of Mexico. During the Mexican Revolution at the beginning of the 20th century, spiritualism and spiritism became inseparable from the anti-government revolutionary movement. Francisco I. Madero, founder of the anti-realist party and the man credited with starting the Mexican Revolution, was a spiritist and medium. In 1911 he published the spiritist manual based upon his spirit teachings in France, and claimed he started the Mexican Revolution upon guidance from the spirit. In his writings, he describes the true Mexican religion from antiquity and argued that the Catholicism that replaced it had become an instrument of domination over the people of Mexico.

Spiritists believed that the original ancient religion of Mexico had been preserved, its teachings passed down to a select few, with the goal of one day overthrowing Catholicism and returning the religion of their ancestors to the Mexican people.

Magdalena and Eleazar Solis left Monterrey together in 1961, traveling south between small indigenous communities. They used the beliefs of these people to their advantage by claiming to be adepts of the true religion, instilling its teachings as a way of scamming food and shelter from these communities before slipping away.

Emboldened by the success of their scam, they traveled into Tulipas, to the village of Yaba Bua, to orchestrate something more long-term. They again presented themselves as adepts of the old religion. Reincarnation is a core part of Mexican spiritism, and so Magdalena played the part of a reincarnated faith healer and mystic, whilst Eleazar was the reincarnation of St. Francis of Assisi. The twins would slowly reveal the true ancient religion to the farmers of Yaba Bua — how their ancestors had believed that the civilizations of ancient Mexico, that of the Aztecs and the Toltec, had been older and even more advanced than the ancient Egyptians.

## 2. Initial Control

In the beginning, very little had changed in the twins' scam. They asked for food and shelter, preying upon the generous hospitality of the villagers. Magdalena and Eleazar slowly drip-fed the farmers information about their supposed identities and their knowledge of the old gods. The scam allowed them to extract many more resources from the villagers than the villagers were willing to spare.

The twins knew that there would be a point when the villagers discovered their dishonesty, and the hospitality — strange as it was — would come to an end. However, this time they planned on staying much longer.

When the farmers began asking for more substantial evidence from the twins, they agreed to perform an ancient ritual to prove their supposed identities. The twins' ritual involved the theatrical brewing of a potion which they claimed had the supernatural power to grant visions, but in reality it was laced with marijuana. They convinced the villagers to drink this potion. Now drugged and in a more suggestible state, the sober twins would continue to reinforce and convince the villagers of their lies. This proved to be a powerful and effective technique.

Every time the villagers began to doubt the lies of Magdalena and Eleazar Solis, the twins would develop new rituals to quell the disbelievers, each time upping the stakes.

They began with an amendment for the first ritual — the addition of animal blood to the ingredients of the drugged potion. Next they incorporated elaborate costumes into the rituals, covering themselves in body paint and feathers, imitating the clothing of ancient Aztec priests and priestesses.

Two groups began to form in Yaba Bua: those who believed Magdalena and Eleazar, and those villagers who still had their doubts.

In the spring of 1962, the twins ordained two of the most faithful villagers to help them perform rituals and to act as muscle to extract tributes from the non-believers. They chose the brothers Santos and Cayetano Hernandez, who had grown up in Yaba Bua. Like all of those in the farming community, the brothers toiled endlessly, day in, day out, in the fields surrounding the village. With little else to do, the brothers had welcomed the arrival of Magdalena and Eleazar Solis. They readily drank the drugged potion and deeply believed the twins' lies, and in their fabricated power.

The Hernandez brothers were cruel and enjoyed exerting their newly found authority over the non-believers.

With the extra manpower, Eleazar — aided by the brothers — constructed a temple for the rituals high in the mountain caves above Yaba Bua. They erected a wooden dais around a flat stone slab to act as an altar, with a thatched roof supported by four wooden pillars. The villagers would make the trek through the jungle and into the cavern to watch the nightly rituals.

The twins now lived lavishly, aided by the additional protection provided by the Hernandez brothers. However, a conflict of interest began to grow between the twins and their newly ordained Santos and Cayetano. While the Solis twins' goal was to draw out the scam for as long as possible, the Hernandez brothers wanted only to increase the following and influence of the fledgling cult. The twins couldn't be seen as limiting the growth of the cult, especially since the brothers kept urging them towards ever greater and greater spectacles.

This conflict between the priests and their teenage leaders would come to a head at the end of April.

## 3. Willing Sacrifices

1962. As night fell upon Yaba Bua, the villagers made the usual trek through the forest, up the mountain path, to the cavern. There, Magdalena, Eleazar, Santos, and Cayetano were gathered beneath the thatched roof of their makeshift temple, clad in the body paint and feathers of ancient Aztec priests — as before. They drugged the villagers, who willingly drank the Solis twins' potion of blood and marijuana, as they had become addicted to the concoction over the course of many rituals.

Cayetano stepped to the front of the dais and quieted the crowd. He called for a volunteer — someone to offer themselves as a willing sacrifice to the old gods. Cayetano promised that whoever they sacrificed would ascend to the realm of gods, to live with them for eternity.

Incredibly — from the testimony of those who had survived the sacrifices and bloodshed yet to come — one of the villagers offered themselves willingly at the very first sacrifice. So powerful were the drugs and lies of the Solis twins that the villagers of Yaba Bua were willing to lay down their lives for Magdalena and Eleazar.

Cayetano guided the unfortunate farmer to the altar and instructed them to lie atop it. The Hernandez brothers then made quick and gruesome work of the individual. Santos and Cayetano sliced open the body and removed the victim's still-beating heart.

After their murderous fervor subsided, Cayetano and Santos collected the victim's blood from atop the altar to substitute the animal blood previously used in the twins' potion. The potion was once again passed round. Finally, the sacrifice's corpse was carried to the corner of the cave and set alight.

The twins' grasp had become so powerful upon the farmers of Yaba Bua that the villagers were now content and complicit with murder and cannibalism. And if they had any doubts or reservations, none were willing to speak out.

At least for the next 12 months. During this time, at the end of each month, the villagers would enter the cave and emerge one fewer in number. Few eyewitness testimonies remain. Those that do describe a tragic and horrifying series of events in which, without fail, every month for a year, a willing sacrifice stepped forth from the crowd and towards that bloody altar.

The Solis twins had lost control of the lies they told. They were soon to owe the lives of at least 15 people.

## 4. Truth: May 31st, 1963

On May 31st, 1963, the villagers of Yaba Bua made their way through the jungle to the mountain cave for the monthly sacrifice. The villagers consumed the ritual potion, and the Hernandez brothers unknowingly called forth their final victim from within the crowd of adult farmers.

Selena Silvana volunteered herself.

However, just before she reached the altar — finally, after months of silence — someone spoke out.

Hector Solis, Selena's childhood sweetheart, could no longer bring himself to sit idly by as the persecution occurred. He caught up to her right before the dais and confronted Selena, desperately trying to intervene. But she wouldn't listen to him. She wanted to be sacrificed — to ascend to a god, to live in guaranteed bliss forever.

Selena picked up a stone and gave it to him, begging him to use it to crush out her life. They struggled. She fell. The others rushed in on them, knocking Hector out and pelting Selena with stones until she was senseless.

By the time Hector had come to his senses, it was too late. Cayetano and Santos had dragged Selena's body atop the altar and removed her heart.

Enraged and grief-stricken, Hector fought briefly with some of the villagers, trying to reach and kill the Hernandez brothers. He quickly realized it was to no avail. Selena was well and truly dead.

Hector departed from the cave after the altercation, leaving the cultists to drain the body of blood and to burn the remains. He returned to his wife and four children in the village, vowing to report the cult to the police.

At dawn, Hector left Yaba Bua and headed towards the nearest police department in Villagrán, where he met with Police Inspector Abelardo G. Gómez. Inspector Gómez asked Hector to recall as many names of the cultists as possible and wrote them down. He instructed Hector to return to Yaba Bua as if nothing had happened, while Inspector Gómez collected his men and prepared for a raid.

When Hector returned to Yaba Bua, the Hernandez brothers were waiting for him and slit his throat with a machete — but not before discovering that the authorities were on their way.

Inspector Gómez gathered his men in Villagrán and started the trek to Yaba Bua, whilst Cayetano and Santos gathered their most loyal followers to defend the Solis twins.

Police poured into the farming community through the rough beaten cart track. Cayetano came out to meet them. Inspector Gómez questioned Cayetano from a distance, demanding to know what was truly going on in Yaba Bua. Cayetano claimed to know nothing of the cult and to be but a simple farmer. But Gómez wasn't fooled so easily.

"He told me he was a farmer, like all the rest. I have not been a rural policeman for long years for nothing. I knew he was lying. I told Luis Martinez, one of my men, to take him in for questioning."

When Martinez stepped forward, Cayetano brought out his machete — fast as lightning — and nearly cut Martinez's head off, killing him instantly. Before Cayetano could make it halfway back to the hut from which the cultists were mounting their defense, police had filled him with lead.

This prompted Santos and the others to return fire. Gómez instructed his officers to spread out, take cover, and return fire. Bullets ripped through the thin walls of the hut in both directions.

When the gunfire settled, Inspector Gómez and his men carefully approached the hut.

"We systematically shot up the mud hut so that no one inside could stay alive. When we went in, they were all on the floor — dead. Santos with them."

Caught in the crossfire, a boy called Sebastian Guerrero, 14, was shot dead.

By the end of the day, the police had taken 40 prisoners and captured Magdalena and Eleazar Solis. In the cave above the village, the police found — piled in the corner — the remains of all 13 victims, including the charred remains of Selena from the night before. Gómez ordered his men to remove the remains for burial and to torch the thatched temple.

"It was the worst thing I've had to deal with in all my years. After this, nothing can surprise me."

Upon questioning the detainees, it was discovered that the cult had spread to the neighboring village of Delgardo. Realizing the situation was too large for the small local police station, the inspector called in federal troops from Tampico. 12 people were ultimately charged and found guilty of murder, whilst the twins were held as the police continued to gather more evidence and dismantle what was left of the cult.

## 5. Truth: 2024

When I first approached this video topic, the themes and outline of the video were completely different from those of the final product. The more research I did into this cult, the more I came to realize that the internet's modern retelling of the story is so far removed from the ground truth that the two are now becoming almost indistinguishable.

We'll begin with the Wikipedia entry for Magdalena Solis. The article begins as follows:

> "Magdalena Solis, known as The High Priestess of Blood, allegedly was a serial killer and cult leader responsible for orchestrating several murders which involve the drinking of victims' blood. She is regarded as one of the few documented instances of a sexually motivated female serial killer, showing organized, visionary, and hedonistic characteristics."

I think you can see where these stories depart from each other now.

Most sources online don't even mention her brother Eleazar, and instead claim the cult was started by the Hernandez brothers, Cayetano and Santos. These same sources fixate themselves on the supposed sexual aspect of the cult, and more disturbingly, hyperfixate on Magdalena's sexuality. I'll remind you that she was just 16 when the cult began.

They claimed that Magdalena was a prostitute from Monterrey, and that when the Hernandez brothers began to lose control of the cult, they traveled from Yaba Bua to Monterrey to recruit her. This seems very unlikely, as Monterrey is over 148 km away from Yaba Bua and across state borders.

The actual position of Yaba Bua is also of some debate. The Wikipedia article claims it's in San Luis Potosí. Indeed, it was initially very difficult to track down its actual location — but Yaba Bua, Villagrán, and Delgardo are all in Tulipas [unclear: Tamaulipas?].

Looking at the edit history of the Wikipedia page, we find that most of it was copied over from the Spanish-language Wikipedia on August 3rd, 2021. Interestingly, while the Spanish page includes the correct location for Yaba Bua, this detail wasn't copied over to the English site. The Spanish page itself has changed very little since it was created. It initially cited only two blog-style sources from 2002 and 2003. Both these sources retell the incorrect version of the tale, but allow us to date with some degree of confidence the latest possible origin of this online version.

The absolute earliest source I could find was a newspaper clipping from Texas — the Valley Evening Monitor — which was dated to June 13th, 1963: one month after the final sacrifice and raid took place. It reports the 12 members of the cult were eventually found guilty of murder, and that the cult was started by the twins and not the Hernandez brothers. There is no mention here of the supposed sexual aspect of the cult.

One year later, in 1964, an American called Bill Starr traveled to Mexico and interviewed Abelardo G. Gómez about a case he had read about in American newspapers. Inspector Gómez relayed to Bill Starr how Magdalena and Eleazar Solis started the cult in the nearby village of Yaba Bua, and how, after years of lies, they lost control of this cult.

Bill Starr would then write up and send this story to Curtis and Mary Fuller, owners of Fate magazine, which published "true stories of the strange and the unknown." Starr's interview of Gómez was later reproduced in 2002 in the book The Mammoth Book of Women Who Kill. This is where I first found Starr's tale.

Whilst it's difficult to say for sure whether Bill Starr's interview of Gómez is the absolute truth of the matter, after months of research I can say it's the most trustworthy source existing on the internet. In comparison, sources like Crimes of the 20th Century claim:

> "Santos and Cayetano convinced lesbian prostitute Magdalena Solis and her homosexual brother Eleazar to come to Yaba Bua to participate in the mountain cave rites. In the midst of this spirited emotional rite, high priest Santos Hernandez sexually purified a teenage girl, Selena Silvana. Magdalena Solis watched the intimacies between Santos and Selena, which stimulated her own desires for the girl. Santos then passed the young Selena to Magdalena, while Eleazar and Cayetano, who also preferred males, took advantage of the boys in the assemblage."
